# Decreto 133 (1994) — Family Procedure Law

## Summary

The Family Procedure Law (Ley Procesal de Familia) establishes the procedural rules for enforcing the rights and obligations set out in El Salvador's Family Code (Decreto 677, 1993) and related legislation. Enacted on September 14, 1994, and effective October 1, 1994, the law created a specialized family court system with its own procedural framework distinct from general civil procedure. It governs all litigation and voluntary proceedings related to marriage, divorce, child custody, filiation (paternity and maternity), adoption, alimony, guardianship, property division between spouses, and the protection of minors, incapacitated persons, and the elderly. The law emphasizes orality, judicial immediacy, concentration of proceedings, and the best interest of children. It assigns multidisciplinary support teams (social workers, psychologists, and educators) to every family court and grants broad protective powers to judges, including the authority to issue precautionary and protection orders at any stage.

## Key Provisions

**Art. 1 — Purpose.** The law provides the procedural framework to make effective the rights and duties recognized in the Family Code and other family-related statutes.

**Art. 3 — Guiding Principles.** Family proceedings are governed by: party initiative with ex officio judicial impulse; mandatory judicial presence at all hearings; oral and public hearings (with discretionary reservation); equality of the parties; procedural good faith; and the requirement that parties present all facts, defenses, and evidence simultaneously.

**Art. 4 — Multidisciplinary Support.** Each family court must have a specialist team composed of at least a social worker, a psychologist, and an educator. Judges may also call on experts from the Institute of Legal Medicine, the Salvadoran Institute for the Protection of Minors (ISPM), or the Attorney General's Office.

**Art. 7 — Duties of the Judge.** The judge must direct proceedings, seek the truth of contested facts while respecting the right of defense, prevent procedural fraud, motivate all decisions, and hear any minor aged twelve or older in all proceedings that affect them.

**Art. 42 — Requirements of the Complaint (Demanda).** Lawsuits must be filed in writing and include identification of the parties, a precise statement of facts and claims, offered evidence, and the designation of a place for notifications. In alimony cases, a sworn declaration of the plaintiff's income, expenses, and assets for the preceding five years must be attached; false or omitted information triggers criminal liability.

**Art. 51 — Admissible Evidence.** Family proceedings accept all forms of evidence recognized in common law, documentary evidence, and scientific evidence. Testimonial incapacity and challenge rules from civil procedure do not apply (Art. 52). Evidence is evaluated under the rules of sound judicial discretion (sana critica) (Art. 56).

**Art. 75-81 — Precautionary Measures.** Protective and precautionary measures may be decreed at any stage, ex officio or on request. They are issued without prior notice to the opposing party. The judge's territorial competence for precautionary measures extends to the entire Republic (Art. 78). Measures remain in force until execution of the judgment. A petitioner who secures measures based on false statements is liable for damages and criminal consequences.

**Art. 82-83 — Judgments.** Judgments must be brief, motivated, and address all claims. Decisions regarding alimony, custody, suspension of parental authority, guardianship, visitation, and cohabitation duties do not acquire the force of res judicata and may be modified when circumstances change. Protection measures for minors must be reviewed by the judge every six months.

**Art. 84-85 — Conciliation and Settlement.** Parties may settle at any stage before the first-instance judgment, provided irrenunciable rights are not compromised. An approved agreement has the same force as a final judgment.

**Art. 91-92 — No Default Declaration.** In family proceedings there is no formal declaration of default; a defendant may appear at any stage and take the case in its current state.

**Art. 95-99 — Pre-Hearing Phase.** The judge must rule on the admissibility of the complaint within five days. The defendant has fifteen days to answer. The judge then conducts a preliminary examination and schedules the preliminary hearing.

**Art. 102-113 — Preliminary Hearing (Audiencia Preliminar).** The hearing opens with a conciliation phase. If settlement fails, the judge moves to the remedial (saneadora) phase: resolving dilatory exceptions, curing procedural defects, fixing contested facts, and ordering evidence. If the facts are agreed or the evidence is conclusive, the judge may rule immediately.

**Art. 114-122 — Sentencing Hearing (Audiencia de Sentencia).** Evidence is received orally, witnesses are examined one by one, parties and the family prosecutor may directly interrogate witnesses. After closing arguments (thirty minutes each), the judge issues the ruling and, if possible, the full judgment; otherwise, the written judgment must follow within five days.

**Art. 124 — Divorce and Nullity Measures.** In contested divorce or marriage nullity proceedings, the judge may order separate residences, provisional custody, child support, spousal maintenance, and preventive annotation of the lawsuit in property registries.

**Art. 130 — Protection Orders.** Protection measures may include: orders to cease harassment or threats; provisional custody assignment; exclusion of the offender from the family home; food allowance obligations; prohibitions on alcohol and drug use; no-contact orders regarding the home, workplace, or school; prohibitions on disposing of household goods; mandatory attendance at educational or therapeutic programs; and sworn undertaking not to reoffend.

**Art. 139 — Alimony (Alimentary Obligations).** The judge orders provisional alimony from the moment the complaint is admitted. The judge must ex officio order evidence of the defendant's economic capacity and the plaintiff's need. The judgment may require the constitution of a mortgage, pledge, or other guarantee. Execution of provisional alimony occurs within the same case file, and only the defense of compliance may be raised.

**Art. 140-143-A — Filiation (Paternity and Maternity).** In paternity or maternity investigation proceedings, the judge orders scientific, hereditary, biological, and anthropomorphic tests. Refusal to submit to testing is assessed under the rules of sound discretion. In provoked-recognition proceedings, if the alleged father fails to appear after a second summons, or refuses to declare or undergo testing, paternity is deemed recognized. A 2015 reform (Art. 143-A) addresses paternity challenges, requiring process confidentiality, scientific testing, and safeguards for the child's best interest including potential moral-damage compensation for abusive claims.

**Art. 147-169 — Appeals.** The law provides for three remedies: revocatoria (reconsideration of procedural orders, decided within three days), apelacion (appeal to the Chamber of Family, covering final judgments and specified interlocutory orders), and casacion (cassation, governed by civil cassation rules). Appeals lodged during the case are accumulated and decided together with the appeal of the final judgment, except for appeals of precautionary-measure orders, which are heard immediately.

**Art. 170-178 — Execution of Judgments.** Judgments are executed by the first-instance judge without a separate file. For monetary obligations, the judge orders seizure of assets following executive-judgment rules. For conduct-specific orders, the judge sets a reasonable compliance period and may impose fines, request police assistance, or refer the matter for criminal prosecution if the order is disobeyed.

**Art. 191-203 — Adoption.** The Family Judge of the adoptee's habitual residence has jurisdiction. Adoption petitions for minors require PGR authorization (resolved within sixty business days) and ISPM certification that the child is eligible. Foreign adopters must present additional documentation from the child-protection authority of their country of residence. Consent must be ratified in a hearing; minors aged twelve or older must express their agreement. After the decree, the judge orders inscription of a new birth certificate without reference to biological parents, and cancellation of the original certificate in a confidential registry.

**Art. 204-205 — Mutual-Consent Divorce.** Spouses file jointly, attaching a settlement agreement covering custody, alimony, and property. A minor spouse may personally sign the agreement and grant power of attorney, except where a community property or participation regime is involved.

**Art. 206-210 — Justices of the Peace.** Justices of the Peace handle family conciliation hearings on custody, visitation, alimony, and marital property liquidation. They may decree precautionary and protection measures, and enforce conciliation agreements. In alimony matters, they may notify immigration authorities to restrict the obligor's departure from the country.

## Who This Affects

- **Spouses and domestic partners** seeking divorce, separation, nullity, or resolution of personal and patrimonial disputes.
- **Parents and children** in custody, visitation, parental authority, and filiation (paternity/maternity) proceedings.
- **Persons seeking or owing alimony** (child support and spousal maintenance), including enforcement of payment obligations.
- **Prospective adoptive parents and adopted children**, both domestic and international.
- **Guardians (tutors) and their wards** (minors and incapacitated persons), including proceedings for appointment, removal, and accounting of guardianship.
- **Elderly persons** in need of court-ordered protection or support.
- **Victims of domestic violence or family conflict** who require protection orders or precautionary measures.
- **Attorneys and legal representatives** practicing family law, who must follow these procedural rules in all family courts.
- **Family Prosecutors (Procuradores de Familia)** who intervene in every family proceeding to protect vulnerable parties.
- **Justices of the Peace** who exercise limited family jurisdiction in conciliation and emergency protective measures.
- **Government agencies** such as the Salvadoran Institute for the Protection of Minors (ISPM), the Attorney General's Office (PGR), and the Institute of Legal Medicine, which provide support and expert services to family courts.

## Related Laws

- **Decreto 677 (1993) — Family Code (Codigo de Familia):** The substantive law whose rights and obligations this procedural law enforces.
- **Codigo de Procedimientos Civiles (Code of Civil Procedure):** Applies suppletorily to the extent its provisions do not conflict with the nature and purpose of this law (Art. 218).
- **Ley Organica Judicial (Organic Law of the Judiciary):** Determines the territorial competence of family courts and chambers (Art. 4).
- **Ley del Instituto Salvadoreno de Proteccion al Menor (ISPM Law):** Governs the child-protection institution that cooperates with family courts on minor-protection measures and adoption certification.
- **Ley de Reparacion por Dano Moral (Moral Damage Compensation Law):** Referenced in paternity-challenge proceedings for potential moral-damage claims arising from abusive paternity disputes (Art. 143-A).
- **Ley de Procedimiento para la Imposicion del Arresto o Multa Administrativos:** Governs the procedure when judges impose sanctions under the Family Code (Art. 213).
- **Ley de Ejercicio Notarial de la Jurisdiccion Voluntaria:** Partially repealed by this law (Art. 219).

## Common Questions

**1. What types of cases are handled under this law?**
All family-related disputes and non-contentious proceedings, including: divorce (contested and mutual-consent), marriage nullity, custody and visitation, alimony (child support and spousal maintenance), paternity and maternity establishment or challenge, adoption, guardianship, protection of minors and the elderly, property division between spouses, and declarations of non-marital unions and cohabitation.

**2. Can a person represent themselves in family court without a lawyer?**
No. Article 10 requires all parties to appear through a legally constituted attorney (apoderado), unless the person is authorized to practice law themselves. Persons of limited economic means may request representation through the Attorney General's Office (PGR).

**3. What happens if the defendant does not respond to the lawsuit or appear at the hearing?**
There is no formal default declaration in family proceedings (Art. 92). If the defendant fails to answer and does not appear at the preliminary hearing, the Family Prosecutor assumes representation of the defendant after the conciliation phase, and the defendant is personally notified of this representation and of the final judgment (Art. 112). The defendant may appear at any later stage and take the case as it stands.

**4. How quickly can a judge order provisional alimony?**
The judge orders provisional alimony from the moment the complaint is admitted (Art. 139(a)). The judge will also ex officio order evidence of the defendant's financial capacity if the parties have not provided it, ensuring rapid determination of the appropriate amount.

**5. What protective measures are available for domestic violence or family conflict?**
Article 130 provides a broad list including: orders to cease harassment, threats, and intimidation; exclusion of the offender from the family home regardless of property rights; no-contact orders for the home, workplace, and school; provisional custody assignment; alimony obligations; prohibition on alcohol and drug consumption; prohibition on disposing of household goods; mandatory therapeutic or educational program attendance; and sworn undertaking not to reoffend. These measures can be decreed at any stage, even before the complaint is formally filed (Art. 75).

**6. How does the adoption process work under this law?**
Adoption petitions for minors require prior administrative authorization from the PGR (within sixty business days) and certification from the ISPM that the child is eligible. The petition is filed with the Family Judge of the adoptee's habitual residence. Consent from biological parents must be ratified at a hearing. Children aged twelve or older must express their agreement. Foreign adopters must present additional documentation including certification from their country's child-protection authority. After the decree, a new birth certificate is issued without reference to biological parents, and the original is cancelled in a confidential registry. Step-parent adoption of a spouse's child does not require the administrative phase.

**7. What appeal options exist against family court decisions?**
Three remedies are available: (a) Revocatoria, a reconsideration request for procedural orders, resolved within three days; (b) Apelacion, an appeal to the Family Chamber covering final judgments and twelve categories of interlocutory orders listed in Art. 153; and (c) Casacion, governed by civil cassation rules. Appeals filed during the proceeding are generally deferred and decided together with the appeal of the final judgment, except appeals against precautionary-measure orders, which are heard immediately (Art. 155).

**8. Can Justices of the Peace handle family matters?**
Yes, but with limited jurisdiction. Under Articles 206-210, Justices of the Peace may conduct conciliation hearings on custody, visitation, alimony, and marital property liquidation. They may also decree emergency protective and precautionary measures. In alimony disputes, they may restrict the obligor's departure from the country through immigration authorities (Art. 209). If conciliation fails, the matter proceeds to the Family Court, and any measures taken remain in force for ten days pending the filing of a formal complaint.
