# Quitting Your Job in El Salvador

The **Labor Code (Decreto 15 of 1972)** establishes the rules for voluntary resignation (renuncia) by employees. While workers are free to leave their jobs, specific notice requirements apply, and the consequences of how you resign affect your final compensation.

## Notice Period Requirements

Employees must provide **written notice** (preaviso) to their employer before leaving:

- **Salaried workers** (empleados): 30 days advance notice
- **Hourly or weekly workers** (obreros): 15 days advance notice

The notice must be in writing and clearly state the intention to resign and the effective date.

## Consequences of Quitting Without Notice

If an employee leaves without providing the required notice, the employer has the right to **deduct the equivalent wages** for the unserved notice period from the employee's final pay. This deduction compensates the employer for the abrupt departure.

## No Severance for Voluntary Resignation

When an employee resigns voluntarily, the employer is **not obligated to pay severance** (indemnizacion). Severance is only owed when the employer terminates the worker without just cause, or when the employee resigns due to the employer's fault (renuncia por causa imputable al patrono).

## Final Pay Entitlements

Regardless of how you resign, the employer must pay you:

- **Accrued wages**: Any unpaid salary up to your last day of work
- **Proportional aguinaldo**: The Christmas bonus proportional to the months worked in the current year
- **Accrued vacation**: Payment for any vacation days earned but not taken, including the 30% vacation premium
- **Proportional AFP/ISSS**: Employer contributions must be current

## Practical Tips

Submit your resignation in writing with a copy for your records. Request a signed acknowledgment from the employer. If final payments are not made, you can file a claim with the **Ministerio de Trabajo**.
