# Privacy and Recording Laws in El Salvador

## Legal Framework

Privacy and recording laws in El Salvador are primarily governed by the **Penal Code, Decreto 1030** (Codigo Penal). The code criminalizes the unauthorized interception, recording, or disclosure of private communications under the offense known as *violacion de comunicaciones privadas*. These provisions protect individuals from having their private conversations and personal activities captured without their knowledge or consent.

## What Constitutes a Violation

Under Decreto 1030, the following acts are punishable offenses:

- **Unauthorized recording**: Capturing audio or video of a person in a private setting without their consent. This includes placing hidden cameras or microphones in homes, offices, or other private spaces.
- **Wiretapping**: Intercepting telephone calls, electronic messages, or other forms of private communication without judicial authorization.
- **Disclosure**: Sharing or distributing recordings of private communications that were obtained without consent, even if the person sharing them was not the one who made the recording.

The law focuses on the expectation of privacy. A private setting includes any place where a person reasonably believes their words and actions are not being observed or recorded by others.

## Penalties

Violations of private communication laws carry penalties of **imprisonment**, with the exact sentence depending on the severity and circumstances of the offense. Aggravating factors, such as distributing intimate recordings or using intercepted communications for extortion, can result in longer sentences. Civil liability for damages may also apply, allowing victims to seek compensation.

## Public Spaces Exception

The law recognizes that individuals in **public spaces** have a reduced expectation of privacy. Recording people in streets, parks, public events, or other open areas is generally not considered a criminal offense, provided the recording does not constitute harassment or target private activities that a person would reasonably expect to remain unseen.

## Authorized Interception

Law enforcement may intercept private communications with a **judicial order** when investigating serious crimes. This process is regulated by the Criminal Procedure Code and requires showing probable cause. Unauthorized interception by government agents is itself a criminal offense.

## Practical Advice

Before recording any conversation or interaction in a private setting, always obtain explicit consent from all parties involved. When in doubt about whether a location qualifies as private, err on the side of caution.
