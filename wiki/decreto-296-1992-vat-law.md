# Decreto 296 (1992) — VAT Law (IVA)

## Summary

The Law on the Tax on the Transfer of Tangible Personal Property and on the Provision of Services (commonly known as the IVA or VAT Law) establishes a value-added tax applicable to the transfer, import, export, and consumption of tangible personal property, as well as the provision, import, export, and self-consumption of services in El Salvador. Enacted on July 24, 1992, and published in Diario Oficial No. 143, Tomo 316, on July 31, 1992, this law replaced the old stamp tax (impuesto de timbres) system with a modern, non-cascading VAT at a rate of 13%. It is the principal indirect tax law in El Salvador and has been extensively amended over the years, with many procedural and administrative provisions later absorbed by the Tax Code (Decreto 230 of 2000).

## Key Provisions

- **Article 1 -- Scope of the Tax.** Establishes the VAT on transfers, imports, exports, and consumption of tangible personal property and on the provision, import, export, and self-consumption of services.

- **Article 4 -- Taxable Event for Transfers.** The transfer of ownership of tangible personal property for valuable consideration constitutes a taxable event.

- **Article 5 -- Definition of Tangible Personal Property.** Any tangible good that can be transported from one place to another. Securities and financial instruments are considered intangible personal property. Monetary loans and credits are treated as services.

- **Article 7 -- Broad Definition of Transfer.** Enumerates acts treated as transfers, including public auctions, barters, contributions of goods to companies, reorganizations, dissolutions, and promissory sales followed by delivery of possession.

- **Article 8 -- Moment the Tax Accrues on Transfers.** The tax is triggered when the document evidencing the transaction is issued, or earlier if payment is made or goods are delivered before document issuance.

- **Article 11 -- Withdrawal of Goods from the Business.** Withdrawal or removal of tangible goods from business inventory for personal use by the taxpayer, partners, directors, or staff constitutes a taxable event equivalent to a transfer.

- **Article 14 -- Import and Internment of Goods and Services.** The definitive import or internment into the country of tangible personal property and of services constitutes a taxable event. Import of services occurs when the activity is performed abroad but consumed exclusively in El Salvador.

- **Article 16 -- Taxable Event for Services.** The provision of services arising from any act, convention, or contract where one party provides services and the other pays a fee, honorarium, commission, interest, premium, royalty, or any other form of remuneration is taxable. Self-consumption of services is also taxable.

- **Article 17 -- Definition of Services.** Provides a comprehensive enumeration including: professional services, leases of movable and immovable property used for commercial purposes, engineering works, construction, freight transport, intellectual property licensing, membership fees, and reimbursement of expenses.

- **Article 22 -- Taxpayers on Transfers.** Producers, wholesalers, retailers, and anyone who habitually transfers tangible personal property in the course of their business are taxpayers. Occasional transfers of goods acquired without intent to resell are excluded.

- **Article 28 -- Small Taxpayer Exclusion.** Persons whose total transfers and services in the prior twelve months were below fifty thousand colones (approximately USD 5,714) and whose total assets are below twenty thousand colones (approximately USD 2,286) are excluded from taxpayer status. This exclusion does not apply to corporations or importers.

- **Article 45 -- Exempt Imports.** Exemptions include: diplomatic and consular imports under international conventions, imports by international organizations, traveler baggage, donations to qualified nonprofits, municipal imports for community benefit, machinery for productive fixed assets, public transit vehicles, and antiretroviral medications imported by public health institutions.

- **Article 46 -- Exempt Services.** Exemptions cover: public health services, residential housing leases, services rendered under labor law, public cultural events, educational services from authorized institutions, banking deposit and lending interest operations, issuance of government bonds, public water supply, public land transit of passengers, personal insurance premiums, pension fund commissions, the National Lottery, and rural community water systems.

- **Article 54 -- Tax Rate.** The VAT rate is thirteen percent (13%) applied to the tax base.

- **Article 64 -- Calculation of Tax Payable.** The tax payable is the difference between the output tax (debito fiscal) generated on taxable sales in the tax period and the input tax (credito fiscal) paid on purchases and imports in the same period.

- **Article 65 -- Requirements for Input Tax Credit Deduction.** Input tax is deductible only on: goods for resale inventory, fixed assets retaining their identity (not incorporated into real property), services used in the business, and general expenses necessary for the taxpayer's taxed activity. Credits must be supported by original tax credit receipts and properly recorded in purchase books.

- **Article 65-A -- Non-Deductible Input Tax.** Lists specific categories where input tax cannot be deducted: food and drink (unless the taxpayer's business is food), luxury vehicles, hotel services unrelated to business, personal airline tickets, clothing and jewelry (unless the taxpayer's business is selling those), personal consumption items, alcoholic beverages and cigarettes, and any acquisition not indispensable to the taxpayer's business activity.

- **Article 66 -- Proportional Deduction (Pro-Rata Rule).** When a taxpayer performs both taxable and exempt or non-subject operations in a period, the deductible input tax is proportional. The factor is calculated by dividing taxable operations by total operations (taxable plus exempt plus non-subject). This calculation is cumulative within the fiscal year and must be recalculated at the start of the following year.

- **Article 74 -- Exports.** Exports of tangible personal property for use abroad and services provided in El Salvador to non-resident users for exclusive use abroad are subject to special rules.

- **Article 75 -- Zero Rate for Exports.** Exports are subject to a zero percent (0%) rate, allowing exporters to recover all input tax credits.

- **Article 77 -- Refund of Export Credits.** Exporters who cannot fully offset their input tax credits against output tax may request a refund from the DGII within thirty days. The refund cannot exceed 13% of the export value shown in customs documents.

- **Article 93 -- Tax Period and Declaration.** The tax period is one calendar month. Taxpayers must file a monthly sworn declaration reporting taxable, exempt, and non-subject operations, along with the corresponding output and input tax.

- **Article 94 -- Filing Deadline and Payment.** The declaration and payment must be submitted within the first ten business days of the month following the tax period. Import VAT is settled at customs at the same time as customs duties.

## Who This Affects

- **All businesses operating in El Salvador** that sell tangible personal property or provide services, including producers, wholesalers, retailers, and service providers.
- **Importers** of goods and services, whether habitual or occasional.
- **Exporters**, who benefit from the zero-rate regime and may claim refunds of input tax credits.
- **Professionals and freelancers** providing independent services (lawyers, accountants, engineers, notaries, consultants, etc.).
- **Construction companies and contractors** providing construction, engineering, and maintenance services.
- **Landlords** leasing commercial or industrial property (residential leases are exempt).
- **Financial institutions**, which enjoy exemptions on deposit and lending interest operations but are subject to VAT on other services.
- **Consumers**, who bear the economic burden of the 13% tax as the final price includes VAT.
- **Small operators** below the statutory thresholds who are excluded from taxpayer obligations but cannot claim input tax credits.
- **Government entities and public institutions**, which are subject to VAT when performing taxable activities regardless of other exemptions in their organic laws.

## Related Laws

- **Decreto 230 (2000) -- Tax Code (Codigo Tributario).** Absorbed most of the procedural, administrative, infraction, and sanction provisions originally in the VAT Law. Articles on registration, document issuance, accounting records, auditing, notifications, penalties, and enforcement were derogated and replaced by the Tax Code.
- **Decreto 134 (1991) -- Income Tax Law (Ley de Impuesto sobre la Renta).** Cross-referenced extensively for nonprofit qualification (Art. 6), donation deductions, and the treatment of non-deductible VAT as a cost for income tax purposes.
- **Decreto 671 (1970) -- Commercial Code (Codigo de Comercio).** Relevant for determining habituality of commercial acts and the classification of business entities.
- **Reglamento de la Ley de IVA (Executive Decree).** The implementing regulations that detail refund procedures, documentation requirements, and other operational aspects.
- **Decreto 645 (2005) -- Authentic Interpretation of Art. 14.** Clarifies that import of services is only taxable when the service is utilized exclusively within Salvadoran territory.
- **Decreto 820 (1994) -- Authentic Interpretation of Art. 45(d).** Clarifies that the traveler baggage exemption refers only to personal effects and does not include motor vehicles.

## Common Questions

**Q: What is the current VAT rate in El Salvador?**
A: The VAT rate is 13%, as established in Article 54. This rate has been in effect since Decreto 877 of 2000 amended the original rate. It applies uniformly to all taxable transactions unless a specific exemption or the zero rate (for exports) applies.

**Q: Who is required to register as a VAT taxpayer?**
A: Any person or entity that habitually transfers tangible personal property or provides services in the course of business must register, unless their annual sales are below approximately USD 5,714 and total assets below approximately USD 2,286. All corporations and importers must register regardless of volume.

**Q: Are exports subject to VAT?**
A: Exports are taxed at a rate of zero percent (0%) under Article 75. This means exporters do not charge VAT on their export sales but are entitled to recover all input tax credits associated with their export activity. If credits exceed output tax, exporters may request a refund from the tax authority (DGII) under the procedure in Article 77.

**Q: What services are exempt from VAT?**
A: Key exempt services include: public health services, residential rental housing, employment-based services under labor law, educational services from authorized institutions, banking deposit and loan interest, public water supply, public land passenger transport, personal insurance premiums, pension fund commissions, and the National Lottery. The full list is in Article 46.

**Q: Can a business deduct VAT paid on all its purchases?**
A: No. Input tax (credito fiscal) is deductible only if the purchase is indispensable to the taxpayer's registered business activity and generates taxable output. Article 65-A lists specific non-deductible items including food (unless the business sells food), luxury vehicles, personal items, alcohol, and cigarettes. Additionally, purchases must be documented with proper tax credit receipts (comprobantes de credito fiscal) and recorded in the purchase ledger.

**Q: How does the proportionality rule work for mixed operations?**
A: Under Article 66, if a taxpayer has both taxable and exempt (or non-subject) operations, input tax credits are deductible only in proportion to the ratio of taxable operations to total operations. This proportional factor is calculated cumulatively throughout the fiscal year, and a final recalculation is performed at the beginning of the next fiscal year to adjust any over- or under-deduction.

**Q: What happens to the input tax credit balance if it exceeds the output tax?**
A: Under Article 67, if input tax exceeds output tax in a given period, the excess carries forward to subsequent periods until fully offset. For exporters specifically, Article 77 provides a mechanism to request a cash refund of unrecovered credits, subject to a cap of 13% of the documented export value.

**Q: When must VAT returns be filed and paid?**
A: The tax period is one calendar month (Article 93). The monthly sworn declaration and corresponding payment must be filed within the first ten business days of the following month (Article 94). VAT on imports is paid at customs at the time of clearing the goods.
