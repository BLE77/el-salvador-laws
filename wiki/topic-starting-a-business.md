# Starting and Running a Business in El Salvador

## Summary

Starting a business in El Salvador requires navigating several legal frameworks: forming a legal entity under the Commercial Code (D-671/1970), registering for taxes under the Tax Code (D-230/2000) and Income Tax Law (D-134/1991), obtaining municipal permits under the Municipal Code (D-274/1986), and optionally protecting your brand under the Intellectual Property Law (D-66/2024). Foreigners can own 100% of a Salvadoran company with no restrictions on equity ownership. Businesses operating in designated free trade zones may qualify for substantial tax exemptions under the Free Trade Zones Law (D-405/1998).

The most common business structure is the *sociedad anonima* (SA), which is the Salvadoran equivalent of a corporation. The entire process -- from drafting articles of incorporation to obtaining a municipal operating permit -- can typically be completed in 2-4 weeks, though timelines vary by municipality.

## Step 1: Choose a Business Entity Type

The Commercial Code (D-671/1970, Book II) defines the business structures available in El Salvador. The three most common are:

### Sociedad Anonima (SA) -- Corporation

The SA is the most widely used entity for medium-to-large businesses and the standard choice for foreign investors.

| Detail | Value |
|---|---|
| **Legal basis** | D-671/1970, Book II |
| **Minimum shareholders** | 2 (can be individuals or legal entities) |
| **Minimum capital** | $2,000 USD |
| **Capital paid at formation** | At least 25% of subscribed capital must be paid in at formation |
| **Liability** | Shareholders' liability is limited to their capital contribution |
| **Governance** | Board of directors (*junta directiva*), general shareholders' assembly (*junta general de accionistas*), statutory auditor (*auditor externo*) |
| **Shares** | Divided into shares (*acciones*) that are freely transferable unless the bylaws impose restrictions |

**Key points:**
- The SA must have a legal representative (*representante legal*), typically the president of the board of directors.
- Annual financial statements must be audited by an external auditor.
- A legal reserve (*reserva legal*) of 5% of net annual profits must be set aside until the reserve reaches 20% of the stated capital (the legal reserve is tax-deductible per Art. 91, D-134/1991).

### Sociedad de Responsabilidad Limitada (SRL) -- Limited Liability Company

The SRL is simpler and more suitable for smaller businesses or closely held ventures.

| Detail | Value |
|---|---|
| **Legal basis** | D-671/1970, Book II |
| **Minimum partners** | 2 |
| **Maximum partners** | 25 |
| **Minimum capital** | $2,000 USD |
| **Capital paid at formation** | At least 5% of each partner's contribution must be paid in |
| **Liability** | Partners' liability is limited to their capital contribution |
| **Governance** | Managed by one or more administrators (*administradores*) appointed in the articles of incorporation or by partner resolution |

**Key points:**
- Ownership interests are called *participaciones sociales*, not shares. They are not freely transferable -- transfer requires consent of partners holding a majority of the capital, unless the bylaws say otherwise.
- The cap of 25 partners makes SRLs unsuitable for businesses expecting many investors.
- SRLs have fewer corporate governance requirements than SAs (no mandatory board of directors).

### Empresa Individual de Responsabilidad Limitada -- Individual Limited Liability Enterprise

This is a single-owner entity with limited liability, an alternative to operating as a sole proprietor.

| Detail | Value |
|---|---|
| **Legal basis** | D-671/1970 |
| **Owners** | Exactly 1 natural person |
| **Minimum capital** | $2,000 USD |
| **Liability** | Owner's liability is limited to the capital assigned to the enterprise |

**Key points:**
- Only natural persons (not legal entities) can form this type of enterprise.
- The owner must clearly separate personal assets from enterprise assets.
- This entity cannot issue shares or participaciones.

### Other Structures

The Commercial Code also recognizes:
- **Sociedad en Nombre Colectivo** (general partnership) -- all partners have unlimited liability. Rarely used.
- **Sociedad en Comandita Simple** (limited partnership) -- has both general and limited partners. Uncommon in practice.
- **Sociedad en Comandita por Acciones** (limited partnership by shares) -- hybrid form. Also uncommon.
- **Sole proprietor (comerciante individual)** -- a natural person engaging in commerce under their own name, with no liability protection. The simplest option but offers no asset separation.

## Step 2: Register the Entity

### Formation Process

1. **Draft the articles of incorporation** (*escritura de constitucion*). This must be done before a Salvadoran notary public (*notario*). The document must include: the name and purpose of the entity, names and nationalities of founders, initial capital, governance structure, duration (can be indefinite), and the initial legal representative.

2. **Obtain a unique business name**. The name must be checked against the Commercial Registry (*Registro de Comercio*) to confirm it is not already in use. The registry is managed by the National Registry Center (*Centro Nacional de Registros*, CNR).

3. **Deposit the initial capital**. The minimum paid-in capital must be deposited in a Salvadoran bank account opened in the name of the company being formed.

4. **Register with the Commercial Registry** (*Registro de Comercio*). File the notarized articles of incorporation along with the required documents. The registry will issue:
   - The commercial registration (*matricula de comercio*), which must be renewed annually.
   - Registration of the accounting system and legalized commercial books.

5. **Obtain the balance sheet registration** (*inscripcion del balance inicial*). The initial balance sheet must be prepared by a licensed public accountant and registered.

**Legal basis:** Arts. 2-5, 24 of D-671/1970 (merchant obligations and commercial registry requirements).

### Annual Obligations at the Commercial Registry

- Renew the *matricula de comercio* each year.
- File annual financial statements.
- Register any changes to the bylaws, capital, legal representative, or board of directors.

## Step 3: Tax Registration

The Tax Code (D-230/2000) and Income Tax Law (D-134/1991) establish the tax obligations for all businesses.

### Obtain NIT and NRC

Every business must register with the Ministry of Finance (*Ministerio de Hacienda*) through the Direccion General de Impuestos Internos (DGII) to obtain:

| Registration | What It Is | Who Needs It |
|---|---|---|
| **NIT** (Numero de Identificacion Tributaria) | Unique tax identification number | All taxpayers (individuals and entities) |
| **NRC** (Numero de Registro de Contribuyente) | IVA (VAT) taxpayer registration number | All businesses that transfer goods or provide services subject to IVA |

**Key points:**
- The NIT is obtained at the time of formation; the NRC is obtained when registering as an IVA taxpayer.
- Both numbers must appear on all tax invoices (*comprobantes de credito fiscal*) and consumer receipts (*facturas*).
- IVA registration is mandatory for businesses with annual revenues exceeding the threshold established by law. In practice, virtually all commercial entities register.

### Filing Obligations

| Obligation | Deadline | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Monthly IVA return** (F-07) | Within 10 business days of the following month | D-296/1992 (IVA Law) |
| **Monthly income tax advance payment** (*pago a cuenta*, 1.75% of gross income) | Within 10 business days of the following month | Art. 72, D-134/1991 |
| **Monthly income tax withholding return** | Within 10 business days of the following month | Art. 152, D-230/2000 |
| **Annual income tax return** | By April 30 | Art. 74, D-134/1991 |
| **Annual IVA informational return** | By January 31 | D-296/1992 |

## Step 4: Understand Business Income Tax

The Income Tax Law (D-134/1991) establishes the following corporate rates:

| Annual Gross Revenue | Tax Rate | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Over $150,000 | **30%** on taxable income | Art. 41, D-134/1991 |
| $150,000 or less | **25%** on taxable income | Art. 41, D-134/1991 |

### Additional Tax Rules for Businesses

- **Advance payments (pago a cuenta):** All businesses must make monthly advance payments of 1.75% of gross monthly income, which are credited against the annual tax liability (Art. 72, D-134/1991).
- **Capital gains:** Taxed at 10% on the gain (Art. 42, D-134/1991).
- **Tax period:** Calendar year, January 1 to December 31 (Art. 14, D-134/1991).
- **Deductible expenses:** Costs and expenses necessary for producing income and maintaining its source are deductible (Art. 29, D-134/1991). This includes salaries, rent, utilities, depreciation, professional fees, and the legal reserve.
- **Legal reserve deduction:** The 5% legal reserve set aside annually (up to 20% of capital) is deductible (Art. 91, D-134/1991).
- **IVA (Value Added Tax):** 13% on the transfer of goods and provision of services (D-296/1992). IVA is not an income tax -- it is a consumption tax collected by the business and remitted to the government.

## Step 5: Obtain Municipal Business Permits

The Municipal Code (D-274/1986) gives municipalities broad authority over local business regulation.

### What You Need

Every business operating within a municipality must obtain:

1. **Municipal operating permit** (*licencia de funcionamiento* or *permiso de apertura*). This is issued by the *alcaldia* (mayor's office) of the municipality where the business is physically located.

2. **Municipal tax registration.** Businesses are subject to municipal taxes based on their assets or revenue, depending on the specific municipal tax ordinance (*tarifa de arbitrios*). Each municipality has its own tax schedule approved by the Legislative Assembly.

3. **Municipal tax clearance certificate** (*solvencia municipal*). Article 100 of D-274/1986 requires municipal tax clearance before any corporate modification can be registered in the Commercial Registry or Property Registry. This means you cannot change directors, amend bylaws, or transfer property if you have outstanding municipal taxes.

### Municipal Tax Rates

Municipal tax rates vary by municipality. They are established by individual municipal tax laws (*tarifas de arbitrios municipales*), which must be approved by the Legislative Assembly. The general framework is set by the General Municipal Tax Law (D-86/1991). Common approaches include:

- Tax based on business assets (common in San Salvador)
- Tax based on gross revenue
- Fixed fees for small businesses

Fines for non-compliance can reach up to 8 monthly minimum wages for commerce (Arts. 126-137, D-274/1986).

## Step 6: Protect Your Brand (Optional but Recommended)

The Intellectual Property Law (D-66/2024) governs trademark registration and other IP protections.

### Trademark Registration

- **Administered by:** The Salvadoran Institute of Intellectual Property (*Instituto Salvadoreno de la Propiedad Intelectual*), a dependency of the National Registry Center (Art. 6, D-66/2024).
- **Who can register:** Any natural or legal person, regardless of nationality or domicile (Art. 4, D-66/2024). Foreigners have the same rights as Salvadorans.
- **What can be registered:** Trademarks, trade names, commercial slogans, and other distinctive signs.
- **Software protection:** Computer programs and video games are automatically protected as copyrighted works (Art. 16, D-66/2024). No registration is required for copyright protection, though registration provides evidentiary advantages.
- **Duration:** Trademark registrations are valid for 10 years and renewable for successive 10-year periods.

### Other IP Protections

- **Patents:** Inventions that are new, involve an inventive step, and are industrially applicable can be patented.
- **Copyright:** Protected automatically upon creation, without need for registration (Art. 15, D-66/2024). Covers literary works, music, art, architecture, software, and more.
- **Trade secrets:** Protected against unfair competition under the same law.

## Foreign Investment

### Can Foreigners Own 100% of a Salvadoran Company?

**Yes.** There is no general restriction on foreign ownership of Salvadoran companies. Foreigners can own 100% of the shares of a *sociedad anonima* or hold 100% of the *participaciones* in an SRL. El Salvador's investment framework is designed to attract foreign capital, and the law provides national treatment to foreign investors -- meaning foreign and domestic investors are subject to the same rules.

### Key Points for Foreign Investors

- **No prior government approval required** for most sectors. Foreign investors do not need authorization from any ministry to form a company or acquire shares.
- **Repatriation of profits:** There are no exchange controls or restrictions on repatriating profits, dividends, or capital. El Salvador uses the US dollar as its legal tender (since 2001), which eliminates currency conversion risk.
- **Legal representative requirement:** The company must have a legal representative (*representante legal*) who can act on behalf of the company in El Salvador. This person does not need to be Salvadoran, but must have the legal capacity to act in the country.
- **Restricted sectors:** Certain activities may have specific requirements or limitations, such as banking (requires Superintendency approval), broadcasting, and activities related to national security. However, these are sector-specific regulations, not general foreign investment restrictions.
- **Land ownership:** Foreigners can own urban real estate. Rural land ownership by foreigners is constitutionally limited (Art. 109 of the Constitution limits rural land ownership to 245 hectares for individuals), but companies -- including those with foreign shareholders -- can own rural land through the legal entity.
- **Bilateral investment treaties:** El Salvador has bilateral investment treaties with several countries and is a member of CAFTA-DR (the Dominican Republic-Central America Free Trade Agreement with the United States), which provides additional investment protections.

## Free Trade Zones

The Free Trade Zones Law (D-405/1998) offers significant tax incentives for businesses engaged in export-oriented manufacturing, assembly, and commercialization.

### Who Qualifies

To qualify as a free trade zone user, a business must meet at least one of these criteria (Art. 17-A, D-405/1998):

| Criterion | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Initial fixed-asset investment | At least $500,000 USD within first 2 years |
| Permanent jobs | At least 50 from the first year |
| Permanent jobs (commercializers) | At least 5 from the first year |

### Tax Benefits for Free Trade Zone Users

| Benefit | Metropolitan Area | Outside Metropolitan Area | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Income tax exemption (full)** | 15 years | 20 years | Art. 17(d), D-405/1998 |
| **Income tax exemption (60%)** | Next 10 years | Next 15 years | Art. 17(d) |
| **Income tax exemption (40%)** | Next 10 years | Next 10 years | Art. 17(d) |
| **Municipal tax exemption (full)** | 15 years | 20 years | Art. 17(e) |
| **Import duty exemption** | Duration of operations | Duration of operations | Art. 17(a-c) |
| **Real estate transfer tax exemption** | Full | Full | Art. 17(f) |

### Additional Benefits

- **Strategic industries bonus:** Manufacturers of microprocessors, integrated circuits, vehicle parts, computer equipment, medical devices, or energy generation equipment get an additional 5 years of full exemption (Art. 17-B).
- **Investment doubling bonus:** Users who double their initial investment (minimum $500,000) receive an additional 10-year extension of all exemptions (Art. 17).
- **Zero-rate IVA:** Local purchases of goods and services necessary for the authorized activity are subject to 0% IVA (Art. 25).

### Limitations

- Companies that previously paid income tax in El Salvador generally cannot switch to free trade zone benefits (Art. 54-B, anti-abuse provision).
- Certain activities are excluded, including oil/gas, cement, mining, explosives, and sugar production (Art. 6).
- All labor and social security laws must be fully complied with (Art. 29).

## Common Questions

### 1. What is the easiest and cheapest way to start a business in El Salvador?

The simplest option is to register as a *comerciante individual* (sole proprietor) at the Commercial Registry. This requires no minimum capital and no articles of incorporation -- just registration. However, you have **no liability protection**: your personal assets are exposed to business debts. For liability protection with minimal complexity, the *empresa individual de responsabilidad limitada* (individual limited liability enterprise) requires $2,000 minimum capital and only one owner.

### 2. What is the difference between an SA and an SRL?

A *sociedad anonima* (SA) is a corporation with freely transferable shares, a mandatory board of directors, and no cap on the number of shareholders. An SRL (limited liability company) has a maximum of 25 partners, ownership interests that are not freely transferable (requiring partner consent), and simpler governance. Both require $2,000 minimum capital. The SA is the standard choice for businesses seeking outside investors or operating at scale. The SRL is better for small, closely held businesses where partners know and trust each other.

### 3. Can a foreigner own 100% of a company in El Salvador?

Yes. There are no general restrictions on foreign ownership of Salvadoran companies. A foreigner can own 100% of an SA or SRL. No government pre-approval is needed for most sectors. El Salvador uses the US dollar, so there is no currency risk, and there are no restrictions on repatriating profits or capital. Certain regulated sectors (banking, broadcasting) have additional licensing requirements but do not prohibit foreign ownership outright.

### 4. How much does it cost to incorporate a company?

Typical costs for forming an SA include: notarial fees for drafting the articles of incorporation (varies, roughly $500-$1,500 depending on complexity), Commercial Registry registration fees, NIT/NRC registration (no government fee for the tax ID itself), municipal permit fees (vary by municipality), and the $2,000 minimum capital deposit (which becomes the company's working capital). Total out-of-pocket costs excluding the capital deposit are generally in the range of $1,000-$3,000 when using a local attorney. Legal fees are the largest variable cost.

### 5. What is the corporate tax rate?

The standard corporate income tax rate is **30%** on taxable income for companies with annual gross revenue over $150,000 (Art. 41, D-134/1991). Companies with gross revenue of $150,000 or less pay **25%**. In addition, all businesses must make monthly advance payments (*pago a cuenta*) of 1.75% of gross monthly income, credited against the annual tax liability (Art. 72, D-134/1991). Capital gains are taxed separately at 10% (Art. 42).

### 6. Do I need a municipal permit even if I already registered at the Commercial Registry?

Yes. Registration at the Commercial Registry (national level) and obtaining a municipal operating permit (local level) are separate requirements. The Commercial Registry makes your company a legally recognized entity. The municipal permit authorizes you to operate a business at a specific physical location within that municipality. You cannot legally open your doors without both. Additionally, you need municipal tax clearance (*solvencia municipal*) for any future changes to your company's registration at the Commercial Registry (Art. 100, D-274/1986).

### 7. What are the ongoing annual obligations for a Salvadoran company?

At minimum: (a) renew the *matricula de comercio* at the Commercial Registry annually; (b) file monthly IVA returns and income tax advance payments; (c) file the annual income tax return by April 30; (d) pay municipal taxes; (e) maintain audited financial statements (for SAs); (f) hold annual shareholders' or partners' meeting; (g) set aside the legal reserve (5% of profits until it reaches 20% of capital). If you have employees, add ISSS, AFP, and INCAF contributions plus income tax withholding.

### 8. How do I register a trademark?

File an application with the Salvadoran Institute of Intellectual Property (*Instituto Salvadoreno de la Propiedad Intelectual*), which is part of the National Registry Center. Any person -- Salvadoran or foreign -- can register a trademark (Art. 4, D-66/2024). The registration is valid for 10 years and renewable. You should conduct a prior search to ensure the mark is not already registered. The process involves filing the application, a formal examination, publication for opposition, and issuance of the registration certificate.

### 9. What tax benefits are available for businesses in free trade zones?

Free trade zone users can receive **full income tax exemption for 15-20 years** (depending on location), followed by partial exemptions that can extend for decades more. They also receive full municipal tax exemption, duty-free importation of machinery and raw materials, and zero-rate IVA on local purchases. To qualify, you generally need at least $500,000 in investment or 50 permanent jobs (Art. 17-A, D-405/1998). These benefits are designed for export-oriented manufacturing and assembly operations, not for businesses selling primarily to the domestic market.

### 10. Is El Salvador a good place for a foreign entrepreneur to start a business?

From a regulatory standpoint, El Salvador offers several advantages: no restrictions on foreign ownership, the US dollar as legal currency (eliminating exchange rate risk), relatively low minimum capital requirements ($2,000), a straightforward incorporation process, free trade zone incentives for manufacturers, and bilateral investment treaties including CAFTA-DR. The corporate tax rate of 25-30% is competitive in the region. Challenges include navigating bureaucracy across multiple government offices (Commercial Registry, tax authority, municipality) and the need for a local notary and often a local attorney to handle formation documents in Spanish.
