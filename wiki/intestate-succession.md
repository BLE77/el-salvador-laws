# Intestate Succession in El Salvador

When a person dies without a valid will in El Salvador, their estate is distributed according to the rules of **sucesion intestada** (intestate succession) established in the **Civil Code (Decreto 644)**. The law sets a fixed order of priority among surviving relatives.

## Order of Succession

The Civil Code establishes the following hierarchy for inheritance:

1. **Children** (hijos): All children inherit in equal shares, regardless of whether they are born within or outside of marriage
2. **Parents** (padres): If there are no children, the estate passes to the deceased's parents
3. **Surviving spouse** (conyuge sobreviviente): The spouse has rights to the estate, including usufruct (right to use and benefit from property)
4. **Siblings** (hermanos): If none of the above survive, siblings inherit
5. **Other relatives**: More distant relatives up to the degree established by law
6. **The State**: If no qualifying relatives exist, the estate passes to the government

## Surviving Spouse's Rights

The surviving spouse receives **usufruct** over a portion of the estate, meaning they have the right to use and enjoy the property during their lifetime even if ownership passes to other heirs. The specific share depends on which other heirs survive.

## Legal Process: Declaratoria de Herederos

To claim an inheritance without a will, heirs must file a **declaratoria de herederos** in civil court. This judicial process:

- Establishes who the legal heirs are
- Requires proof of kinship (birth certificates, marriage certificates)
- Involves publication of notices to allow other potential heirs to appear
- Results in a court order declaring the rightful heirs and their shares

Once declared, heirs can proceed with the partition and distribution of assets. A notary or attorney is essential for navigating this process.
