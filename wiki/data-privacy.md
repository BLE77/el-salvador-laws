# Data Privacy and Protection in El Salvador

El Salvador does **not yet have a comprehensive data protection law** comparable to the EU's GDPR or similar frameworks in other Latin American countries. However, several constitutional provisions and existing laws provide partial protections for personal data.

## Constitutional Protections

The **Constitution of El Salvador** protects the right to **intimacy** (intimidad) and personal dignity. Article 2 recognizes the right to honor, personal and family intimacy, and one's own image. These constitutional principles serve as the foundation for data privacy claims.

## Penal Code Provisions

The **Penal Code (Decreto 1030 of 1997)** criminalizes certain data-related offenses:

- **Unauthorized access** to personal data stored in computer systems or databases
- **Disclosure of confidential information** obtained through unauthorized access
- **Interception of communications** — unauthorized wiretapping or monitoring of private communications

These provisions carry penalties including fines and imprisonment.

## Sector-Specific Protections

- **Financial institutions** — banks and financial entities are required to protect customer data under banking secrecy laws. The **Superintendencia del Sistema Financiero** oversees compliance.
- **Telecommunications** — providers must protect subscriber data and communications under the Telecommunications Law.
- **Health records** — the Health Code imposes confidentiality obligations on medical professionals regarding patient information.

## Habeas Data

El Salvador recognizes the remedy of **habeas data**, which allows individuals to petition courts to access, correct, or delete personal information held by public or private entities that is inaccurate, incomplete, or unlawfully obtained. This is the primary legal mechanism for individuals to challenge misuse of their personal data.

## Practical Considerations

The absence of a dedicated data protection law means enforcement is fragmented. Individuals concerned about data misuse should rely on constitutional remedies and sector-specific regulations. Legislative proposals for a comprehensive data protection framework have been discussed but not yet enacted.
