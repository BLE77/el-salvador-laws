# Decreto 405 (1998) — Free Trade Zones Law

## Summary

The Free Trade Zones and Marketing Law (Ley de Zonas Francas Industriales y de Comercializacion) is El Salvador's primary legal framework for establishing, operating, and regulating free trade zones (zonas francas) and active improvement warehouses (Depositos para Perfeccionamiento Activo, or DPAs). Enacted on September 3, 1998, and published in Diario Oficial No. 176, Tomo 340, on September 23, 1998, it replaced the earlier 1990 Regime of Free Trade Zones and Fiscal Compounds law (Decreto 461). The law creates a comprehensive incentive regime designed to attract foreign investment, promote export-oriented manufacturing (especially maquila/assembly operations), generate productive employment, and insert El Salvador's economy into the global marketplace. It grants substantial tax exemptions -- including income tax, municipal taxes, import duties, and real estate transfer taxes -- to developers, administrators, and users of free trade zones, as well as to DPA operators, with differentiated benefit periods depending on whether operations are located inside or outside the San Salvador Metropolitan Area. The law has been amended numerous times (at least 17 reform decrees) and includes an authentic interpretation decree (Decreto 397 of 2019) clarifying interaction with the International Services Law.

## Key Provisions

**Article 1 -- Purpose.** The law regulates the operation of Free Trade Zones and Active Improvement Warehouses (DPAs), as well as the benefits and responsibilities of the companies that develop, administer, or use them.

**Article 2 -- Definitions.** Establishes 24 key definitions including: Free Trade Zone (area where goods are treated as outside the national customs territory for import/export tax purposes); DPA (area of the national territory subject to special customs treatment where goods enter with suspension of import taxes for transformation); Developer (person who establishes and develops free trade zones); User (person authorized to operate within a free trade zone); Beneficiary (developers, administrators, and users authorized by the Ministry of Economy); Commercializer (beneficiary dedicated to trading industrial goods); and Vertical Free Trade Zone (delimited area formed by integrated land and buildings).

**Article 3 -- Permitted Activities.** Enterprises in free trade zones may engage in: (I) production, assembly, maquila, manufacturing, processing, transformation, or marketing of industrial goods; (II) maritime fishing for industrial transformation; (III) cultivation, processing, and marketing of flora species or unprocessed foods under natural or artificial systems; (IV) breeding of amphibians and reptiles in captivity; (V) ethyl alcohol dehydration; (VI) production using alcohols as inputs (provided the final product is not liquor); (VII) aquaculture; and (VIII) production, processing, or marketing of foods or animal feeds. Related service activities (design, cutting, stamping, quality control, recycling, repair, etc.) are also permitted but only between beneficiaries.

**Article 5 -- Categories of Beneficiaries.** Four types of entities may benefit under the law: (a) Developers of free trade zones; (b) Administrators of free trade zones; (c) Users established in free trade zones; and (d) entities whose establishments are declared DPAs.

**Article 6 -- Excluded Activities.** The law specifically prohibits benefits for: oil/gas exploration and processing; cement and clinker production; scrap metal trading; mineral exploitation from Salvadoran subsoil; explosives and radioactive materials handling; products causing environmental contamination or health damage; sugar and its derivatives (with exceptions for chemicals, cosmetics, pharmaceuticals, and food); alcohol production (except as permitted under Art. 3); synthetic sack manufacturing; prepared food supply to beneficiary employees; and machinery importation for leasing purposes. Entities with suspended or revoked benefits, those with pending tax obligations, or those engaged in illicit activities are also excluded.

**Article 10 -- Zone Development Requirements.** Developers must complete precertification, authorization, and operations-commencement stages. Free trade zones require a minimum area of 30,000 square meters (which for vertical zones can be distributed between land and buildings), common buildings (administrative offices, customs delegation office, control and surveillance booth), industrial buildings with offices, production areas, warehouses, loading/unloading zones, and parking, plus urbanization including 30% green areas, perimeter fencing, and at least one pedestrian and vehicle access point.

**Article 11 -- Developer Tax Incentives.** Authorized developers receive: (a) total income tax exemption for 10 years (Metropolitan Area) or 15 years (outside Metropolitan Area); (b) total municipal tax exemption for the same periods; (c) total exemption from real estate transfer tax on property used for the incentivized activity. An additional 10-year extension of all exemptions is available if the developer invests in expanding the zone by an area at least equal to or greater than its existing area.

**Article 17 -- Free Trade Zone User Benefits.** Users receive: (a-c) duty-free importation of machinery, equipment, raw materials, components, lubricants, and other production necessities for the duration of operations; (d) total income tax exemption for 15 years (Metropolitan Area) or 20 years (outside), followed by phased partial exemptions (60% for 10-15 years, then 40% for 10 years); (e) total municipal tax exemption for 15 years (Metropolitan Area) or 20 years (outside), followed by 90% exemption for 10-15 years and then 75% indefinitely; (f) real estate transfer tax exemption. An additional 10-year extension is available upon doubling the initial investment (minimum USD 500,000 for employment-qualifying users).

**Article 17-A -- User Qualification Requirements.** Applicants must meet at least one of: (a) initial fixed-asset investment of at least USD 500,000 within the first two years; (b) at least 50 permanent jobs from the first year; or (c) at least 5 permanent jobs from the first year (for commercializers). Greenhouses and laboratories have reduced thresholds (USD 100,000 investment, 15 jobs, minimum area of 5,000 m2 or 1,000 m2).

**Article 17-B -- Strategic Industry Bonus.** Manufacturers of microprocessors, integrated circuits, vehicle parts, computer equipment, medical devices, or energy generation equipment receive an additional 5 years of full income tax and municipal tax exemption. Enterprises declared as strategic industrial activities (investment over USD 10 million, including vehicles, aircraft, maritime vessels, or nanotechnology) receive an additional 10 years.

**Article 19 -- DPA Benefits.** DPA operators receive: (a) total exemption from import duties on machinery and equipment for the duration of operations; (b) suspension of import duties on raw materials and components; (c) exemption on lubricants and other production materials; (d) total income tax exemption for 10 years (Metropolitan Area) or 15 years (outside), followed by phased partial exemptions; (e) municipal tax exemption on the same terms; (f) real estate transfer tax exemption. An additional 10-year extension is available upon doubling initial investment (minimum USD 800,000).

**Article 19-A -- DPA Qualification Requirements.** DPA applicants must meet at least one of: (a) initial fixed-asset investment of USD 800,000 or more within two years; (b) at least 75 permanent jobs from the first year; or (c) at least 15 permanent jobs for commercializers.

**Article 22 -- Customs Regime Timelines.** Goods admitted temporarily for active improvement have a maximum stay of 12 months (non-extendable) from the date the customs declaration is accepted. Definitive transfers must occur within 12 months; temporary transfers within 6 months. Temporary transfers from free trade zones or DPAs to the national customs territory have a 2-month limit.

**Article 25 -- Zero-Rate IVA on Local Purchases.** Sales of goods and services necessary for the authorized activity, made by persons in the national customs territory to a beneficiary, are subject to a 0% IVA (value-added tax) rate. Market prices must be applied in all transactions between beneficiaries and national territory parties.

**Article 27 -- Domestic Sales and Waste.** Raw materials and inputs may be sold domestically upon authorization from the Ministry of Economy, paying full import duties. Waste and scrap may be sold domestically with import duties on customs value. Waste handled by authorized environmental management companies for destruction or recycling is duty-free. Goods unfit for industrial or commercial use may be destroyed under customs supervision.

**Article 28 -- User Obligations.** Users must: maintain electronic inventory records available to the Customs Directorate; submit semiannual operations reports to the Ministry of Economy; allow inspections; maintain adequate storage facilities; keep cost records for domestic sales; comply with social security payment verification requirements; and report any irregularities with merchandise.

**Article 29 -- Labor and Social Security Obligations.** All beneficiaries must comply with labor and social security laws, including: freedom of association, right to unionize, prohibition of forced labor, minimum working age, acceptable conditions regarding minimum wage, working hours, and occupational health and safety. In the event of closure, workers must receive full indemnification, annual bonus, and proportional vacation pay.

**Article 36 -- Sanctions.** Minor infractions receive written warning (repeat offenses: fine of 3 minimum monthly wages); serious infractions receive fines of 5 minimum monthly wages; very serious infractions result in temporary suspension of benefits for up to 3 months (repeat offenses: permanent suspension).

**Article 40 -- Abandonment Proceedings.** When a beneficiary abandons operations, judicial proceedings may be initiated. After a 10-business-day presumption period, the court may order seizure of goods, and ultimately a public auction with proceeds distributed in order of priority: worker salaries and benefits first, then taxes and duties, then court costs, then other debts.

**Article 54-B -- Anti-Abuse Provision.** Persons who previously operated in the national customs territory and paid income tax may not subsequently qualify under this law. The same applies to entities resulting from mergers or transformations of previously-taxed companies, aimed at obtaining benefits.

## Who This Affects

- **Foreign investors and multinational corporations** seeking to establish manufacturing, assembly, or maquila operations in El Salvador under favorable tax conditions
- **Domestic companies** dedicated to export-oriented production, processing, or commercial activities that wish to benefit from import duty suspensions and tax exemptions
- **Free trade zone developers** who establish and build the physical infrastructure of industrial parks for lease or sale to operating companies
- **Zone administrators** responsible for day-to-day management and services provision within free trade zones
- **Commercializers** (wholesale/retail traders of industrial goods) who must operate from within a free trade zone
- **Workers employed in free trade zones and DPAs**, who are protected by the law's labor rights provisions including freedom of association, unionization rights, minimum wage, occupational safety, and severance protections
- **The Ministry of Economy**, which authorizes, supervises, and sanctions all beneficiaries under this law
- **The Ministry of Finance** (through the Customs Directorate and the Internal Revenue Directorate), responsible for customs regime enforcement, fiscal oversight, and tax compliance verification
- **Municipal governments**, which receive reduced or zero tax revenue from beneficiary enterprises but may offer additional incentives to promote local development
- **The Salvadoran Social Security Institute (ISSS) and Pension Fund Administrators (AFPs)**, which verify that beneficiaries are current on social security and pension contributions as a precondition for operating
- **Customs brokers and logistics operators**, who facilitate the import/export and transfer processes under the special customs regimes
- **Environmental authorities (Ministry of Environment and Natural Resources)**, which authorize waste destruction and recycling operations

## Related Laws

- **Decreto 461 (1990) -- Law of the Regime of Free Trade Zones and Fiscal Compounds** -- the predecessor law that was repealed by Decreto 405
- **Decreto 431 (2007) -- International Services Law (Ley de Servicios Internacionales)** -- regulates service parks and logistics operators that interact with free trade zone beneficiaries; Decreto 397 (2019) provides authentic interpretation of the intersection between the two laws
- **Central American Uniform Customs Code (CAUCA) and its Regulations (RECAUCA)** -- the regional customs framework that governs aduanas procedures referenced throughout this law
- **Decreto 296 (1992) -- IVA Law (Ley de Impuesto a la Transferencia de Bienes Muebles y a la Prestacion de Servicios)** -- governs the zero-rate VAT treatment on local sales to beneficiaries (Art. 25)
- **Decreto 134 (1991) -- Income Tax Law (Ley de Impuesto sobre la Renta)** -- base law for the income tax exemptions granted under this regime
- **Decreto 230 (2000) -- Tax Code (Codigo Tributario)** -- general tax compliance framework, transfer pricing rules applicable to transactions between beneficiaries and national territory parties
- **Special Law for Sanctioning Customs Infractions (Ley Especial para Sancionar Infracciones Aduaneras)** -- referenced for penalties on customs violations including defrauding customs revenue
- **Decreto 15 (1972) -- Labor Code (Codigo de Trabajo)** -- labor protections that beneficiaries must comply with, including indemnification, vacation, and bonus obligations
- **General Law for the Prevention of Risks in Workplaces** -- workplace safety standards applicable to zone facilities
- **Law of Equal Opportunities for Persons with Disabilities** -- accessibility requirements for zone infrastructure
- **Law of Territorial Development and Planning of the Metropolitan Area of San Salvador (AMSS)** -- defines the Metropolitan Area used to determine benefit periods

## Common Questions

**1. What is the difference between a Free Trade Zone and a DPA?**
A Free Trade Zone is a designated area (minimum 30,000 m2) with shared infrastructure, customs offices, and multiple tenant companies, where goods are treated as outside the national customs territory. A DPA (Deposito para Perfeccionamiento Activo) is a single company's establishment that has been declared a special customs area, typically because the company cannot locate within an existing free trade zone for technical reasons. DPAs have higher investment thresholds (USD 800,000 vs. USD 500,000) and shorter initial income tax exemption periods (10/15 years vs. 15/20 years).

**2. How long do the tax exemptions last?**
It depends on the type of beneficiary and location. Free trade zone users receive full income tax exemption for 15 years (Metropolitan Area) or 20 years (outside), followed by 60% exemption for 10-15 years, then 40% for another 10 years. DPA operators receive full income tax exemption for 10 years (Metropolitan Area) or 15 years (outside), with similar phased reductions. Developers receive 10 or 15 years of full exemption. All categories can qualify for a 10-year extension by doubling their initial investment.

**3. Can beneficiaries sell goods on the domestic Salvadoran market?**
Yes, but with conditions. Goods entering the domestic market must go through standard import procedures and pay all applicable duties and taxes (using the most-favored-nation tariff rate). The declared value cannot be less than the value at which goods originally entered the zone. Commercializers making domestic sales must pay IVA and do not benefit from income tax or municipal tax exemptions on those domestic-market transactions.

**4. What labor protections apply to workers in free trade zones?**
The law explicitly requires all beneficiaries to comply with labor, social security, and workplace safety laws. This includes the right to associate and unionize, prohibition of forced labor, minimum age requirements, minimum wage compliance, and acceptable working hours and conditions. In the event of total or partial closure, workers must receive full severance, proportional annual bonus, and vacation pay. Non-compliance with labor obligations is classified as a serious infraction that can lead to suspension of benefits.

**5. What happens if a company abandons its operations?**
If a beneficiary ceases operations without justification for 12 continuous months, it loses its status. If it abandons operations entirely without notifying customs and settling regime obligations, this constitutes the crime of customs revenue fraud. A judicial abandonment process can be initiated where courts may order seizure and public auction of remaining goods, with proceeds distributed first to workers (salaries and benefits), then to the state (taxes), then to court costs, and finally to other creditors.

**6. What activities are prohibited from receiving benefits under this law?**
Excluded activities include oil and gas exploration/processing, cement production, scrap metal trading, mineral exploitation from Salvadoran subsoil, explosives and radioactive materials, environmentally contaminating products, sugar and derivatives (with limited exceptions for chemical/pharmaceutical/food applications), alcohol production (except ethanol dehydration and certain manufacturing uses), synthetic sack manufacturing, prepared food supply to zone employees, and machinery importation for leasing.

**7. Can a company that previously paid income tax in El Salvador qualify for free trade zone benefits?**
No. Article 54-B explicitly prevents persons who previously operated in the national customs territory and paid income tax from subsequently qualifying under this law. This anti-abuse provision also applies to companies formed through mergers or transformations of previously-taxed entities, specifically designed to access the benefit regime. If the tax authorities discover that a beneficiary obtained access through such means, benefits will be revoked and all taxes owed from the date of authorization must be paid.

**8. What are the requirements to qualify as a free trade zone user?**
An applicant must meet at least one of three criteria: (a) initial fixed-asset investment of at least USD 500,000 within the first two years of operations; (b) operation with at least 50 permanent jobs from the first year; or (c) for commercializers, at least 5 permanent jobs from the first year. Greenhouse and laboratory operations have lower thresholds (USD 100,000 investment, 15 jobs, and minimum area requirements). Failure to meet the investment threshold results in revocation; failure to meet employment thresholds results in suspension of benefits until compliance is restored.
