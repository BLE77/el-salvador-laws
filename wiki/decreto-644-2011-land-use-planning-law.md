# Decreto 644 (2011) — Land Use Planning Law

## Summary

The Land Use Planning and Territorial Development Law (Ley de Ordenamiento y Desarrollo Territorial, LODT) is a comprehensive framework enacted on March 11, 2011, that governs how territory in El Salvador is planned, zoned, and developed. It establishes constitutional principles relating to land use, creates a multi-level institutional structure (national, departmental, and municipal), defines a system of planning instruments ranging from a National Plan down to local partial plans, and sets out a sanctions regime for violations. The law entered into force twelve months after its publication in the Diario Oficial (Tomo 392, No. 143, published July 29, 2011).

The law is rooted in several constitutional mandates: the state's duty to pursue justice and the common good, the social function of private property, the obligation of environmental protection, sustainable development, and coordinated action between national and municipal governments. El Salvador's high population density and rapid territorial transformation are cited as key motivations.

## Key Provisions

### Purpose and Scope (Articles 1-5)

**Article 1 -- Object of the law.** The law develops constitutional principles related to territorial planning and development; establishes rules governing those processes; enumerates guiding principles for public and municipal administration; organizes the institutions that will implement the law; and regulates planning, programming, evaluation, and management instruments as well as the sanctions regime.

**Article 2 -- Objectives.** The territorial planning system pursues environmental sustainability, social equity, territorial cohesion, economic competitiveness, orderly urban and rural development, risk prevention, gender equity, and the rational use of natural resources.

**Article 3 -- Scope of application.** The law applies to all public and private activities that affect land use throughout El Salvador's territory. It binds both national and municipal government entities.

**Article 5 -- Guiding principles.** Public administration in territorial matters must act in accordance with the principles of (1) comprehensiveness, (2) economic and social equilibrium, (3) citizen participation, (4) legality, (5) inter-institutional coordination, and (6) respect for private property.

### Institutional Framework (Articles 6-21)

**Article 14 -- National Council for Territorial Planning (CNODT).** The law creates the National Council for Territorial Planning and Development as an autonomous entity with legal personality and its own assets. It is attached to the Executive Branch through the Presidency. The Council is composed of delegates from the Presidency's Technical Secretariat, the Ministries of Public Works, Environment, Agriculture, Economy, and Education, six elected mayors, two business-sector representatives, and two academic representatives.

**Article 15 -- Functions of the National Council.** Key responsibilities include formulating the National Territorial Policy, approving national and departmental plans, coordinating inter-institutional action, creating and maintaining the National Territorial Information System (SNIT), and proposing the annual budget for the territorial planning system.

**Article 16 -- Departmental Councils.** The law creates Departmental Councils for Territorial Planning in each of El Salvador's 14 departments. These councils coordinate departmental strategies, approve micro-regional plans, and ensure coherence between national directives and local plans. Each council includes delegates from national ministries, six elected mayors (allocated proportionally among political parties), two business representatives, and two academic representatives.

**Article 19 -- Municipal micro-regional associations.** Municipal councils may form micro-regional associations for territorial planning purposes, creating joint technical offices and delegating planning functions through special ordinances.

**Article 20 -- Municipal attributions.** Municipal governments, individually or in association, coordinate and approve local plans, issue construction and subdivision permits, ensure plan compliance, and preserve designated reserve areas.

**Article 21 -- Technical teams.** National and departmental councils must employ multidisciplinary technical teams including professionals in territorial planning, environmental science, seismology, geology, sociology, law, economics, and (at the national level) archaeology.

### Planning Instruments (Articles 22-44)

**Article 22 -- System of instruments.** The territorial planning system consists of: (1) the National Territorial Policy; (2) planning instruments at national/departmental level (National Plan, departmental strategies and plans, special territorial plans) and local level (municipal/micro-regional plans, urban-rural development plans, partial plans); (3) programming instruments (territorial development programs); and (4) analysis, evaluation, and participation instruments (SNIT, territorial impact evaluation system, citizen participation system).

**Article 25 -- National Plan.** The National Plan for Territorial Planning and Development is the highest-ranking planning instrument. It identifies problems and opportunities, sets policy principles, defines intervention objectives, and establishes directives for all lower-level plans. It covers urban systems, transport corridors, natural spaces, biological corridors, water resources, risk prevention, waste management, and strategic economic activities.

**Article 30 -- Departmental plans.** These regulate the organization of public and private activities at the departmental level, addressing environmental, social, economic, rural, urban, and infrastructure dimensions. They must include strategic environmental evaluation, risk identification, geographic distribution of land uses, conservation directives, and a financial program.

**Article 34 -- Municipal and micro-regional plans.** Local plans provide detailed land use regulation including zoning (urban consolidated, urban non-consolidated, urbanizable, rural, non-urbanizable), infrastructure networks, public facility locations, building height and density norms, and environmental protection zones.

**Article 35 -- Urban and rural development plans.** These detailed plans govern urban and rural processes at the municipal or micro-regional level, establishing specific urbanization and building conditions.

**Article 43 -- Partial plans.** Partial plans detail the content of municipal or micro-regional plans for specific areas, specifying lot-level urbanization and building regimes.

### Zoning and Land Use Regime (Articles 61-70)

**Article 61 -- Compensation for land use restrictions.** Any limitation on land use that prevents the continuation of lawful economic activities entitles the property owner to compensation, except for subsurface uses subject to specific concessions or licenses.

**Article 62 -- Zoning categories.** The law establishes four main land classifications: (a) urban zones (consolidated and non-consolidated), (b) urbanizable zones, (c) rural zones with primary-sector uses, and (d) non-urbanizable zones excluded from development due to environmental protection, natural risks, or other legal restrictions.

**Article 64 -- Planning requirement.** All urban-purpose actions on land require prior approval from the competent authority based on the Municipal Territorial Plan. No land use authorizations may be granted in the absence of an approved planning instrument.

**Article 66 -- Densification compensation.** Property owners in consolidated urban zones who seek to increase population density must compensate the municipality for impacts on traffic, solid waste, basic services, and public facilities such as green areas, schools, and community services.

### Property Owners' Rights and Obligations (Article 70)

**Article 70 -- Rights and duties of landowners.** Owners may use their land according to its nature and sustainable capacity. Building rights in urban and urbanizable zones require compliance with the applicable plan, project approval, and payment of applicable taxes and fees. Owners in non-urbanizable zones may maintain compatible economic uses but must accept conservation conditions; if existing lawful uses are prohibited by a new plan, compensation is owed.

### Sanctions Regime (Articles 71-98)

**Article 74 -- Classification of infractions.** Infractions are classified as very serious or serious.

**Article 75 -- Very serious infractions.** These include activities affecting non-urbanizable or protected zones, non-compliance with precautionary measures, and initiating subdivisions that damage protected or public-interest areas.

**Article 76 -- Serious infractions.** These include unauthorized construction, subdivision, or land transformation; development of uses incompatible with planning instruments; and obstruction of inspections.

**Article 81 -- Fines.** Serious infractions carry fines of up to 200 monthly minimum wages (commerce sector); very serious infractions carry fines of up to 1,000 monthly minimum wages. Fines may be increased to eliminate any economic benefit obtained from the infraction.

**Article 82 -- Demolition.** In addition to fines, responsible parties may be ordered to demolish unauthorized works at their own expense and to pay compensation.

**Article 87 -- Nullity of illegal acts.** Administrative acts contrary to planning instruments are null and void and cannot generate rights for their beneficiaries. Public officials who issue such acts face disciplinary, administrative, civil, and potentially criminal liability.

### Transitional and Final Provisions (Articles 99-106)

**Article 100 -- Municipal plan deadline.** Municipalities had five years from the law's entry into force to adopt Urban and Rural Development Plans. Pre-existing plans had to be adapted within two years.

**Article 104 -- Specialty.** The LODT is a special law and prevails over any conflicting provision.

**Article 106 -- Entry into force.** The law entered into force twelve months after its publication in the Diario Oficial.

## Who This Affects

- **Municipal governments (alcaldias):** Must adopt territorial plans, issue construction and subdivision permits, enforce zoning, and participate in departmental and micro-regional planning councils. Failure to adopt plans within the statutory deadline triggers substitute planning by the competent authority.
- **Property owners and real estate developers:** Must obtain planning-based approvals before any construction, subdivision, or change of land use. Owners in non-urbanizable zones face use restrictions. Developers of urbanization projects must provide land for public infrastructure and pay applicable contributions.
- **Construction professionals (architects, engineers, contractors):** Bear direct legal responsibility for projects that violate planning instruments. Technical directors of unauthorized works face personal fines and potential demolition orders.
- **National government ministries:** The Ministries of Public Works, Environment, Agriculture, Economy, and Education all participate in the National and Departmental Councils, coordinating sector-specific policies with territorial plans.
- **Business and academic sectors:** Each have two representatives on the National Council and Departmental Councils, providing input on territorial policy and planning decisions.
- **Citizens and community organizations:** The law mandates at least two public consultations before any territorial plan is approved (one for proposals at least 150 business days before approval, and one for comments on the final draft at least 25 business days before approval).
- **Financial institutions and investors:** Urban development and construction projects require verified plan compliance before authorization; illegal projects may be demolished and produce no transferable rights.

## Related Laws

- **Constitution of El Salvador (1983)** -- Articles on social function of property, environmental protection, economic freedom, and inter-governmental collaboration, which the LODT implements.
- **Ley de Urbanismo y Construccion (Urbanism and Construction Law)** -- Establishes technical standards for construction and subdivision that the LODT references, particularly regarding minimum public-space requirements in urbanization projects.
- **Ley de Desarrollo y Ordenamiento Territorial del Area Metropolitana de San Salvador y de los Municipios Aledanos (AMSS Territorial Law)** -- The special regime for the San Salvador metropolitan area continues to apply under Art. 101, coexisting with the LODT.
- **Ley del Medio Ambiente (Environmental Law, Decreto 233 of 1998)** -- Environmental impact assessment and protected-area designations interact directly with LODT zoning and non-urbanizable zone restrictions.
- **Codigo Municipal (Municipal Code)** -- Governs municipal procedure for sanctions (Art. 90), public consultations (Title IX), and municipal ordinance authority used to give legal force to local plans.
- **Ley de Areas Naturales Protegidas (Protected Natural Areas Law)** -- Areas designated under this law are classified as non-urbanizable under the LODT and receive heightened infraction protections.
- **Ley de Expropiacion (Expropriation Law)** -- Governs the procedure for acquiring private land designated for public infrastructure and facilities in territorial plans (Art. 67).

## Common Questions

### 1. What is the difference between a "plan departamental" and a "plan municipal"?

A departmental plan covers an entire department (analogous to a province or state), setting broad territorial strategies, infrastructure networks, environmental protections, and urban-system guidelines. A municipal plan covers a single municipality (or micro-region of associated municipalities) and provides the detailed, lot-level zoning, building heights, density limits, and specific land use classifications that directly govern construction permits. Municipal plans must be consistent with the departmental plan above them in the hierarchy.

### 2. Can I build on rural or non-urbanizable land?

On rural land, you may build in accordance with what the applicable Municipal Territorial Plan or Partial Plan allows, after obtaining project approval and paying required taxes. On non-urbanizable land, development is heavily restricted: only economic uses compatible with the environmental or protective values that justified the zone's designation are permitted. Unauthorized construction on non-urbanizable or protected land constitutes a very serious infraction (Art. 75).

### 3. What happens if a municipality has not adopted a plan?

Until a municipality approves its Urban and Rural Development Plan or Urban Development Scheme, the competent authority at the departmental or national level may authorize subdivisions and urbanizations directly (Art. 101), subject to environmental and risk restrictions. Construction permits in municipalities without plans may only be issued for consolidated urban zones and may not involve new subdivisions.

### 4. What are the penalties for building without a permit?

Unauthorized construction is a serious infraction (Art. 76) punishable by fines of up to 200 monthly minimum wages for commerce. If the unauthorized construction is on non-urbanizable or protected land, it becomes a very serious infraction with fines of up to 1,000 monthly minimum wages. In addition, the authority may order demolition at the infractor's expense (Art. 82) and require the offender to restore the site to its previous state. Fines are increased if the violation produced economic benefit exceeding the fine amount (Art. 86).

### 5. How does the public consultation process work before a plan is approved?

At least two public consultations are required (Art. 57). The first consultation, held at least 150 business days before plan approval, is for receiving proposals from citizens and organizations. The second consultation, held at least 25 business days before approval, is for receiving observations on the final draft. All citizen and business organizations related to territorial matters in the relevant geographic scope must be invited.

### 6. Who is liable when construction violates the law -- the developer, the engineer, or the official who issued the permit?

All of them can be held liable simultaneously (Art. 77). Project engineers are responsible if the project design is incompatible with planning instruments. Developers, builders, financiers, and site directors are responsible for execution. Public officials who granted permits without required reports, or against unfavorable reports, are personally responsible, as are members of collegiate bodies who voted in favor. Fines imposed on different responsible parties for the same infraction are independent of each other (Art. 84).

### 7. Does this law apply in the San Salvador metropolitan area?

Partially. Article 101 states that in the municipalities within the San Salvador Metropolitan Area (AMSS) and neighboring municipalities, the competence regime established by the separate AMSS Territorial Law continues to apply. However, the LODT's general principles, planning hierarchy, and sanctions framework still operate as the overarching national framework.

### 8. Can property owners be compensated if a territorial plan restricts their land use?

Yes. Article 61 establishes that any land use limitation preventing the continuation of lawful economic activities that were being conducted prior to the plan's adoption gives rise to compensation, provided the owner demonstrates actual economic harm. Article 70(7) confirms that owners of land in non-urbanizable zones who lose the right to continue lawful pre-existing uses are entitled to compensation. However, no compensation is owed for the prohibition of uses that were not already being lawfully exercised when the plan was approved.
