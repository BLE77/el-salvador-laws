# Daily Life and Consumer Rights in El Salvador

## Summary

This page covers the legal rules that affect everyday life in El Salvador -- from returning a defective product and dealing with an internet provider, to the drinking age, driving, carrying weapons, and what to do if you get scammed. The primary consumer protection framework comes from the Consumer Protection Law (Decreto 776/2005), enforced by the Defensoria del Consumidor. Telecommunications are regulated by SIGET under Decreto 142/1997. Firearms are controlled under the Ley de Control y Regulacion de Armas de Fuego, Municiones, Explosivos y Articulos Similares. Traffic rules fall under the Ley de Transporte Terrestre, Transito y Seguridad Vial.

## Consumer Protection (Decreto 776/2005)

### Your Basic Rights as a Consumer

Under Art. 5 of D-776/2005, every consumer in El Salvador has the right to:

- **Accurate information.** Complete, truthful, and timely information about any product or service before and after purchase.
- **Safe products.** Protection from goods and services that pose a danger to health or safety.
- **Fair pricing.** Protection from price gouging on essential goods and services.
- **Compensation for defects.** The right to a remedy (replacement, repair, or refund) when a product is defective or a service is deficient.
- **No deceptive advertising.** Protection from false, misleading, or manipulative advertising (Art. 31).
- **Free choice.** The right to choose freely among available products without being forced to buy bundled items (Art. 18).

### Refunds, Repairs, and Warranties

**Defective products.** Providers are strictly liable for damages caused by defects in their products, particularly food, beverages, pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, appliances, and transportation services (Art. 38, D-776/2005). If a product is defective, the consumer is entitled to:

- **Replacement** with an identical or equivalent product
- **Repair** at no cost to the consumer
- **Refund** of the purchase price

The consumer can choose among these remedies. The provider cannot force you to accept only a repair if you prefer a refund or replacement.

**Warranties.** Providers must honor any warranty (garantia) offered on a product, whether written or implied. The warranty period and conditions must be clearly communicated at the time of sale. If the product fails within the warranty period, the provider must remedy the defect at no additional cost.

**Installment purchases.** Sellers offering installment plans (venta a plazos) must clearly disclose the cash price, total financing cost, interest rate, payment schedule, and all fees before the consumer commits (Art. 14).

### Price Gouging and Essential Goods

The Consumer Protection Law empowers the Defensoria del Consumidor to intervene when providers engage in unjustified price increases on essential goods and services. During emergencies, the government can impose temporary price controls.

### Online Purchases (E-Commerce)

Online sellers must present a clear order summary before the consumer confirms the purchase, including individual item prices, shipping costs, and the total amount. The consumer has the right to cancel the order before it is confirmed (Art. 21-B). E-commerce providers are subject to the same consumer protection obligations as physical stores.

### How to File a Consumer Complaint

1. **Try to resolve it directly** with the provider first. Keep receipts, invoices, and any written communication.
2. **File a complaint with the Defensoria del Consumidor.** You can file in person at their offices, by phone, or online. Provide your identification, a description of the problem, the provider's name, and supporting documents.
3. **Mediation.** The Defensoria will attempt mediation between you and the provider.
4. **Sanctions Tribunal.** If mediation fails, the case can be escalated to the Tribunal Sancionador, which can impose fines on the provider of up to 200 monthly minimum wages for serious violations (Art. 51).
5. **Consumer arbitration.** Both parties can agree to binding arbitration through the Defensoria's arbitration system (Art. 125). The provider has 5 business days to accept or reject arbitration; silence counts as refusal.

## Drinking Age

The legal drinking age in El Salvador is **18 years old**. It is illegal to sell alcoholic beverages to minors under 18. Establishments that sell alcohol to minors face administrative sanctions and potential criminal liability. The Health Code (Decreto 955/1988) and municipal ordinances regulate the sale and consumption of alcohol, including restrictions on sale hours and proximity to schools.

## Driver's License

### Salvadoran Driver's License

Driver's licenses in El Salvador are issued by the Vice Ministerio de Transporte (VMT) under the Ley de Transporte Terrestre, Transito y Seguridad Vial.

**Requirements to obtain a license:**
- Be at least 18 years old
- Pass a medical examination (examen medico)
- Pass a written knowledge test on traffic laws
- Pass a practical driving test
- Present identification (DUI for Salvadorans, passport and residency card for foreigners)
- Pay the applicable fee

**License categories** include light vehicles (Class A), motorcycles (Class B), commercial vehicles, and heavy vehicles, each with specific requirements.

### Foreign Driver's Licenses

- **Tourists and short-term visitors.** A valid foreign driver's license is generally accepted for driving in El Salvador for a limited period (typically up to 90 days, aligning with the standard tourist visa). An International Driving Permit (IDP) is recommended as a supplement but not always strictly required.
- **Residents.** If you become a resident of El Salvador, you must obtain a Salvadoran driver's license. The process may allow you to convert your foreign license without taking the full practical test, depending on bilateral agreements and VMT policies at the time.
- **No driving without a license.** Operating a motor vehicle without a valid license is a traffic infraction punishable by fines and potential vehicle impoundment.

## Traffic Violations and Fines

Traffic infractions are classified by severity under the Ley de Transporte Terrestre, Transito y Seguridad Vial:

| Category | Examples | Typical Fine Range |
|---|---|---|
| **Minor (leves)** | Failure to wear a seatbelt, expired registration, illegal parking | Lower-tier fines |
| **Serious (graves)** | Running a red light, speeding, driving without a license, using a phone while driving | Mid-tier fines, possible license suspension |
| **Very serious (muy graves)** | Drunk driving (DUI), reckless driving causing danger, fleeing an accident | High fines, license suspension or revocation, potential criminal charges |

### Drunk Driving (DUI)

Driving under the influence of alcohol or drugs is a very serious infraction. The legal blood alcohol limit is **0.05 g/dL** (50 mg per 100 mL of blood). Penalties include:

- Substantial fines
- License suspension or revocation
- Vehicle impoundment
- Criminal charges if the DUI causes injury or death (prosecuted under the Penal Code, D-1030/1997)

### What to Do If You Get a Traffic Ticket

- The officer issues a written citation (boleta de infraccion).
- You have a period (typically 15 business days) to pay the fine or contest it before the VMT.
- Unpaid fines accumulate and can prevent you from renewing your license or vehicle registration.
- You have the right to appeal an infraction administratively.

## Gun Laws (Ley de Control y Regulacion de Armas)

### Firearm Permits

Gun ownership and carrying in El Salvador are regulated by the Ley de Control y Regulacion de Armas de Fuego, Municiones, Explosivos y Articulos Similares, administered by the Ministerio de la Defensa Nacional (Ministry of National Defense).

**Two main permits:**

| Permit | Description |
|---|---|
| **Tenencia (possession permit)** | Authorizes you to keep a firearm at your home or business. Does not allow carrying it in public. |
| **Portacion (carry permit)** | Authorizes you to carry a firearm in public, concealed or in a holster. More stringent requirements. |

### Requirements for a Firearm Permit

- Be at least 21 years old (for portacion; 18 for tenencia in some categories)
- Salvadoran citizen or legal resident
- No criminal record
- Pass a psychological evaluation
- Pass a firearms safety and handling course
- Present a medical certificate
- Not be a member of a gang or criminal organization
- Pay the applicable fees

### Restrictions

- **Prohibited weapons.** Military-grade weapons, fully automatic firearms, silencers, and explosives are prohibited for civilian ownership.
- **Prohibited places.** Even with a carry permit, firearms are prohibited in government buildings, schools, hospitals, bars, sporting events, and other designated locations.
- **Registration.** Every legally owned firearm must be registered with the Ministry of Defense. Unregistered firearms are illegal.
- **Foreigners.** Non-residents generally cannot obtain a firearm permit. Residents may apply under the same rules as citizens.

### Penalties for Violations

Illegal possession or carrying of firearms is a criminal offense under the Penal Code (D-1030/1997) and the Ley de Armas. Penalties include prison sentences of 3-6 years for illegal possession, with higher penalties if the weapon is military-grade or used in the commission of another crime.

## Knife and Weapon Carrying Rules

### Knives and Bladed Weapons

There is no comprehensive standalone law regulating knife carrying in El Salvador. However:

- **Ordinary knives.** Carrying a common pocket knife or utility knife for lawful purposes (work, camping, daily use) is generally not a criminal offense.
- **Weapons-grade blades.** Carrying machetes, large knives, or bladed weapons in urban areas without a legitimate purpose (such as agricultural work) can be treated as illegal weapon carrying under the Penal Code.
- **Context matters.** A machete carried by a farmer in a rural area is normal and legal. The same machete carried in a bar in San Salvador at night will attract police attention and potential charges.
- **White weapons (armas blancas).** The Penal Code and police regulations address "armas blancas" (edged weapons) used in the commission of crimes. Carrying a weapon with intent to harm is a criminal offense regardless of the type of weapon.

### Other Prohibited Items

Brass knuckles, nunchucks, switchblades, and similar weapons designed primarily for combat are treated similarly to prohibited firearms accessories and can lead to criminal charges.

## Telecommunications and Internet (Decreto 142/1997)

### Regulatory Authority: SIGET

The Superintendencia General de Electricidad y Telecomunicaciones (SIGET) regulates all telecommunications in El Salvador, including telephony, internet, broadband, and broadcasting (Art. 1, D-142/1997).

### Your Rights as a Telecom Consumer

Telecom services are subject to both the Telecommunications Law and the Consumer Protection Law:

- **Quality of service.** Operators must maintain service quality standards established by SIGET. Persistent outages, slow speeds, or degraded service below contracted levels are violations.
- **Transparent pricing.** Providers must clearly disclose all charges, fees, and contract terms before the consumer signs up.
- **No forced bundling.** Under consumer protection rules (Art. 18, D-776/2005), telecom providers cannot force you to buy services you do not want as a condition of getting the service you do want.
- **Contract cancellation.** Consumers have the right to cancel telecom contracts, though early termination fees may apply if specified in the contract. SIGET has issued regulations limiting excessive cancellation penalties.
- **Portability.** Mobile number portability allows you to switch carriers while keeping your phone number.

### How to Complain About Your Internet or Phone Provider

1. **Contact the provider's customer service** and document the complaint in writing.
2. **File a complaint with SIGET** if the provider does not resolve the issue. SIGET handles complaints about service quality, billing disputes, unauthorized charges, and contract violations.
3. **File with the Defensoria del Consumidor** for consumer rights violations (deceptive advertising, refusal to honor promotional terms, etc.).
4. **SIGET can impose sanctions** on operators that violate service quality standards or consumer rights, including fines and orders to correct the violation.

## What to Do If You Get Scammed

### Civil and Criminal Remedies

Getting scammed (estafa) in El Salvador can give rise to both civil and criminal actions:

**Criminal complaint (denuncia penal):**
- Fraud/swindle (estafa) is a crime under Art. 215 of the Penal Code (D-1030/1997), punishable by 2-5 years in prison.
- File a complaint (denuncia) at the Fiscalia General de la Republica (Attorney General's Office) or at the nearest police station (PNC).
- Provide all evidence: receipts, contracts, messages, screenshots, bank transfers, witness information.
- The Fiscalia will investigate and, if warranted, prosecute the case.

**Consumer complaint:**
- If the scam involves a business or provider, file a complaint with the Defensoria del Consumidor. The Defensoria can mediate, investigate, and escalate to its Sanctions Tribunal.
- The Defensoria can deploy undercover investigators (delegado encubierto) to verify fraudulent practices (Art. 102, D-776/2005).

**Civil lawsuit:**
- You can file a civil action for damages (accion de danos y perjuicios) against the scammer to recover your money plus compensation.
- This is independent of any criminal prosecution and can proceed simultaneously.

### Practical Tips

- **Preserve all evidence.** Screenshots, messages, emails, contracts, receipts, and bank records.
- **Act quickly.** Report scams as soon as possible. Delays make investigation harder and may allow the scammer to disappear.
- **Bank fraud.** If money was transferred electronically, contact your bank immediately to attempt to freeze or reverse the transaction. Banks have limited windows for chargebacks.
- **Online scams.** Report to the PNC's Division de Investigacion de Delitos Informaticos (cybercrime unit) for internet-based fraud.

## Common Questions

### 1. Can I get a refund for a defective product?

Yes. Under Art. 38 of D-776/2005, providers are strictly liable for defective products. You are entitled to a replacement, repair, or refund -- your choice, not the provider's. If the provider refuses, file a complaint with the Defensoria del Consumidor. Keep your receipt and any proof of the defect.

### 2. What is the legal drinking age?

18 years old. Selling alcohol to anyone under 18 is illegal. Establishments that do so face administrative sanctions, and responsible individuals may face criminal liability for contributing to the corruption of a minor.

### 3. Can I drive with my foreign license in El Salvador?

Yes, for a limited period (generally up to 90 days while on a tourist visa). An International Driving Permit is recommended as a supplement. If you become a resident, you must obtain a Salvadoran driver's license from the VMT.

### 4. What happens if I get caught driving drunk?

Drunk driving is a very serious traffic infraction. You face heavy fines, license suspension or revocation, and vehicle impoundment. The legal blood alcohol limit is 0.05 g/dL. If the DUI causes injury or death, it becomes a criminal offense prosecuted under the Penal Code with potential prison time.

### 5. Can I legally own a gun in El Salvador?

Yes, with the proper permits. A tenencia permit allows you to keep a firearm at home. A portacion permit allows you to carry it in public. You must be at least 21 (for carry), have no criminal record, pass psychological and medical evaluations, complete a firearms safety course, and register the weapon with the Ministry of Defense. Military-grade weapons are prohibited for civilians.

### 6. Is it legal to carry a knife?

Carrying a common pocket knife or utility knife for legitimate purposes is generally not a crime. However, carrying large blades, machetes in urban areas without a work-related reason, or any weapon with intent to harm is illegal. Context and intent matter -- a machete on a farm is fine; the same machete in a nightclub is not.

### 7. What can I do if my internet provider gives me terrible service?

First, complain to the provider in writing and document everything. If they do not resolve the issue, file a complaint with SIGET (the telecom regulator) for service quality violations, or with the Defensoria del Consumidor for consumer rights violations such as deceptive advertising or refusal to honor contract terms. SIGET can fine operators and order them to fix service problems.

### 8. I got scammed. What are my options?

File a criminal complaint (denuncia) at the Fiscalia General de la Republica or the PNC for fraud (estafa, Art. 215, Penal Code -- 2-5 years prison). Simultaneously, file a consumer complaint with the Defensoria del Consumidor if a business was involved. You can also sue civilly for damages. Act fast, preserve all evidence (messages, receipts, transfers), and contact your bank immediately if money was sent electronically.

### 9. Can a business force me to buy something I do not want to get what I actually need?

No. Tying the sale of one product to the purchase of another (unless they are naturally complementary) is a prohibited practice under Art. 18 of D-776/2005. This applies to telecom providers, retailers, and any other business. If this happens to you, report it to the Defensoria del Consumidor.

### 10. Are online purchases protected by consumer law?

Yes. E-commerce transactions are fully covered by D-776/2005. Online sellers must show you a clear order summary with all costs before you confirm the purchase, and you have the right to cancel before confirmation (Art. 21-B). If you receive a defective product from an online purchase, you have the same rights to replacement, repair, or refund as with any in-store purchase.
