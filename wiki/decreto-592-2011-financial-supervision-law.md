# Decreto 592 (2011) — Financial System Supervision Law

## Summary

The Financial System Supervision and Regulation Law (Ley de Supervision y Regulacion del Sistema Financiero) was enacted on January 14, 2011, and entered into force 180 days after its publication in the Diario Oficial (No. 23, Tomo 390, published February 2, 2011). It consolidated three previously separate supervisory bodies -- the Superintendency of the Financial System, the Superintendency of Securities, and the Superintendency of Pensions -- into a single unified Superintendency of the Financial System (Superintendencia del Sistema Financiero, SSF). The law also assigns a macro-prudential regulatory role to the Central Reserve Bank of El Salvador (Banco Central de Reserva, BCR).

The law was motivated by the 2008 global financial crisis, which underscored the need for effective consolidated supervision, and by the reality that Salvadoran financial conglomerates operated across banking, securities, insurance, and pension sectors simultaneously. Its stated purpose is to preserve the stability of the financial system and ensure its efficiency and transparency, in line with international best practices.

The law has 126 articles organized into six titles covering supervision structure, regulatory powers, sanctions, intervention procedures, technical regulation, and transitional provisions. It repealed the organic laws of the three predecessor superintendencies.

## Key Provisions

**Article 1 -- Composition of the Supervision System.** The Financial Supervision and Regulation System is composed of the Superintendency (SSF) and the Central Reserve Bank (BCR). The SSF handles day-to-day supervision; the BCR approves the macro-prudential regulatory framework. The SSF is created as a public-law institution integrated into the BCR, with its own legal personality, assets, and administrative and budgetary autonomy.

**Article 2 -- Object of the System.** The system's purpose is to preserve financial system stability, ensure its efficiency and transparency, and protect the soundness of financial institutions, in accordance with applicable laws, regulations, technical standards, and international best practices.

**Article 3 -- Superintendency Competencies.** The SSF supervises individual and consolidated activities of all financial system members. Its duties include enforcing laws and regulations; authorizing the formation, operation, merger, and closure of financial entities; preventive risk monitoring for solvency and liquidity; cooperating with consumer-protection and deposit-guarantee agencies; and imposing sanctions.

**Article 4 -- Superintendency Powers.** The SSF may issue resolutions binding on supervised entities; define supervisory policies; conduct individual and consolidated supervision; authorize the establishment, modification, and revocation of financial entities; cooperate and exchange information with foreign supervisory bodies; and publish information on financial system members.

**Article 5 -- Securities Market Powers.** With respect to the securities market, the SSF may authorize, suspend, or cancel public offerings; manage the Public Stock Exchange Registry (Registro Publico Bursatil); order suspension of securities quotations during disorderly market conditions; and promote effective self-regulation.

**Article 6 -- Pension System Powers.** The SSF supervises the Pension Savings System and the Public Pension System, including ISSS, INPEP, IPSFA, and private pension fund administrators (AFPs). It oversees pension fund investments, supervises benefits processes, and reviews pension-related insurance contracts.

**Article 7 -- Supervised Entities.** The law enumerates all entities subject to SSF supervision: the BCR (in limited respects), commercial banks and their subsidiaries, financial conglomerates, pension fund administrators, insurance companies, stock exchanges, brokerage firms, securities depositories, risk-rating agencies, cooperative banks, savings-and-loan societies, reciprocal guarantee companies, payment system operators, the Social Housing Fund, INPEP, ISSS (pensions and occupational risks), IPSFA, state development banks (BFA, Banco Hipotecario, BMI), CORSAIN, foreign-exchange houses, securitization companies, the Deposit Guarantee Institute, money transfer operators, and others designated by law.

**Article 9 -- Board of Directors (Consejo Directivo).** The Board is the highest authority of the SSF. It is composed of the Superintendent (who chairs it), the Deputy Superintendents, and four directors nominated respectively by the Ministry of Economy, the Ministry of Finance, the College of Economic Sciences Professionals, and the Auditing Profession Oversight Council. Members serve five-year terms and may be re-elected once.

**Article 18 -- Superintendent.** The Superintendent is appointed by the President of the Republic for a five-year term (re-electable once). The Superintendent directs the SSF, executes Board resolutions, holds legal representation, and serves full-time.

**Article 22 -- Deputy Superintendents.** At least four Deputy Superintendents are appointed, each heading a specialized unit: banks and insurers; state financial institutions; securities; and pensions. They are appointed by the President from shortlists proposed by the Council of Ministers.

**Article 30 -- Coordination with the Central Bank.** The SSF and BCR must establish coordination mechanisms through formal cooperation and information-exchange agreements to jointly preserve financial stability.

**Article 33 -- Confidentiality.** Information gathered by the SSF is confidential and may only be shared with the BCR, the Financial System Appeals Committee, the Deposit Guarantee Institute, the Attorney General, the courts, and foreign supervisory bodies under reciprocal information-sharing agreements.

**Article 35 -- Obligations of Supervised Entities.** Directors, managers, and officers of financial institutions must conduct business with the highest ethical standards, adopt risk-management and corporate-governance policies, maintain adequate internal controls, ensure accurate financial disclosure, and report material events to the SSF.

**Article 42 -- Preventive Measures.** When a financial institution shows difficulty meeting technical ratios, deficient risk management, or practices that could weaken its financial condition, the Board may require immediate corrective action, including restricting new lending, requiring asset sales, suspending dividends, demanding capital increases, or other measures. These resolutions exhaust the administrative remedy.

**Article 43 -- Sanctions.** The SSF may impose written warnings, fines, disqualification, suspension, registry cancellation, or revocation of authorization. The sanctioning procedure established in the law applies even when the underlying infraction is defined in another financial law.

**Article 44 -- Fine Limits.** Fines for legal persons may reach up to 2% of equity; for natural persons, up to 500 urban minimum wages (commerce sector). Article 46 raises the cap to 4% of equity (or 1,000 minimum wages for individuals) for falsification of financial statements or misuse of privileged information.

**Article 54 -- Sanctioning Procedure.** The administrative procedure includes: initiation resolution, service of process, response period, evidentiary phase (10 business days), and final resolution. Rights of hearing, defense, presumption of innocence, legality, proportionality, and responsibility must be respected.

**Article 65 -- Financial System Appeals Committee.** A permanent Appeals Committee (Comite de Apelaciones) is created to hear appeals against sanctioning resolutions. It is composed of a President and four members appointed by the President of the Republic from shortlists submitted by the BCR, Supreme Court, Ministry of Finance, and Ministry of Economy. Its decisions, requiring at least three concurring votes, exhaust the administrative remedy.

**Article 73 -- Administrative Intervention.** The Board may decree intervention of a financial institution when its applicable law contemplates such a measure, when it falls under Commercial Code Article 187 grounds, or when its situation gravely endangers public interests. Intervention lasts up to one year, extendable by one additional year.

**Article 85 -- Budget.** Financial system members fund the SSF budget in proportion to services received. Banks and state entities pay up to 0.15% of total assets annually; AFPs pay up to 4% of net commission income; insurers pay up to 0.8% of net annual premiums; and securities-market participants pay up to 3% of annual income.

**Article 99 -- Technical Regulation.** The BCR, through its Standards Committee (Comite de Normas), approves technical standards on solvency, liquidity, provisioning, asset classification, corporate governance, financial disclosure, and other prudential matters. Draft standards must be published for industry consultation at least 15 days before taking effect. In emergencies, the Committee may issue temporary standards (up to 180 days) without prior consultation.

**Article 124 -- Special Prevalence.** Due to its special character, this law prevails over any other law that contradicts it. Any amendment or repeal must expressly reference this law.

## Who This Affects

- **Banks and financial institutions** operating in El Salvador (commercial banks, cooperative banks, savings-and-loan societies, state development banks), which must comply with SSF supervision, reporting, and prudential requirements.
- **Insurance companies** authorized to operate in El Salvador, subject to SSF oversight of reserves, investments, and solvency.
- **Pension fund administrators (AFPs)** and public pension entities (ISSS, INPEP, IPSFA), whose investments, benefit processes, and operations fall under SSF supervision.
- **Securities market participants**, including stock exchanges, brokerage firms, securities depositories, risk-rating agencies, securitization companies, and issuers of publicly offered securities.
- **Directors, officers, managers, and auditors** of all supervised entities, who face personal liability and potential disqualification for violations.
- **External auditors, appraisers, insurance intermediaries, stockbrokers, and pension service agents**, who must register with the SSF and comply with professional requirements.
- **Financial conglomerates** and their controlling and member companies, subject to consolidated supervision.
- **Money transfer operators** (national and international), brought under SSF supervision.
- **The Central Reserve Bank**, subject to SSF supervision of its banking operations, reserve management, and monetary species custody.
- **The general public** -- depositors, policyholders, pension contributors, and investors -- who are the ultimate beneficiaries of the financial stability and transparency the law seeks to ensure.

## Related Laws

- **Ley Organica del Banco Central de Reserva de El Salvador** -- Organic law of the Central Reserve Bank, which exercises macro-prudential regulatory authority under Decreto 592.
- **Ley de Bancos (Banking Law)** -- Governs commercial bank operations, licensing, and prudential requirements; sanctioning procedures now follow Decreto 592.
- **Ley del Mercado de Valores (Securities Market Law)** -- Regulates securities offerings, brokerage, and market conduct; SSF assumes former Superintendency of Securities powers.
- **Ley del Sistema de Ahorro para Pensiones (Pension Savings System Law)** -- Governs the private pension system (AFPs); SSF assumes former Superintendency of Pensions powers.
- **Ley de Sociedades de Seguros (Insurance Companies Law)** -- Regulates insurance company formation, reserves, and operations under SSF supervision.
- **Ley de Bancos Cooperativos y Sociedades de Ahorro y Credito** -- Governs cooperative banks and savings-and-loan societies supervised by the SSF.
- **Ley de Titularizacion de Activos (Asset Securitization Law)** -- Regulates securitization vehicles under SSF oversight.
- **Ley de Casas de Cambio de Moneda Extranjera** -- Governs foreign-exchange houses; supervisory powers transferred from BCR to SSF by this law.
- **Ley Contra el Lavado de Dinero y de Activos (Anti-Money Laundering Law)** -- The SSF Board is authorized to create an Anti-Money Laundering Unit.
- **Ley del Instituto de Garantia de Depositos** -- Governs the Deposit Guarantee Institute, a supervised entity that cooperates with the SSF.
- **Ley de Bolsas de Productos y Servicios** -- Governs commodity and services exchanges, modified by Decreto 592.
- **Codigo de Comercio (Commercial Code)** -- Serves as supplementary law for intervention, liquidation, and general corporate matters.

## Common Questions

**1. What did this law replace?**
It repealed the organic laws of three separate supervisory bodies: the Superintendency of the Financial System (Decreto 628 of 1990), the Superintendency of Securities (Decreto 806 of 1996), and the Superintendency of Pensions (Decreto 926 of 1996). All their powers, assets, obligations, and staff were transferred to the new unified SSF.

**2. How is the SSF different from the Central Reserve Bank?**
The SSF handles direct day-to-day supervision -- inspections, sanctions, licensing, and enforcement. The BCR is responsible for approving the macro-prudential regulatory framework (technical standards on solvency, provisioning, corporate governance, etc.) through its Standards Committee. The SSF is legally integrated into the BCR but operates with its own legal personality and administrative autonomy.

**3. Who pays for the SSF's operations?**
Supervised financial institutions fund the SSF budget proportionally. Banks and state financial entities contribute up to 0.15% of total assets; pension fund administrators up to 4% of net commission income; insurers up to 0.8% of net premiums; and securities-market entities up to 3% of annual income. The BCR collects these contributions and remits them to the Ministry of Finance.

**4. What sanctions can the SSF impose?**
The SSF may impose written warnings, monetary fines, disqualification from holding positions in the financial system, suspension, cancellation of registry entries, or revocation of operating authorization. Fines for institutions can reach 2% of equity (4% for falsification of financial information), and fines for individuals can reach 500 to 1,000 urban minimum wages depending on the violation. Criminal conduct must be reported to the Attorney General.

**5. How does the appeals process work?**
Sanctioning resolutions issued by the Superintendent may first be challenged through an optional rectification request (recurso de rectificacion) within four business days. The sanctioned party may then appeal to the permanent Financial System Appeals Committee (Comite de Apelaciones) within ten business days. The Appeals Committee's decision, requiring at least three concurring votes, is final and exhausts the administrative remedy. Judicial review may follow before the contentious-administrative courts.

**6. Can the SSF intervene a failing financial institution?**
Yes. The Board may decree administrative intervention when a financial institution meets grounds specified in its governing law, when it falls into situations described in Commercial Code Article 187 (e.g., losses exceeding capital), or when its condition gravely threatens public interests. An intervention lasts up to one year, extendable by one additional year. If recovery is not possible, the SSF requests the Attorney General to seek judicial dissolution and liquidation.

**7. What confidentiality protections apply?**
Information gathered by the SSF is confidential by law. It may only be shared with the BCR, the Appeals Committee, the Deposit Guarantee Institute, the Attorney General, the courts, and foreign supervisory counterparts under formal reciprocal agreements. SSF personnel who disclose confidential information face immediate dismissal and civil and criminal liability. The Superintendent must report monthly to the President of the Republic on material events in the financial system.

**8. What is the statute of limitations for sanctioning proceedings?**
The statute of limitations is ten years from the date the infraction was completed or the omission occurred. It is interrupted when any director or manager of the financial institution acknowledges the infraction, or by any investigative action by the SSF communicated in writing to the alleged offender. During the first year of the law's effectiveness, a transitional five-year period applied.
