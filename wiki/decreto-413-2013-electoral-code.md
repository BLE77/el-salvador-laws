# Decreto 413 (2013) -- Electoral Code

## Summary
The Electoral Code regulates all aspects of the electoral process in El Salvador, including voter registration, the organization and powers of the Supreme Electoral Tribunal (*Tribunal Supremo Electoral*, TSE), political party registration, candidate nomination, campaign rules, voting procedures, vote counting, and challenges to election results. It replaced the 1992 Electoral Code to consolidate the numerous reforms that had accumulated over two decades.

## Key Provisions
- **Article 1**: The code regulates the activities of the electorate (*cuerpo electoral*), the voter registry, electoral bodies, and all state activity related to the electoral process.
- **Article 3**: Voting is a right and civic duty of all citizens. The vote is free, direct, equal, and secret.
- **Article 7**: The following persons may not vote: those under a formal arrest warrant, the mentally incapacitated, those judicially declared incompetent, those who refuse to serve in an elected office, those of notoriously immoral conduct, those convicted of a crime, those who buy or sell votes, and those who sign fraudulent voter petitions.
- **Article 11**: Electoral territorial districts (*circunscripciones*) are municipal, departmental, and national. The national district is used for the election of President and Vice-President, as well as deputies to the Central American Parliament (*PARLACEN*).
- **Article 12**: Each department constitutes an independent district for electing its corresponding deputies to the Legislative Assembly.
- **Article 13**: The number of deputies per department is determined by population, with a total of 84 deputies in the Legislative Assembly.
- **Article 46**: The Supreme Electoral Tribunal (*TSE*) consists of five Magistrates who serve 5-year terms. Three are nominated by the three political parties or coalitions that received the most votes in the last presidential election, and two are elected by the Supreme Court of Justice.
- **Article 66**: The TSE President's powers include convening sessions, directing debates, managing administrative activities, and requesting the National Civil Police to maintain public order during electoral processes.
- **Article 85**: The electoral register (*registro electoral*) contains the list of all citizens entitled to vote, organized by municipality.
- **Article 114**: Employers must grant employees assigned to electoral duties paid leave for the duration of their training and service, including the day after the election.
- **Article 150**: Political parties and coalitions must register their candidates within the deadlines set by the TSE for each type of election.
- **Article 175**: Parties are prohibited from conducting campaign propaganda in languages other than Spanish, using religious images, or using national symbols for partisan purposes.
- **Article 179**: Electoral propaganda must not incite violence, offend public morals, or attack the dignity of candidates or institutions.
- **Article 181**: Parties need municipal authorization to hold rallies, demonstrations, and gatherings in public places for electoral propaganda.
- **Article 206**: Valid votes are those where the voter's intent is clearly determined by any mark on the party flag or candidate photo. In legislative elections, voters may vote for individual candidates by name (*voto cruzado*) or for an entire party list.
- **Article 220**: Once the final count (*escrutinio final*) is completed without challenge within the legal period, the election results become legally binding and the TSE issues a formal decree.
- **Article 271-273**: Election results may be challenged through a nullity petition (*nulidad de eleccion*) filed within 24 hours of the election, based on grounds such as fraud, voter intimidation, or procedural irregularities.

## Who This Affects
All Salvadoran citizens of voting age (18+), political parties, independent candidates (*candidaturas no partidarias*), employers (who must grant electoral leave), and public officials involved in the electoral process. It also affects the Supreme Electoral Tribunal and all subordinate electoral bodies.

## Common Questions
- **Q: Who runs elections in El Salvador?** -- A: The Supreme Electoral Tribunal (*Tribunal Supremo Electoral*, TSE), composed of five Magistrates serving 5-year terms (Art. 46).
- **Q: Can I vote for individual candidates, not just parties?** -- A: Yes. For legislative elections, voters may mark individual candidates by name on the ballot (*voto cruzado*), or vote for an entire party list by marking the party flag (Art. 206).
- **Q: Who cannot vote?** -- A: Persons under formal arrest, the mentally incapacitated, convicted criminals, vote buyers/sellers, and others listed in Article 7.
- **Q: Do employers have to give time off to vote or serve as poll workers?** -- A: Yes. Employers must grant paid leave for electoral duties including training, Election Day service, and the day after (Art. 114).
- **Q: How are election results challenged?** -- A: Through a nullity petition filed with the TSE within 24 hours of the election, specifying the grounds and evidence (Arts. 271-273).
- **Q: How many deputies are in the Legislative Assembly?** -- A: 84 deputies, distributed among departments based on population (Art. 13).
