# Foreign Property Ownership in El Salvador

The **Constitution of El Salvador** permits foreigners to own property, but imposes certain restrictions based on location and property type. These rules are designed to protect national sovereignty and border security.

## General Rules

- **Urban property** — foreigners face **no restrictions** on purchasing urban real estate. Apartments, houses, and commercial buildings in cities and towns can be freely acquired.
- **Rural property** — foreign ownership of rural land is **limited**. The Constitution restricts how much agricultural or rural land a foreigner may own, and some acquisitions may require government authorization.

## Border and Coastal Restrictions

Foreigners **cannot own property** within certain distances of national borders or the coastline without special authorization. Specifically:

- Properties near **land borders** with Guatemala and Honduras are restricted
- **Coastal properties** within a specified distance from the high-tide line face ownership limitations
- Special authorization from the government may be sought, and exceptions exist for tourism development projects

In practice, some foreigners use Salvadoran legal entities (corporations) to hold coastal or restricted property, though this approach carries legal risks.

## Registration Process

All property transactions must be registered at the **CNR (Centro Nacional de Registros)**. The process includes:

1. Obtain a **NIT (Numero de Identificacion Tributaria)** — the tax identification number required for all property transactions
2. Conduct **due diligence** — verify title, liens, and encumbrances at the CNR
3. Execute the **escritura publica** (public deed) before a Salvadoran notary
4. Pay applicable **transfer taxes**
5. **Register** the deed at the CNR

## Practical Advice

Foreigners should always work with a qualified Salvadoran attorney for property purchases. Title insurance is not common in El Salvador, making thorough due diligence at the CNR essential.
