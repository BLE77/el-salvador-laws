# Statute of Limitations (Prescripcion) in El Salvador

The statute of limitations (prescripcion) in El Salvador determines the time period within which legal action must be initiated. Both **civil** and **criminal** matters have specific limitation periods, governed respectively by the **Civil Code** and the **Penal Code (Decreto 1030 of 1997)**.

## Civil Prescription (Prescripcion Civil)

The Civil Code distinguishes between **acquisitive prescription** (prescripcion adquisitiva, for acquiring property rights through possession) and **extinctive prescription** (prescripcion extintiva, for the expiration of the right to bring a claim).

### Common Limitation Periods

- **General civil claims**: **10 years** for ordinary personal actions where no shorter period is specified.
- **Contractual obligations**: Typically **5 to 10 years**, depending on the type of contract.
- **Tort claims** (responsabilidad extracontractual): Generally **3 years** from when the injured party knew or should have known of the damage.
- **Commercial obligations**: Under the **Commercial Code (Decreto 671)**, commercial claims generally prescribe in shorter periods, often **3 to 5 years**.
- **Labor claims**: Under the **Labor Code (Decreto 15)**, workers generally have **60 days to 1 year** to bring employment claims, depending on the type (e.g., unjust dismissal claims have specific short deadlines).
- **Tax claims**: The **Tax Code (Decreto 230)** sets specific limitation periods for the government to assess and collect taxes, typically **3 to 5 years**, or **10 years** in cases of fraud.

### Tolling and Interruption

The limitation period can be **interrupted** (interrumpida) by:
- Filing a lawsuit
- Acknowledgment of the debt by the debtor
- Any act constituting a formal demand

Once interrupted, the period restarts from zero.

## Criminal Prescription (Prescripcion Penal)

Under the **Penal Code (Decreto 1030)** and the **Criminal Procedure Code (Decreto 733)**, criminal action prescribes based on the severity of the offense:

- **Serious crimes (delitos graves)** with penalties over 10 years: Prescription period equal to the **maximum penalty**, but not exceeding **15 years**.
- **Less serious crimes (delitos menos graves)**: Generally **3 to 5 years**.
- **Misdemeanors (faltas)**: Typically **1 year**.
- **Sexual offenses against minors**: Extended prescription periods apply; the clock may not begin running until the victim reaches the age of majority (18).
- **Crimes against humanity and genocide**: **No prescription** (imprescriptibles).

### When the Clock Starts

- For criminal matters, the limitation period generally begins on the **date the crime was committed** or, for continuing offenses, when the criminal conduct ceases.
- For civil matters, the period begins when the **right of action arises** or when the party becomes aware of the facts giving rise to the claim.

## Practical Notes

Parties should be aware that courts strictly enforce limitation periods, and claims filed after expiration will be dismissed. When in doubt about applicable deadlines, seeking legal counsel promptly is essential, as some periods (particularly in labor and tax law) are notably short.
