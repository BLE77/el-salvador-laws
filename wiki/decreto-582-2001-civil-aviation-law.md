# Decreto 582 (2001) — Civil Aviation Law

## Summary

The Civil Aviation Law (Ley de Aviacion Civil), enacted by Legislative Decree No. 582 on October 18, 2001 and published in Official Gazette No. 198, Volume 353 on October 19, 2001, is the principal statute governing civil aviation in El Salvador. It regulates the exploitation and use of Salvadoran airspace, the provision and development of air transport services, and the construction, rehabilitation, administration, operation, and maintenance of civil aerodromes and heliports.

The law creates the Civil Aviation Authority (Autoridad de Aviacion Civil, or AAC) as an autonomous public-service institution with its own legal personality and assets, possessing administrative, technical, and financial autonomy. The AAC functions as the sole regulator of all aspects of civil aviation, both technical and economic.

The law spans 222 articles organized across topics including the AAC's governance structure, aircraft registration, air traffic control, aerodrome operations, air transport services (public, private, and aerial work), operating permits and certificates, passenger and cargo transport contracts, aircraft leasing and mortgages, liability and insurance, accident investigation, security offenses, and an administrative sanctions regime. It adopts an "open skies" policy framework based on effective reciprocity and aligns El Salvador's aviation regime with the standards of the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO).

The law has been amended multiple times, notably by Legislative Decrees No. 528/2004 and others, which introduced refinements to the AAC's governance, the Salvadoran Civil Aviation Registry (RAS), licensing of aeronautical personnel, infractions and sanctions, and unmanned aerial vehicles.

## Key Provisions

**Article 1 — Scope.** The law governs civil aviation throughout national territory and regulates the exploitation and use of Salvadoran airspace for air transport services, as well as the construction, rehabilitation, administration, operation, and maintenance of civil aerodromes and heliports. Salvadoran airspace is subject to national jurisdiction and sovereignty under Article 84 of the Constitution.

**Article 2 — Jurisdiction and competence.** All civil aircraft in Salvadoran territory or overflying it, along with their crews, are subject to Salvadoran jurisdiction for purposes of inspection, oversight, and control of air navigation. Acts and crimes committed aboard Salvadoran-registered aircraft fall under Salvadoran law.

**Article 3 — Definitions.** The law establishes key definitions including "civil aeronautics," "aircraft," "Civil Aviation Authority," "certificate of airworthiness," "certificate of registration," "certificate of operation," and "open skies" (a state policy aimed at liberalizing air transport markets based on effective reciprocity as established in bilateral or multilateral agreements).

**Article 4 — Creation of the AAC.** The Civil Aviation Authority is established as an autonomous public-service institution without profit motive. Its autonomy covers administrative, technical, and financial matters, and it possesses its own legal personality and assets.

**Article 6 — Functions of the AAC.** The AAC regulates, oversees, and controls all civil aviation activities, including the authorization, modification, cancellation, and revocation of operator certificates and operating permits for air transport operators, civil aerodromes and heliports, air traffic services, aeronautical communications, meteorological services, aeronautical information services, and air navigation facilities.

**Article 8 — CDAC governance.** The AAC is governed by the Civil Aviation Directive Council (Consejo Directivo de Aviacion Civil, CDAC), composed of: a President appointed by the President of the Republic, a director appointed by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, and two private-sector directors nominated by the private sector and appointed by the President of the Republic. Decisions are taken by simple majority, with the President holding a casting vote.

**Article 13 — Executive Director.** The Executive Director administers the institution under CDAC policies, represents the AAC before ICAO and COCESNA, sets safety and operational standards, establishes air routes and restricted zones, and grants operating permits.

**Article 20 — AAC revenues.** The AAC's income comes from 10% of boarding fees, aircraft parking, and landing charges; income from its own activities; financial interest income; training and advisory proceeds; and extraordinary state allocations and international donations.

**Article 22 — Tariff freedom.** Fares and freight rates for commercial air transport (passenger, cargo, and mail, national and international) are set freely by air transport operators according to market conditions. Operators must disclose tariffs and service conditions to users at the time of contracting. For reasons of national interest, the AAC may temporarily set minimum and maximum tariffs, subject to binding opinion from the Competition Superintendency.

**Article 26 — Aircraft classification.** Salvadoran aircraft are classified as civil aircraft (public service or private) and state aircraft (government-use or military). Public service aircraft are those accessible to the public via payment of a fare; private aircraft are used for non-commercial purposes.

**Article 27 — Nationality and registration.** Civil aircraft hold the nationality of the state where they are registered. Inscription in El Salvador confers Salvadoran nationality. No aircraft may hold dual registration.

**Article 30-A — Salvadoran Civil Aviation Registry (RAS).** The RAS is an organic unit of the AAC where property instruments for Salvadoran-registered aircraft and their engines, aircraft utilization contracts, mortgages and other real rights, loss and abandonment declarations, registration certificates, airworthiness certificates, aeronautical personnel licenses, and operator certificates and permits must be registered.

**Articles 48-51 — Aerodromes.** A civil aerodrome is any defined area of land or water, including buildings, installations, and equipment, used for aircraft arrival, departure, and surface movement. Aerodromes are classified as public use or private use. No person may operate a civil aerodrome for public air transport without AAC authorization.

**Articles 52-59 — Air transport services.** Air transport services are classified as public air transport (national/cabotage, international, regional; regular or non-regular; passenger, cargo, mail, or mixed), private air services, and aerial work services. Public air transport requires an operating permit and payment of a fare.

**Article 68 — Air operator certificates.** Air Operator Certificates are valid for five years from issuance, renewable for equal periods subject to compliance with laws, regulations, and international treaties. Holders are subject to periodic AAC supervision of operational safety and technical capacity.

**Article 83 — Aerodrome permits.** The AAC may grant operating permits for up to 50 years for civil airports, up to 10 years for civil heliports, and up to 3 years for landing strips, all renewable.

**Article 89 — Open skies policy.** El Salvador adopts an open skies policy for international air transport based on the principle of real and effective reciprocity, to be implemented through bilateral and multilateral agreements.

**Article 91 — Traffic rights.** Traffic rights are state property. The law enumerates the nine freedoms of the air (from first freedom overflight rights through ninth freedom cabotage), which form the basis of aerocommercial rights exchange between El Salvador and other states.

**Articles 103-A through 109 — Accident investigation.** The AAC, through its designated investigator, is responsible for investigating air accidents and incidents in Salvadoran territory. International norms and methods recommended by ICAO are followed. Operators must report any accident or incident within 24 hours.

**Articles 123-133 — Transport contracts.** The law regulates passenger transport contracts (requiring tickets with specified contents), cargo transport contracts (governed by air waybills), and establishes carrier liability regimes for each.

**Articles 150-156 — Aircraft mortgages.** Aircraft and their engines may be mortgaged. For purchase-money mortgages, parties may agree to irrevocable powers of attorney allowing the creditor to take possession, cancel registration, physically deport the aircraft to its country of origin, and sell or lease the aircraft extrajudicially.

**Articles 165-176 — Liability.** The carrier is liable for delays or route deviations unless caused by force majeure. The law establishes time limits for claims: 3 days for baggage damage, 7 days for cargo damage, and 21 days for delay. The general statute of limitations for transport-related actions is 2 years.

**Articles 178-184 — Insurance.** All operators of national or foreign aircraft overflying Salvadoran territory must carry liability insurance for third-party surface damage. Air transport operators must maintain insurance covering liability to passengers, baggage, cargo, and third parties, in amounts not less than those established in applicable international conventions.

**Articles 191-199 — Sanctions.** Infractions are classified as minor, serious, and very serious. Sanctions include warnings, fines, temporary suspension and disqualification, cancellation, and destruction of unauthorized installations. Fines for minor infractions range from 1 to 30 minimum wages; serious infractions up to 190-250 minimum wages. A 3% monthly penalty applies for late payment of fines. Repeat offenders face a 25% surcharge.

## Who This Affects

- **Airlines and air transport operators** (national and foreign) providing scheduled or non-scheduled passenger, cargo, or mail services in, to, or from El Salvador, who must obtain Air Operator Certificates and Operating Permits from the AAC
- **Airport and aerodrome operators** who construct, rehabilitate, administer, or operate civil aerodromes and heliports under AAC-issued permits lasting up to 50 years
- **The Civil Aviation Authority (AAC)** and its Directive Council (CDAC), as the autonomous regulatory body with administrative, technical, and financial authority over all civil aviation matters
- **Aeronautical personnel** including pilots, co-pilots, flight crews, and ground technical staff who must hold AAC-issued licenses valid for up to five years
- **CEPA (Comision Ejecutiva Portuaria Autonoma)** which provides air navigation support services including air traffic control, aeronautical information, meteorology, and communications under AAC tariff regulation
- **Aeronautical training schools** (public and private) that must obtain a Certificate of Operation to train aeronautical technical personnel
- **Aircraft owners and lessors/lessees** involved in registration, mortgage, leasing, chartering, or transfer of aircraft with Salvadoran registration
- **Passengers and cargo shippers** who benefit from tariff transparency requirements and carrier liability protections for delays, damage, and loss
- **Insurance companies** providing mandatory aviation liability coverage for passengers, cargo, baggage, and third-party surface damage
- **Aerial work operators** performing commercial non-transport activities such as agricultural aviation, aerial photography, surveying, advertising, and firefighting
- **The general public** affected by airspace sovereignty, flight safety, airport security regulations, and prohibitions on carrying weapons or explosive materials aboard aircraft

## Related Laws

- **Constitution of El Salvador (Article 84)** — establishes national sovereignty over airspace
- **Legislative Decree No. 528 of 2004** — major amendment to Decreto 582 modifying AAC governance, open skies policy, tariff regulation, and various operational provisions (multiple articles repealed or reformed)
- **Penal Code (Decreto 1030 of 1997)** — referenced for criminal penalties related to aviation security offenses such as carrying weapons aboard aircraft, interference with aircraft operations, and hijacking
- **Law on Administrative Procedures (Ley de Procedimientos Administrativos)** — applies supplementally for imposing sanctions and administrative appeals under Article 191-A
- **Competition Law (Ley de Competencia)** — the Competition Superintendency provides binding opinions on anticompetitive practices in air transport and on temporary tariff regulation
- **Chicago Convention (Convention on International Civil Aviation, 1944)** — the foundational international treaty governing civil aviation, referenced throughout regarding ICAO standards and recommended practices
- **Warsaw Convention / Montreal Convention** — international frameworks for airline liability for passenger injury, baggage loss, cargo damage, and delay, referenced in the liability and insurance chapters
- **Bilateral and multilateral air service agreements** — implement the open skies policy under the reciprocity principle established in Article 89
- **COCESNA Treaty** — governs the Central American Air Navigation Services Corporation through which El Salvador coordinates regional air navigation

## Common Questions

**What is the AAC and what does it do?**
The Autoridad de Aviacion Civil (AAC) is an autonomous public institution created by this law to regulate all aspects of civil aviation in El Salvador, both technical and economic. It issues operating permits and certificates, sets safety standards, regulates airport tariffs for monopolistic services, investigates accidents, enforces aviation law, and represents El Salvador before ICAO and COCESNA. It is governed by the CDAC (a four-member directive council) and administered by an Executive Director.

**What permits or certificates does an airline need to operate in El Salvador?**
An airline needs two main authorizations: an Air Operator Certificate (Certificado de Operador Aereo), valid for five years and renewable, which certifies operational safety and technical capacity (Article 68); and an Operating Permit (Permiso de Operacion), which authorizes specific air transport services (Article 71). Foreign airlines must also hold valid certificates from their home state and comply with bilateral or multilateral agreements. Airlines must begin operations within 90 days of permit issuance or face cancellation (Article 75), and must post a bond to guarantee passenger obligations (Article 76).

**How are airfares regulated?**
Airfares for commercial air transport (passenger, cargo, and mail) are generally set freely by operators according to market conditions (Article 22). However, for reasons of national interest or public necessity, the AAC may temporarily impose minimum and maximum tariffs, subject to a binding opinion from the Competition Superintendency. Airport service fees for monopolistic services (landing, aircraft parking, boarding fees, cargo fees, fuel facility easements, and approach services) are subject to AAC tariff regulation using mathematical models with maximum rates reviewed every 3 to 5 years.

**What is the "open skies" policy?**
Open skies (cielos abiertos) is a state policy aimed at liberalizing air transport markets by reducing government intervention in passenger, cargo, and combined services for both scheduled and non-scheduled flights (Article 3 definition and Article 89). It operates on the principle of real and effective reciprocity, meaning El Salvador opens its skies to foreign carriers to the extent that the other state reciprocates. The policy is implemented through bilateral and multilateral air service agreements.

**What happens after an aviation accident?**
The AAC, through its designated investigator, leads the investigation of air accidents and incidents occurring in Salvadoran territory (Article 103-A). ICAO-recommended norms and methods are followed. The state must protect all evidence and maintain effective custody. Operators must report any accident or incident within 24 hours (Article 106). The AAC may delegate investigations to a national or international accident investigation body. The investigation is separate from any criminal proceedings that the Attorney General's Office may pursue.

**What are the penalties for violating aviation law?**
The AAC may impose sanctions ranging from warnings to fines, temporary suspension, cancellation of permits or licenses, and destruction of unauthorized installations (Article 191). Infractions are classified as minor (fines of 1-30 minimum commercial-sector wages), serious, and very serious (fines up to 190-250 minimum wages for operators). Specific fines of 1-20 minimum wages apply to aeronautical technical personnel. A 3% monthly late-payment interest accrues on unpaid fines, and repeat offenders face a 25% surcharge on the applicable fine (Article 196-C). Unpaid fines may be collected through executive judicial proceedings.

**Can foreign aircraft operate in Salvadoran airspace?**
Yes, but subject to conditions. Foreign aircraft overflying or landing in El Salvador are subject to Salvadoran jurisdiction (Article 2). Foreign airlines seeking to provide public air transport to and from El Salvador must obtain operating permits from the AAC, provided their home-state certificates meet Salvadoran requirements and the operations align with bilateral or multilateral agreements (Article 73). The open skies policy facilitates international operations based on reciprocity. All foreign aircraft must carry adequate insurance coverage (Article 178).

**How long can airport operating permits last?**
The AAC may grant operating permits for up to 50 years for civil airports, up to 10 years for civil heliports, and up to 3 years for landing strips (Article 83). These are renewable provided the permit holder has fully complied with the requirements set by the law, its regulations, and applicable international treaties. Permits terminate upon expiration, holder withdrawal, AAC cancellation for noncompliance, failure to fulfill the permit's purpose, or dissolution or bankruptcy of the permit-holding entity (Article 85).
