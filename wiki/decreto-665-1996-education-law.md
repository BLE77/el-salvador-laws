# Decreto 665 (1996) — Teaching Career Law

## Summary

The Teaching Career Law (Ley de la Carrera Docente), enacted on March 7, 1996, is the primary statute governing the professional status, rights, obligations, and disciplinary regime of educators in El Salvador. It regulates the relationship between the State and the educational community with respect to teachers serving in public, autonomous, municipal, and private institutions. The law establishes a comprehensive teacher registration and ranking system (escalafon), defines the process for entering the teaching profession, sets salary structures tied to academic level and seniority, creates governance bodies at the school and national level, and provides a detailed disciplinary framework with due-process protections.

The law replaced prior fragmented legislation on the teaching profession, consolidating teacher ranking rules and career regulations into a single instrument. It has been amended numerous times since 1996 to address issues such as sexual harassment protections (reform 20), performance bonuses (reforms 21-22), seniority subcategories (reform 17), voluntary resignation benefits (reform 26), and modernized teacher selection procedures using electronic systems (reform 29).

## Key Provisions

**Article 1 -- Purpose.** The law regulates the State's relationship with educators serving in public, autonomous, municipal, and private institutions, and systematically evaluates the teacher ranking system based on academic training and seniority.

**Article 2 -- Objective.** Guarantees that teaching is exercised only by educators registered in the Ministry of Education's Teacher Registry (Registro Escalafonario), ensuring job stability as a means to achieve quality education.

**Article 3 -- Scope of Application.** Applies to: (1) educators in public teaching and educational-technical positions; (2) educators in private schools regarding registration, classification, and teaching capacity; and (3) retired and pensioned educators.

**Article 3-A -- Guiding Principles.** Equality, prohibition of all forms of discrimination, and the best interest of the child are the special guiding principles for interpretation and application of the law.

**Article 5 -- Teacher Training Objectives.** The Ministry of Education must plan and regulate teacher training to: form teachers scientifically and ethically, stimulate improvement through merit-based compensation, prepare sufficient teachers to meet population needs, guarantee employment for trained educators, and promote education as a tool for personal and national development.

**Articles 6-11 -- Teacher Registry (Registro Escalafonario).** Establishes a systematic, updated, and reliable registry of all national teachers. Each educator's file must contain personal data, birth certificate, teaching degree and transcripts, service time, positions held, promotions, specialization courses, awards, and disciplinary sanctions. Upon registration, the educator receives a Professional Identification Number.

**Article 14 -- Requirements to Practice Teaching.** Teaching is a professional career requiring: (1) a recognized pedagogical degree; (2) registration in the Teacher Registry; and (3) no disqualifying impediments.

**Article 18 -- Teacher Selection Process.** Registered educators may compete for public teaching positions through a formal selection process administered by the Qualifying Tribunal (Tribunal Calificador). Applicants submit credentials within five business days of a vacancy announcement. Applications may be filed in person at departmental education offices or electronically. Selection criteria include, in order: specialty, graduation seniority, place of residence, knowledge test scores (in case of ties), and re-entry status. Salvadoran educators have priority over other Central Americans, who in turn have priority over other foreigners. Persons with disabilities receive priority per the Special Inclusion Law.

**Article 18-A -- Teacher Transfers.** Transfers are voluntary and may be requested for advanced studies, improved working conditions, family convenience, health reasons, or imminent life risk. Educators must have at least three years of service at their current school, except when health or life risks are involved.

**Articles 19-21 -- Teacher Ranking Structure.** Teachers are classified into two levels based on academic degree: Level One (holders of a Licenciatura, Master's, or Doctorate in Education, or other professionals who completed a pedagogical training course) and Level Two (holders of a Professor's degree or equivalent). Teachers are further classified into six seniority categories: Category 1 (over 25 years, with subcategories 1-A, 1-B, 1-C), Category 2 (20-25 years), Category 3 (15-20 years), Category 4 (10-15 years), Category 5 (5-10 years), and Category 6 (up to 5 years).

**Article 30 -- Rights of Educators.** Educators have the right to: job stability (removal only through legal procedures), free association in unions, consultative participation in curriculum planning, representation in administrative bodies, salary advancement, vacations, licenses, holidays and year-end bonus, indemnification upon position elimination, voluntary resignation benefits, examination of their own registry file, transfers and exchanges, performance bonuses, special benefits for terminal or incapacitating illnesses, protection against discrimination based on chronic illness, and protection from physically incompatible work during pregnancy or illness.

**Article 31 -- Obligations of Educators.** Educators must: perform duties diligently, attend punctually, obey lawful instructions, maintain good conduct, respect the physical and moral integrity of students and colleagues, report any sexual violence against students, conserve educational materials, provide emergency assistance, observe hygiene and safety rules, maintain discretion on sensitive matters, and pursue ongoing professional development.

**Article 32 -- Prohibitions.** Educators are prohibited from: abandoning work without cause, engaging in partisan political or religious propaganda in schools, carrying weapons, committing any form of physical/psychological/sexual abuse against students, influencing political decisions of students or subordinates, coercing free association rights, making mandatory collections, using school premises for personal housing or unauthorized activities, and selling merchandise for personal benefit within school grounds.

**Articles 33-35 -- Salary and Compensation.** Teacher salaries are set considering the position held, with mandatory reviews at least every three years. Salary components include: base salary by level and category, position-related supplements, rural/remote area supplements, double-shift supplements, and supplements for ten consecutive years as director/subdirector/supervisor. A biannual performance bonus (June and December) is established for public-school teachers. Each category promotion carries a percentage increase: 10% from Category 6 to 5, 8% from 5 to 4 and 4 to 3, 6% from 3 to 2 and into Subcategories 1-C and 1-B, and 7% upon reaching Subcategory 1-A.

**Article 36 -- Indemnification for Position Elimination.** If an educator's position is eliminated, they receive indemnification equal to one monthly salary per year of service, with caps depending on salary level. Payment is made in equal monthly installments and is suspended if the educator takes another public position.

**Articles 36-A through 36-G -- Voluntary Resignation Benefits.** Educators who resign voluntarily after more than two years of service receive 15 days of base salary per year of service. Those who resign upon reaching pension eligibility receive 15 base salaries as compensation. Resignation benefits are exempt from income tax.

**Articles 41-52 -- Administrative Bodies.** The teaching career is jointly administered by: (1) the Ministry of Education's Human Resources unit; (2) school directors; (3) the School Board (Consejo Directivo Escolar); (4) the Qualifying Tribunal (Tribunal Calificador); (5) Teaching Career Boards (Juntas de la Carrera Docente); and (6) the Teaching Career Tribunal (Tribunal de la Carrera Docente). School Boards include the director, two teacher representatives, three parent representatives, and two student representatives (minimum age 12).

**Articles 53-56 -- Disciplinary Infractions.** Infractions are classified as minor (e.g., misuse of materials, negligence, smoking while teaching), serious (e.g., disrupting work, disobedience, disrespect, unauthorized absence, political propaganda in schools), and very serious (e.g., immoral conduct, substance abuse at work, sexual harassment, falsifying school records, theft of school property, carrying weapons, physical or psychological abuse of students, demanding enrollment fees).

**Articles 57-64 -- Sanctions.** Principal sanctions are: written warning (for minor faults), suspension without pay for 3-30 days (serious faults) or 30-60 days (very serious faults), and dismissal. The accessory sanction is disqualification from teaching. Dismissal results from committing a second very serious fault, except for sexual offenses which warrant dismissal on the first offense. Disqualification is permanent but rehabilitation may be requested after one year of separation.

## Who This Affects

- **Public school teachers** at all levels (preschool, basic, middle, and special education) who hold positions funded by the State
- **Private school teachers** regarding their registration, classification, and capacity to teach
- **School directors and subdirectors** in public institutions, who must meet specific qualifications and undergo selection processes
- **The Ministry of Education (MINEDUCYT)** as the entity responsible for administering the teacher registry, planning teacher training, and overseeing the teaching career
- **School Boards (Consejos Directivos Escolares)** composed of directors, teachers, parents, and students, who manage school-level governance
- **The Qualifying Tribunal and Teaching Career Boards and Tribunal** which administer teacher selection, disciplinary proceedings, and appeals
- **Students and parents** who participate in school governance through the School Board and have standing to file disciplinary complaints against educators
- **Teacher unions and professional organizations** whose members' rights to association, collective consultation, and representation are protected
- **Retired and pensioned educators** who remain subject to certain registry and classification provisions
- **Non-education professionals** who wish to enter teaching, subject to completing a one-year pedagogical training course (32 academic units minimum)

## Related Laws

- **Constitution of the Republic of El Salvador** -- Articles on education as a fundamental right and the State's obligation to organize the educational system
- **General Education Law (Ley General de Educacion)** -- The broader framework law governing the national education system, curricula, and educational levels
- **Higher Education Law (Ley de Educacion Superior)** -- Governs universities and higher education institutions that grant valid teaching degrees
- **Labor Code (Codigo de Trabajo)** -- Applies to private-school teachers in matters not covered by this law
- **Special Inclusion Law for Persons with Disabilities (Ley Especial de Inclusion de las Personas con Discapacidad)** -- Referenced for priority in teacher selection for persons with disabilities
- **General Law on Prevention of Risks in the Workplace (Ley General de Prevencion de Riesgos en los Lugares de Trabajo)** -- Referenced for workplace protections for educators with chronic illnesses
- **Law of the Salvadoran Institute of Teacher Welfare (Ley del Instituto Salvadoreno de Bienestar Magisterial)** -- Governs teacher pensions, health benefits, and the institute that validates incapacitating illness diagnoses
- **Salary Law (Ley de Salarios)** -- Sets the specific salary figures and budget allocations for teacher positions

## Common Questions

**Q: Who qualifies to be registered as a teacher in El Salvador?**
A: Any person holding a recognized pedagogical degree (Professor, Licenciado in Education, Master's in Education, or Doctorate in Education) issued by a Ministry-authorized higher education institution may register in the Teacher Registry. Non-education professionals (e.g., engineers, lawyers) may also qualify by completing a pedagogical training course of at least 32 academic units, or by holding a degree whose curriculum already includes at least 32 units of pedagogical training.

**Q: How are vacant teaching positions filled in public schools?**
A: Vacant positions are announced through two annual calls: March (for transfers only) and June (for new hires and re-entries). Candidates apply through the Qualifying Tribunal, either in person at departmental education offices or electronically. The Tribunal evaluates applicants based on specialty, graduation seniority, residence, knowledge test results, and re-entry status, then issues a binding decision within 45 business days.

**Q: What salary increases do teachers receive as they gain seniority?**
A: Teachers automatically advance through six seniority categories based on years of active service. Each promotion carries a percentage increase on the base salary: 10% when moving from Category 6 to 5 (at 5 years), 8% from 5 to 4 and 4 to 3, 6% from 3 to 2 and through the Subcategory 1 tiers, and 7% upon reaching Subcategory 1-A (over 35 years). Category advancement is automatic (de pleno derecho) and requires no application.

**Q: Can a teacher be fired, and what protections exist?**
A: Teachers enjoy strong job stability. They can only be dismissed, suspended, or disqualified through the procedures established in this law. Dismissal requires committing a very serious fault for the second time (with the exception of sexual offenses, which warrant dismissal on the first occurrence with a final conviction). All disciplinary proceedings are conducted before the Teaching Career Boards, with rights to defense, evidence presentation, and appeal to the Teaching Career Tribunal and ultimately to administrative courts.

**Q: What happens if a teacher is accused of sexual harassment or abuse?**
A: The law provides for immediate preventive suspension without pay when there is reasonable evidence of current or imminent risk to the alleged victim. The director or School Board must file a formal complaint with the Teaching Career Board within five business days and simultaneously notify the Attorney General's Office. If the final decision is acquittal, the teacher receives back pay for the suspension period and is reinstated.

**Q: What benefits does a teacher receive upon voluntary resignation?**
A: Teachers who resign voluntarily after more than two years of service receive 15 days of base salary per year of service (and proportionally for partial years). Those who resign upon reaching pension eligibility receive a lump sum of 15 monthly base salaries. Resignations must be submitted by the last business day of August to be funded in the following fiscal year's budget. The compensation is exempt from income tax.

**Q: What is the School Board (Consejo Directivo Escolar) and who serves on it?**
A: Every public school must have a School Board with legal personality granted by the Ministry of Education. It consists of: the school director (who serves as president), two elected teacher representatives, three elected parent representatives, and two elected student representatives (minimum age 12, with full voice and vote). The Board manages school finances, initiates disciplinary proceedings, requests teacher selection processes, and assigns positions based on Qualifying Tribunal decisions. Members serve two-year terms (except the director), receive no compensation, and may not be related to each other within the fourth degree of consanguinity or second degree of affinity.

**Q: Can a disqualified teacher ever return to teaching?**
A: Yes. Disqualification from teaching is permanent in principle, but the affected educator may request rehabilitation from the corresponding Teaching Career Board after at least one year of separation from the position. For sexual offense cases, the minimum rehabilitation period is determined by the Board based on the severity of the case. The Board evaluates whether rehabilitation is appropriate based on the circumstances.
