# Non-Compete Agreements in El Salvador

Non-compete agreements (clausulas de no competencia) are **not specifically regulated by statute** in El Salvador. There is no dedicated law that defines their enforceability, scope, or limits. As a result, their validity is determined by general principles of contract law and constitutional protections.

## Constitutional Considerations

The **Constitution of El Salvador** guarantees the **freedom to work** (libertad de trabajo) as a fundamental right. Courts generally view non-compete clauses with skepticism because they restrict this constitutional right. An overly broad non-compete that effectively prevents a person from earning a living in their field is likely to be struck down.

## When Non-Competes May Be Enforceable

While courts disfavor non-competes, they may be upheld if they meet reasonable conditions:

- **Limited duration**: Typically no more than **1 to 2 years** after the employment relationship ends
- **Limited geographic scope**: Restricted to a specific area rather than a blanket national or international prohibition
- **Specific activity**: Narrowly defined to protect legitimate business interests such as trade secrets or client relationships
- **Compensation**: The employee received adequate consideration (compensation) in exchange for accepting the restriction

## Likely Unenforceable Provisions

Non-compete agreements that are **overly broad** in any of the following ways are likely unenforceable:

- Indefinite or excessively long duration
- No geographic limitation
- Prohibiting the employee from working in their entire profession
- No compensation provided for the restriction period

## Practical Recommendations

Employers drafting non-compete clauses should keep them narrow and reasonable. Employees asked to sign one should negotiate the terms and understand that overly restrictive clauses may not hold up in court. In disputes, a judge will balance the employer's legitimate interests against the worker's constitutional right to employment.
