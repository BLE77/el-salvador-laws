# Decreto 849 (2000) -- Cooperative Banks and Savings and Credit Societies Law

## Summary
The Cooperative Banks and Savings and Credit Societies Law regulates the organization, operation, and financial intermediation activities of non-bank financial intermediaries in El Salvador. Originally enacted as the "Law of Non-Bank Financial Intermediaries" and later renamed, the law was created to strengthen access to financial services in urban and rural communities, particularly for small savings and financing for micro, small, and medium enterprises. It subjects cooperative banks (including credit unions and former *cajas de credito rurales* and *bancos de los trabajadores*), savings and credit societies, and their federations to prudential regulation and supervision by the Superintendency of the Financial System (SSF). The law replaced the earlier Law of Credit Unions and Workers Banks (Decreto 770, 1991) and has been substantially reformed, most notably by Decreto 697 (reform package referenced as "(6)" throughout the text).

## Key Provisions
- **Article 1**: The law regulates the organization, operation, and financial intermediation activities of cooperative banks and savings and credit societies, ensuring they meet their economic and social objectives and guarantee depositors and members the most efficient and reliable administration of their resources.
- **Article 2**: Regulated entities include: (a) cooperative banks, comprising credit unions that accept public deposits or whose deposits and contributions exceed 600 million colones; (b) savings and credit societies; and (c) federations of cooperative banks qualified by the Superintendency to perform intermediation operations with their affiliates.
- **Article 3**: Regulated entities are governed by their own organizational laws except where this law prevails; gaps are filled by the Banking Law (*Ley de Bancos*). All entities are subject to SSF supervision and must comply with the Anti-Money Laundering Law.
- **Article 7**: A cooperative must have at least 100 members to receive SSF authorization.
- **Article 15**: Directors must be members of recognized honorability with extensive financial and administrative experience. The law sets out detailed disqualifications including age (under 30), criminal convictions, participation in narcotics or money laundering, outstanding debts with significant reserves, and holding senior government positions.
- **Article 16**: Minimum paid-in capital for a regulated cooperative is five million colones. The SSF updates this amount every two years based on the Consumer Price Index.
- **Article 19**: Cooperatives must set aside at least 20% of annual profits as a legal reserve until it reaches 50% of paid-in capital.
- **Article 25**: To maintain solvency, cooperative banks must maintain a ratio of at least 12% between their net worth (*fondo patrimonial*) and their risk-weighted assets. New cooperative banks must maintain 14.5% for their first three years. The SSF may impose additional requirements of up to two percentage points for operational risk, market risk, and other risks.
- **Article 27**: The SSF establishes a liquidity reserve proportional to deposits and obligations. Negotiable public-offering obligations backed by mortgage credits with maturities of five years or more are exempt from this reserve.
- **Article 34**: Cooperative banks may perform a wide range of operations in national and foreign currency, including: receiving demand deposits (checking), savings and term deposits, issuing debit cards, discounting bills, granting all types of loans, custody services, fund transfers, issuing or administering credit cards (with SSF authorization), investing in government securities, foreign exchange operations, issuing bonds, and granting guarantees.
- **Article 42**: Cooperatives freely set interest rates, commissions, and surcharges. The Central Bank may fix rates only in cases contemplated by Article 29 of the Constitution or during serious monetary imbalances, for periods not exceeding 180 days.
- **Article 44**: Each cooperative must establish and publish a single reference rate for lending operations. For adjustable-rate loans, the contract must specify the spread over the reference rate, adjustment frequency, and default interest. Charging interest on unearned interest is prohibited, except for mortgage loans of five years or more where interest capitalization may facilitate access to housing.
- **Article 48**: No cooperative bank may extend credit or assume risk with a single person or entity exceeding 10% of its net worth. Related borrowers are aggregated.
- **Article 49**: Total credits, guarantees, and endorsements to directors, managers, and federation officials may not exceed 5% of the net worth. These may not be on more favorable terms than those offered to third parties for similar operations.
- **Article 59**: All cooperative accounts and operations must be audited annually by an external auditor registered with the SSF.
- **Article 69**: When a cooperative's net worth falls below the required level, its board must present a regularization plan to the SSF within 15 business days. The cooperative has 90 days (extendable to 180) to restore compliance. Losses must be amortized immediately without needing a general assembly vote.
- **Article 75**: The SSF may decree intervention of a cooperative when insolvency is not remedied during special supervision, or when illegal practices of such magnitude endanger public deposits. In cases of grave insolvency, intervention may proceed without prior special supervision.
- **Article 82**: If the Superintendent determines that a cooperative's financial situation cannot be normalized, the SSF prepares a file requesting judicial dissolution and forced liquidation before the competent commercial court.
- **Article 103**: Payments during liquidation follow a strict priority: (a) wages, social and food-related benefits; (b) other privileged obligations; (c) obligations to foreign banks from credit lines; (d) public deposits; (e) obligations to local and foreign banks; (f) other obligations.
- **Article 106**: Cooperative banks are members of the Deposit Guarantee Institute (*Instituto de Garantia de Depositos*) with the same obligations and rights as commercial banks.
- **Article 123**: Directors, administrators, officers, employees, and external auditors who violate applicable laws or intentionally cause harm to the institution or third parties are personally liable for damages. The cooperative is jointly and severally liable. Disclosure of confidential information is prohibited, with exceptions for courts, the Attorney General, the tax authority during audits, and inter-bank data exchange for operational security.
- **Article 133**: Executive judicial proceedings brought by cooperative banks follow common rules with modifications: the evidence period is eight days, the cooperative serves as depositary of seized assets without bond, and property auctions use valuations by two SSF-registered appraisers.
- **Article 149**: Federations are cooperative associations or societies whose members are cooperative banks or savings and credit societies.
- **Article 158**: Savings and credit societies are subject to the Banking Law provisions on organization, administration, and operation, with modifications: they may not be established by public promotion, they may not form banking groups, and they require a minimum of 25 founding shareholders.

## Who This Affects
This law affects all cooperative banks (including former credit unions, *cajas de credito rurales*, and *bancos de los trabajadores*), savings and credit societies, and their federations operating in El Salvador. It directly impacts the members and depositors of these institutions, the officers and directors who manage them, the external auditors who examine them, and the federations (such as FEDECREDITO) that coordinate and support their affiliated cooperatives. Borrowers from these institutions are also affected by the credit and collection provisions. The Superintendency of the Financial System, the Central Bank of El Salvador, and the Deposit Guarantee Institute are the primary regulatory bodies involved.

## Related Laws
- Banking Law (*Ley de Bancos*) -- serves as the supplementary legal framework for matters not specifically addressed in this law; provisions on supervision, deposit guarantee, and consolidated supervision apply to cooperative banks
- Anti-Money Laundering Law (*Ley Contra el Lavado de Dinero y de Activos*) -- all regulated entities must comply with its provisions (Art. 3)
- Organic Law of the Superintendency of the Financial System (*Ley Organica de la Superintendencia del Sistema Financiero*) -- governs the supervisory authority, sanctions, and administrative procedures
- Law of the Deposit Guarantee Institute (*Ley del Instituto de Garantia de Depositos*) -- cooperative banks are members with the same rights and obligations as commercial banks (Art. 106)
- General Law of Cooperative Associations (*Ley General de Asociaciones Cooperativas*) -- governs the constitution and internal organization of cooperative entities
- Commercial Code (*Codigo de Comercio*) -- applies to mergers, capital reductions, share transfers, and other corporate matters where this law does not provide specific rules
- Penal Code (Decreto 1030, 1997) -- criminal sanctions for fraudulent administration (Art. 215), falsification of financial statements (Art. 283), obstruction of supervision (Art. 286), and fraudulent bankruptcy (Art. 242)
- Decreto 770 (1991) -- former Law of Credit Unions and Workers Banks, repealed by this law (Art. 186)

## Common Questions
- **Q: What types of institutions does this law regulate?** -- A: Three categories: (1) cooperative banks, which include credit unions that accept public deposits or exceed 600 million colones in deposits and contributions; (2) savings and credit societies; and (3) federations of cooperative banks qualified by the SSF (Art. 2).
- **Q: What is the minimum capital requirement?** -- A: Regulated cooperatives must maintain paid-in capital of at least five million colones. For cooperative banks that wish to offer demand deposits (checking accounts), the minimum net worth is six million US dollars (Arts. 16, 34).
- **Q: How are depositors protected?** -- A: Cooperative banks are members of the Deposit Guarantee Institute with the same protections as commercial bank depositors. Additionally, cooperatives must maintain a 12% capital adequacy ratio, a liquidity reserve, and the SSF exercises ongoing supervision (Arts. 25, 27, 106).
- **Q: Can cooperative banks offer checking accounts and credit cards?** -- A: Yes. Cooperative banks may offer demand deposits withdrawable by checks (subject to SSF approval and a minimum net worth of six million US dollars), savings accounts, term deposits, debit cards, and credit cards with prior SSF authorization (Art. 34).
- **Q: What happens if a cooperative becomes insolvent?** -- A: The law establishes a three-stage process: (1) regularization, where the cooperative presents a plan to restore its capital within 90 to 180 days; (2) special supervision by the SSF for up to 240 days; (3) intervention and, if recovery is impossible, judicial dissolution and forced liquidation (Arts. 69, 73-75, 82).
- **Q: Are there limits on lending to related parties?** -- A: Yes. Total credits, guarantees, and endorsements to directors, managers, and federation employees may not exceed 5% of the cooperative's net worth. No single borrower may receive more than 10% of the net worth. Related-party loans may not have more favorable terms than those offered to the general public (Arts. 48-49).
- **Q: What are the criminal penalties under this law?** -- A: Directors, managers, or employees who present false financial statements face charges of document falsification (Penal Code Art. 283). Those who obstruct SSF supervision face sanctions under Penal Code Art. 286. Self-dealing and fraudulent operations are punishable under Penal Code Art. 215, and in case of forced liquidation, responsible persons may be charged with fraudulent bankruptcy under Penal Code Art. 242 (Arts. 126-132).
- **Q: What is the role of FEDECREDITO and the federations?** -- A: Federations are cooperative associations whose members are cooperative banks or savings and credit societies. They are qualified by the SSF to perform intermediation operations with their affiliates, set deposit norms, administer the Stabilization Fund, and may propose board members for cooperatives under intervention (Arts. 149-157).
