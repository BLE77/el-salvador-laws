# Decreto 697 (1999) — Banking Law (Ley de Bancos)

## Summary

This is El Salvador's primary banking regulation law. It governs the creation, operation, and supervision of banks and financial intermediation, aiming to ensure a transparent, reliable, and agile banking system. The law establishes requirements for bank incorporation, capital minimums, ownership restrictions, branching, operations, and supervision by the Superintendency of the Financial System.

## Key Provisions

- **Article 1**: The law regulates financial intermediation and other operations performed by banks, ensuring transparent, reliable, and agile service (servicio transparente, confiable y agil) that contributes to national development.
- **Article 2**: Banks are incorporated as corporations with fixed capital (sociedades anonimas de capital fijo), for a fixed term, and operate under a concession from the Superintendency of the Financial System (Superintendencia del Sistema Financiero).
- **Article 3**: Public or private financial institutions established by special laws continue to be governed by those laws in everything that does not contradict this law. The Central Reserve Bank (Banco Central) is excluded.
- **Article 4**: Banks may adopt any commercial name not belonging to another entity. The term "Bank" (Banco) is exclusive to authorized institutions. No unauthorized person may use signage or materials suggesting a banking business.
- **Article 8**: Bank articles of incorporation must include provisions for treasury shares (acciones de tesoreria) at nominal value, held in deposit at the Central Bank, equivalent to the greater of the bank's actual or required patrimonial fund.
- **Article 10**: Ownership provisions (derogated by D.L. 526/2026).
- **Article 11**: No person may hold more than 1% of a bank's capital without prior authorization from the Superintendency. Authorization is denied for persons who are bankrupt, convicted of crimes, involved in narcotrafficking/money laundering, or are delinquent debtors in the financial system.
- **Article 12**: A shareholder is considered "relevant" (accionista relevante) when holding 10% or more of voting shares (including spouse and first-degree relatives), or having power to elect one or more directors. Relevant shareholders must submit annual audited financial statements.
- **Article 17**: Bank promoters (minimum 10 persons) must submit applications to the Superintendency including names, backgrounds, proposed operations, economic justification, and initial capital.
- **Article 20**: The Superintendency authorizes an entity to operate as a bank once all requirements are met, internal controls verified, and the articles of incorporation registered.
- **Article 22**: Banks must inform the Superintendent of each planned branch opening in the country. The Superintendent has 30 days to object only if it would negatively impact the bank's financial or administrative capacity.
- **Article 23**: Banks may operate abroad through offices and subsidiaries, subject to Superintendency authorization and provided the host country has adequate prudential regulation.

## Who This Affects

All commercial banks operating in El Salvador, their shareholders and directors, the Superintendency of the Financial System, bank customers and depositors, and any entity seeking to establish a banking business in the country.

## Common Questions

- **Q: Who supervises banks in El Salvador?** — A: The Superintendency of the Financial System (Superintendencia del Sistema Financiero) authorizes, regulates, and supervises all banks.
- **Q: Can anyone own more than 1% of a bank?** — A: Only with prior Superintendency authorization (Article 11). Authorization is denied to persons convicted of crimes, involved in money laundering, or in financial distress.
- **Q: What is required to start a new bank?** — A: At least 10 promoters must apply to the Superintendency with background information, a business plan, economic justification, and the initial paid-in capital (Article 17).
- **Q: Can Salvadoran banks operate internationally?** — A: Yes, through offices and subsidiaries in countries with adequate prudential regulation, with prior Superintendency authorization (Article 23).
