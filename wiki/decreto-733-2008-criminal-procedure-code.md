# Decreto 733 (2008) -- Criminal Procedure Code

## Summary
The Criminal Procedure Code establishes the rules and procedures for investigating, prosecuting, and adjudicating criminal offenses in El Salvador. It replaced the 1996 procedural code to create a more effective instrument for criminal justice. The code implements constitutional guarantees including the presumption of innocence, the right to defense, judicial independence, and due process, while structuring the criminal process through distinct phases: investigation, preliminary hearing, instruction, and trial.

## Key Provisions
- **Article 1**: No one may be convicted or subjected to a penalty without a prior trial conducted in accordance with this code, with full observance of constitutional guarantees.
- **Article 2**: Every person to whom a criminal act is attributed is presumed innocent (*presuncion de inocencia*) and must be treated as such at every stage of the proceeding until a final conviction.
- **Article 4**: Every accused person has the inviolable right to defense (*derecho de defensa*), including the right to a lawyer from the moment of detention.
- **Article 5**: Judges and magistrates are subject only to the Constitution, applicable international law, and the laws of the Republic. They must be independent and impartial. The same judge may not preside over different stages of the same case.
- **Article 8**: Personal liberty may only be restricted in the cases and under the requirements established by the Constitution and this code. Pretrial detention may not exceed 12 months for less serious crimes or 24 months for serious crimes.
- **Article 17**: Criminal actions are classified as: public action (prosecuted by the state), public action requiring a private complaint (*instancia particular*), and private action.
- **Article 18**: The Attorney General's Office (*Fiscalia General de la Republica*) is obligated to exercise public criminal action. If the prosecutor fails to file charges or archive an investigation within 4 months, the victim may demand a response within 5 days.
- **Article 30**: Public actions may be converted to private actions at the victim's request when the prosecutor decides to archive the investigation, when the offense requires a private complaint, or in property crimes (unless organized crime is involved).
- **Article 43**: In public action crimes, the civil action against participants must be exercised jointly with the criminal action. The victim may alternatively pursue civil claims in civil court, but not simultaneously in both.
- **Article 107**: In public action crimes, the victim may intervene in the process through a representative with all the rights and powers established by the Constitution and the code.
- **Article 211**: The parties may object to questions during witness and expert examinations. Objections must be timely and specific.
- **Article 268**: Upon receiving a complaint, the Attorney General must file charges (*requerimiento fiscal*) within 72 hours if the suspect is detained; otherwise, the investigation must proceed within established timelines.
- **Article 306**: Anticipated testimony may be taken when there is a difficult obstacle to overcome, such as grave illness, danger of violence, imminent departure from the country, or when the victim or witness is a child.
- **Article 351**: The judge may issue a definitive dismissal (*sobreseimiento definitivo*) when it is certain the act did not occur, does not constitute a crime, or the accused did not participate.
- **Article 392**: After evidence presentation, the prosecutor, complainant, and defense counsel give closing arguments (*conclusiones finales*). The defense always speaks last.
- **Article 419**: In the abbreviated procedure (*procedimiento abreviado*), the prosecutor and defense may agree on a reduced sentence. The accused must freely consent after understanding the consequences.
- **Article 452**: Judicial decisions may be challenged through the following appeals: revocation, appeal (*apelacion*), and cassation (*casacion*).
- **Article 502-I through 502-L**: International legal cooperation provisions, including mutual legal assistance, extradition cooperation, and the rule that political crimes, war crimes, and crimes against humanity are not considered political offenses for purposes of extradition.

## Who This Affects
Every person accused of a crime in El Salvador, as well as victims, witnesses, prosecutors, defense attorneys, and judges involved in criminal proceedings. It also affects law enforcement (police) who carry out investigations under the direction of the Attorney General.

## Common Questions
- **Q: How long can someone be held in pretrial detention?** -- A: Up to 12 months for less serious crimes and 24 months for serious crimes. Exceptionally, these limits may be extended during complex proceedings (Art. 8).
- **Q: Does the victim have a role in the criminal process?** -- A: Yes. The victim may intervene through a representative, has the right to be informed of proceedings, and can convert public actions to private actions in certain cases (Arts. 30, 107).
- **Q: What is the abbreviated procedure?** -- A: A streamlined process where the prosecutor and defense agree on charges and sentencing. The accused must freely consent. It results in a faster resolution with a potentially reduced sentence (Art. 419).
- **Q: How quickly must charges be filed?** -- A: Within 72 hours if the suspect is detained. Otherwise, the investigation proceeds within timelines set by the code (Art. 268).
- **Q: What appeals are available?** -- A: Revocation (for procedural orders), appeal (*apelacion*), and cassation (*casacion*) for final judgments (Art. 452).
- **Q: Can public crimes become private cases?** -- A: Yes, the victim can request conversion to a private action when the prosecutor archives the case, the crime requires a private complaint, or it is a property crime without organized crime involvement (Art. 30).
