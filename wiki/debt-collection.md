# Debt Collection Laws

Debt collection in El Salvador is governed by the **Civil Code** (which covers contracts and obligations), the **Civil and Commercial Procedure Code (Decreto 712)** (for enforcement actions), and the **Consumer Protection Law (Decreto 776)** (which prohibits abusive collection practices).

## Creditor Rights

- Creditors can pursue **civil legal action** (juicio ejecutivo) to recover unpaid debts when they have documented proof of the obligation, such as a signed contract, promissory note (pagare), or check.
- The executive process (juicio ejecutivo) is faster than ordinary civil proceedings and allows the court to order seizure of debtor assets.
- For debts without a formal title document, creditors must file an ordinary civil lawsuit (juicio ordinario), which takes longer.

## Consumer Protection Against Abuse

- While El Salvador does not have a specific debt collection harassment statute, the **Consumer Protection Law (Decreto 776)** prohibits **abusive practices** by businesses and their collection agents.
- Prohibited conduct includes threats, intimidation, harassment at unreasonable hours, contacting the debtor's employer or family to embarrass them, and misrepresenting the legal consequences of non-payment.
- Complaints about abusive collection practices can be filed with the **Defensoria del Consumidor**.

## Wage Garnishment

- Courts may order **wage garnishment (embargo de salario)** to satisfy unpaid debts.
- However, the **Labor Code (Decreto 15)** limits garnishment to protect workers' livelihoods. Only a **percentage of salary above the minimum wage** may be garnished.
- The minimum wage itself is generally exempt from garnishment, except for child support (pension alimenticia) obligations, which have priority.

## Statute of Limitations

- The right to collect debts is subject to **prescription (prescripcion)**. General civil obligations prescribe after a period defined by law, typically ranging from 3 to 10 years depending on the type of debt.
- Once prescribed, the debt cannot be legally enforced through the courts.
