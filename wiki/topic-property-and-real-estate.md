# Property and Real Estate in El Salvador

## Summary

El Salvador has a relatively open real estate market. Foreigners can buy property in most circumstances, though there are constitutional restrictions on border-zone land. The property system revolves around the Registro de la Propiedad Raiz e Hipotecas (Real Property and Mortgage Registry), where all land titles, mortgages, and encumbrances are recorded. Buying, building on, and renting property involves multiple legal frameworks: the Constitution (1983), the Civil Code (1860, with reforms), the Land Use Planning Law (Decreto 644/2011), the Municipal Code (Decreto 274/1986), and various municipal ordinances. Property taxes are handled at both the national level (transfer tax) and the municipal level (annual taxes on real estate).

---

## Can Foreigners Buy Property?

### General Rule: Yes

The Constitution of El Salvador (Art. 109) and the Civil Code do not prohibit foreigners from acquiring real property. A foreign individual or company can buy houses, apartments, commercial buildings, and agricultural land on the same terms as a Salvadoran citizen. There is no requirement to obtain residency before purchasing property.

### Border Zone Restriction

**Article 109 of the Constitution** establishes a restriction: land within a border zone cannot be owned by foreigners (by birth or naturalization from the neighboring country). The purpose is national security. This prohibition covers a strip of land along the borders with Guatemala and Honduras. In practice, this affects rural border parcels; most urban and suburban property is unaffected.

### Practical Considerations for Foreign Buyers

- **NIT required.** Any buyer (foreign or domestic) needs a NIT (Numero de Identificacion Tributaria) -- a tax identification number issued by the Ministry of Finance. Foreigners can obtain one with their passport.
- **No residency requirement.** You do not need to be a resident to buy, own, or sell property. However, you may need a local representative or attorney to handle paperwork if you are not physically present.
- **Power of attorney.** Foreign buyers who cannot attend the closing in person can grant a special power of attorney (poder especial) to a Salvadoran attorney to sign the deed on their behalf. The power of attorney must be notarized and, if executed abroad, apostilled.
- **Corporate ownership.** Foreigners may also buy property through a Salvadoran corporation (sociedad anonima). There is no restriction on foreign ownership of Salvadoran companies.

## Property Registry (Registro de la Propiedad Raiz e Hipotecas)

### How Titles Work

El Salvador uses a deed-registration system, not a Torrens title system. The key features are:

- **Public registry.** All real property transactions -- sales, donations, mortgages, easements, liens, and judicial orders -- must be registered at the Registro de la Propiedad Raiz e Hipotecas to be enforceable against third parties.
- **Escritura publica (public deed).** Every property transfer must be formalized in a public deed (escritura publica) executed before a Salvadoran notary. The notary verifies the identity of the parties, the property description, and the legal capacity of the seller.
- **Inscription creates opposability.** Ownership is transferred between the parties at the moment the deed is signed, but the transfer only becomes effective against third parties (oponible a terceros) when the deed is inscribed in the Registry.
- **Chain of title.** The Registry maintains a folio or file for each property showing every transaction in chronological order. A clean chain of title means an unbroken sequence of registered transfers from a recognized origin to the current owner.
- **Marginal annotations.** Mortgages, liens (embargos), lawsuits affecting the property (anotaciones preventivas), and other encumbrances appear as marginal annotations on the property's registry folio.

### Registry Offices

There are registries in San Salvador, Santa Ana, and San Miguel covering the three judicial zones of the country. The Centro Nacional de Registros (CNR) oversees the system.

## Buying Process and Due Diligence

### Step-by-Step

1. **Identify the property.** Verify the property's cadastral number (numero catastral) and registry inscription number.
2. **Title search (estudio de titulo).** A lawyer performs a title search at the Registro de la Propiedad, reviewing the full chain of title, checking for mortgages, liens, embargos, pending lawsuits, and any other encumbrances. This is the single most critical step.
3. **Cadastral verification.** Confirm the property boundaries and area match the registered description. A surveyor (topografo) may be needed if boundaries are unclear or if the property has never been formally surveyed.
4. **Municipal solvency (solvencia municipal).** Obtain a certificate from the municipality confirming the seller has no outstanding municipal taxes on the property (Art. 100, Decreto 274/1986). The Registry will not inscribe a transfer without this.
5. **Tax solvency.** Verify that the seller is current on national tax obligations. The buyer should also confirm no pending tax liens.
6. **Negotiate and sign a promise of sale (promesa de venta).** Optional but common. This preliminary contract locks in the price and conditions while the parties complete due diligence.
7. **Execute the public deed (escritura publica).** Both parties (or their attorneys via power of attorney) appear before a notary. The deed includes the property description, price, payment terms, and any conditions.
8. **Pay the transfer tax (impuesto de transferencia).** The buyer pays the transfer tax to the Ministry of Finance before inscription (see Property Taxes below).
9. **Inscribe the deed at the Registry.** The notary or buyer presents the deed and tax receipts to the Registro de la Propiedad for inscription. Once inscribed, the buyer is the recognized owner against the world.

### Red Flags During Due Diligence

- **Gaps in the chain of title.** Any period where ownership is unclear or transfers were not registered.
- **Pending embargos or lawsuits.** Marginal annotations showing judicial seizures or ongoing litigation.
- **Informal subdivisions.** Property that was subdivided without municipal or planning authority approval.
- **Possessory claims.** Occupants who may have established rights through adverse possession (see below).
- **Missing municipal solvency.** Indicates the seller owes municipal taxes.

## Land Use Planning and Zoning (Decreto 644/2011)

The Land Use Planning Law (Ley de Ordenamiento y Desarrollo Territorial, LODT) governs how land is classified and what can be built where.

### Zoning Categories (Art. 62, D-644/2011)

| Zone | Description |
|---|---|
| **Urban consolidated** | Fully developed areas with existing infrastructure |
| **Urban non-consolidated** | Urban areas with partial development |
| **Urbanizable** | Land designated for future urban development |
| **Rural** | Agricultural and primary-sector uses |
| **Non-urbanizable** | Protected from development (environmental, risk, or legal reasons) |

### Key Rules

- **No building without a plan.** All urban-purpose construction requires prior approval based on the Municipal Territorial Plan. No permits may be issued if the municipality has not adopted a plan (Art. 64).
- **Environmental restrictions.** Non-urbanizable zones cannot be developed. Building there is a "very serious infraction" with fines up to 1,000 monthly minimum wages (Art. 75, 81).
- **Densification compensation.** Owners who increase population density in consolidated urban zones must compensate the municipality for impacts on traffic, services, and public facilities (Art. 66).
- **Owner compensation.** If a new plan prohibits a landowner from continuing a lawful pre-existing economic activity, the owner is entitled to compensation (Art. 61).
- **Public consultation.** At least two public consultations are required before any territorial plan is approved (Art. 57).

## Construction Permits

### Who Issues Permits

Construction permits are issued by the municipal government (alcaldia) where the property is located. In the San Salvador Metropolitan Area (AMSS), the Oficina de Planificacion del Area Metropolitana de San Salvador (OPAMSS) handles permits for the metropolitan zone.

### General Process (Decreto 274/1986 and D-644/2011)

1. **Submit plans.** The property owner or developer submits architectural and engineering plans to the municipality, signed by a licensed professional (architect or civil engineer registered with the relevant professional association).
2. **Zoning review.** The municipality verifies the proposed construction is compatible with the zoning classification and applicable territorial plan.
3. **Environmental review.** Projects above certain thresholds require an environmental impact assessment under the Environmental Law (Decreto 233/1998).
4. **Permit issuance.** If everything complies, the municipality issues the construction permit (permiso de construccion). The permit specifies what may be built, maximum height, setbacks, and other conditions.
5. **Inspections.** The municipality may inspect the work during construction to verify compliance.
6. **Occupancy permit.** After completion, the owner requests a habitability or occupancy permit (permiso de habitabilidad) confirming the building was constructed according to the approved plans.

### Penalties for Building Without a Permit

Unauthorized construction is a serious infraction under Art. 76 of D-644/2011, punishable by fines up to 200 monthly minimum wages. If the construction is on non-urbanizable or protected land, fines increase to 1,000 monthly minimum wages. Demolition at the owner's expense may be ordered (Art. 82). The developer, engineer, and any official who improperly approved the project can all be held individually liable (Art. 77).

## Rental and Landlord-Tenant Rules

### Legal Framework

Rental relationships for real property are governed primarily by the Civil Code (Titulo XXVI, "Del arrendamiento"), the Ley de Inquilinato (Tenancy Law), and the Land Use Planning Law (Decreto 644/2011). Consumer protection provisions under Decreto 776/2005 may also apply to certain service aspects of rental agreements.

**Decreto 644 (2011)** applies to all residential and commercial rental properties by requiring that all inhabited structures comply with applicable zoning, habitability, and urban development standards set by municipal territorial plans. Landlords must ensure rented properties meet the minimum habitability requirements established under D-644/2011. Rental of properties that do not meet these standards (substandard structures in non-urbanizable zones, uninspected buildings, etc.) can expose the landlord to sanctions.

### Key Tenant Rights

- **Written lease recommended.** While verbal leases are legally valid, a written contract (contrato de arrendamiento) is strongly recommended and often required for enforcement purposes.
- **Deposit limits.** The deposit typically cannot exceed one or two months' rent. The landlord must return the deposit at the end of the lease, minus legitimate deductions for damages.
- **Habitability.** The landlord must deliver and maintain the property in habitable condition. The tenant has the right to demand essential repairs.
- **No arbitrary eviction.** The landlord cannot unilaterally evict a tenant. Eviction requires a judicial process (see below).
- **Rent increases.** For regulated tenancies, rent increases may be subject to restrictions. For freely negotiated commercial leases, the terms of the contract govern.

### Eviction Process

Eviction in El Salvador is a judicial procedure, not a self-help remedy. Key points:

- **Grounds for eviction.** Common grounds include non-payment of rent, expiration of the lease term, use of the property for purposes not authorized in the lease, and causing significant damage to the property.
- **Notice requirement.** The landlord must provide notice to the tenant before initiating judicial proceedings. For non-payment, the tenant is typically given an opportunity to cure the default.
- **Judicial proceeding.** If the tenant does not vacate voluntarily, the landlord must file a civil action (juicio de desahucio or juicio sumario de inquilinato) in the competent court. The court issues a judgment ordering eviction if the grounds are proven.
- **Forced eviction.** Only after a court order can the police or court officer physically remove a tenant. Self-help eviction (changing locks, cutting utilities, removing belongings) is illegal and exposes the landlord to civil and potentially criminal liability.
- **Timeline.** Judicial evictions can take several months depending on the court's caseload and whether the tenant contests the action.

### Landlord Obligations

- Maintain the property in a condition suitable for the agreed use.
- Perform major structural repairs.
- Respect the tenant's peaceful enjoyment of the property.
- Comply with municipal health and safety regulations.

## Squatter Rights and Adverse Possession (Usucapion/Prescripcion Adquisitiva)

### What Is Adverse Possession?

Under Salvadoran civil law (Civil Code, Arts. 2231-2260), a person who possesses real property openly, peacefully, continuously, and as if they were the owner (animo de dueno) for a statutory period can acquire legal title through a judicial proceeding called prescripcion adquisitiva or usucapion.

### Requirements

| Element | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Possession type** | Public, peaceful, continuous, uninterrupted |
| **Intent** | Animo de dueno (acting as owner, not as tenant or caretaker) |
| **Ordinary prescription** | 10 years of possession with just title and good faith |
| **Extraordinary prescription** | 20 years of possession without the need for just title or good faith |

### Key Rules

- **Judicial process required.** Adverse possession does not transfer title automatically. The possessor must file a judicial action (juicio de prescripcion adquisitiva) and prove all statutory elements before a court.
- **Not against the state.** Public land (bienes nacionales de uso publico) cannot be acquired through adverse possession.
- **Interruption.** The prescription period is interrupted by a lawsuit filed by the owner, by the possessor's acknowledgment of the owner's rights, or by the possessor abandoning the property.
- **Practical reality.** Many rural and peri-urban properties in El Salvador have occupants who lack formal title. Regularization programs (titulacion de tierras) have been used historically to formalize these situations, particularly for land distributed during the agrarian reform of the 1980s.

### Squatters in Practice

Unauthorized occupation of another person's property is not a right. The property owner can file a civil action to recover possession (accion reivindicatoria) or seek an interdict (interdicto de despojo) for recent dispossession. However, if the occupant has been present for many years and meets the statutory requirements, they may have a viable adverse possession claim.

## Property Taxes

### Transfer Tax (Impuesto sobre Transferencia de Bienes Raices)

When real property is sold, the buyer pays a transfer tax to the national government.

| Detail | Value |
|---|---|
| **Rate** | 3% of the sale price or appraised value (whichever is higher) |
| **Who pays** | The buyer (by law and custom) |
| **When due** | Before the deed is inscribed at the Registry |
| **Exemptions** | Certain transfers between spouses or to the state may be exempt |

### Municipal Property Taxes

Municipalities levy annual taxes on real property within their jurisdiction, authorized under the Municipal Code (Decreto 274/1986) and specific municipal tax laws approved by the Legislative Assembly.

- **Rate varies.** Each municipality has its own tax rate schedule, typically based on the assessed value of the property. Rates are generally modest.
- **Solvencia municipal.** Before any property transfer can be registered, the seller must obtain a municipal tax clearance certificate (solvencia) proving all municipal taxes are paid (Art. 100, D-274/1986).
- **Penalty for non-payment.** Municipalities can impose surcharges and pursue administrative collection for unpaid property taxes.

### Income Tax on Rental Income

Rental income is subject to income tax under Decreto 134/1991. Landlords must declare rental income in their annual tax return. Non-resident landlords are subject to withholding at the source.

### Capital Gains

Profit from selling real property is treated as income and is subject to income tax. The gain is calculated as the difference between the sale price and the acquisition cost (adjusted for improvements and inflation where applicable). A special capital gains rate of 10% applies to real property sales under certain conditions established by reforms to the Income Tax Law.

## Common Questions

### 1. Can a foreigner buy property in El Salvador?

Yes. There is no general prohibition on foreign ownership of real property. The only restriction is constitutional: land in the border zone with Guatemala or Honduras cannot be owned by nationals of the neighboring country (Art. 109, Constitution). In practice, most property purchases by foreigners proceed without issue. You will need a NIT (tax ID number), which can be obtained with a passport.

### 2. How do I verify that the seller actually owns the property?

Hire a Salvadoran attorney to perform a title search (estudio de titulo) at the Registro de la Propiedad Raiz e Hipotecas. The attorney will review the full chain of title, check for mortgages, liens, embargos, and pending lawsuits. This is the most important step in any property purchase. Do not skip it.

### 3. What taxes do I pay when buying property?

The main tax is the 3% transfer tax on the sale price or appraised value (whichever is higher), paid by the buyer before the deed is inscribed. You will also pay notary fees (typically 1-2% of the transaction value) and registry inscription fees. Additionally, you must obtain the seller's municipal solvencia confirming no outstanding municipal taxes.

### 4. Can my landlord evict me without a court order?

No. Eviction in El Salvador requires a judicial proceeding under the Civil Code and the Ley de Inquilinato. The landlord must file a lawsuit (juicio de desahucio) and obtain a court order. Self-help eviction -- such as changing locks, cutting water or electricity, or removing your belongings -- is illegal. The minimum notice period for eviction must comply with both the lease agreement terms and applicable legal minimums; one week's notice is generally not sufficient under Salvadoran law. If your landlord attempts self-help eviction, you can file a complaint with the police and pursue civil remedies. Additionally, the Land Use Planning Law (Decreto 644/2011) sets minimum habitability standards for rented residential properties -- landlords must ensure the property meets these standards throughout the tenancy.

### 5. What happens if someone has been living on my land for years?

If the occupant has possessed the land openly, peacefully, continuously, and with the intent of being the owner for 10 years (with just title and good faith) or 20 years (without), they may file a judicial claim for adverse possession (prescripcion adquisitiva). You should consult an attorney immediately. You can file a civil action to recover possession (accion reivindicatoria), but acting sooner is better -- the longer you wait, the stronger the occupant's potential claim becomes.

### 6. Do I need a permit to build on my own land?

Yes. All construction requires a permit from the municipal government (alcaldia), or from OPAMSS in the San Salvador metropolitan area. You must submit plans signed by a licensed architect or engineer, and the project must comply with the applicable zoning and territorial plan. Building without a permit is a serious infraction under D-644/2011, punishable by fines up to 200 monthly minimum wages, and the municipality can order demolition at your expense.

### 7. What are the zoning categories for land in El Salvador?

Under D-644/2011, land is classified as: urban consolidated, urban non-consolidated, urbanizable, rural, or non-urbanizable. Urban and urbanizable zones allow construction subject to permits. Rural zones are for agricultural use. Non-urbanizable zones are off-limits to development due to environmental protection, natural risks, or other legal restrictions. Building on non-urbanizable land carries fines up to 1,000 monthly minimum wages.

### 8. How much are annual property taxes?

Annual property taxes are set by each municipality and are generally low compared to many other countries. The rate depends on the assessed value of the property and the specific municipal tax law. Contact the alcaldia where the property is located for the exact rate. Remember that municipal tax clearance (solvencia) is required for any property transfer.

### 9. Is rental income taxable?

Yes. Rental income is subject to income tax under D-134/1991. You must declare it in your annual tax return. If you are a non-resident landlord, the tenant or property manager must withhold income tax at the source and remit it to the tax authority. Capital gains from selling property are also taxable, with a special 10% rate applying under certain conditions.

### 10. Can the government take my property?

Yes, through expropriation, but only for public utility or social interest purposes and with fair compensation. The Municipal Code (Arts. 138-155, D-274/1986) and the Ley de Expropiacion establish the procedure: the government must attempt voluntary purchase first, and if negotiation fails, must go through a judicial expropriation proceeding where a court determines the compensation amount. The landowner has the right to contest the valuation in court.
