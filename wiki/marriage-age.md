# Legal Marriage Age in El Salvador

The minimum legal age for marriage in El Salvador is **18 years old**, with **no exceptions**. This absolute minimum was established by reforms to the **Family Code (Codigo de Familia, Decreto 677 of 1993)** enacted in **2017**.

## The 2017 Reform

Prior to the 2017 reform:
- Girls could marry at **14** and boys at **16** with parental or judicial authorization.
- Judges could grant permission for marriage of minors in cases of pregnancy or other circumstances deemed to justify it.

The **2017 legislative reform** eliminated all exceptions:
- The minimum marriage age was raised to **18 for both men and women**.
- **Judicial authorization** for minor marriages was abolished entirely.
- **Parental consent** can no longer override the age requirement.
- No exceptions exist for pregnancy, religious custom, or any other circumstance.

This reform aligned El Salvador with international standards promoted by UNICEF and the UN Committee on the Rights of the Child to combat child marriage.

## Legal Framework

- **Article 14 of the Family Code (Decreto 677)**, as amended, establishes 18 as the minimum age for contracting marriage.
- The **Constitution of El Salvador** recognizes the family as the fundamental basis of society and mandates state protection of marriage.
- The **LEPINA (Decreto 839)** and **Decreto 431 (2022)** reinforce protections for children and adolescents, consistent with the prohibition on child marriage.

## Related Provisions

- **Age of majority**: 18 years, matching the marriage age.
- Marriages contracted in violation of the age requirement are **void** (nulo).
- Civil registrars and judges who solemnize marriages involving minors can face **criminal and administrative penalties**.
- Marriages performed abroad involving Salvadoran minors may not be recognized in El Salvador.

## Context

El Salvador's prohibition on child marriage without exceptions is among the strongest in Central America. The reform was driven by high rates of adolescent pregnancy and recognition that early marriage disproportionately affected girls, limiting their educational and economic opportunities. According to data preceding the reform, a significant percentage of Salvadoran women had married before age 18, making the legal change an important step in child protection policy.
