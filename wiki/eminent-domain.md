# Eminent Domain and Government Expropriation in El Salvador

The government of El Salvador has the power to expropriate private land, but this power is constitutionally limited. **Article 106 of the Constitution** establishes that expropriation is permitted only for reasons of **public utility or social interest**, and only with **prior just compensation**.

## Constitutional Requirements

Under Article 106, the following conditions must be met for lawful expropriation:

- **Public utility or social interest**: The government must demonstrate a legitimate public purpose, such as building roads, schools, hospitals, or infrastructure
- **Prior compensation**: The property owner must receive fair payment **before** the government takes possession
- **Just valuation**: Compensation must reflect the fair market value of the property
- **Legal authorization**: Expropriation requires a **legislative decree** or an **executive order** with proper legal basis

## The Expropriation Process

1. The government identifies the property needed for a public project
2. A **legislative decree or executive order** authorizes the expropriation
3. An **independent appraisal** determines the property's fair market value
4. The government offers compensation to the owner based on the appraisal
5. If the owner accepts, payment is made and title transfers
6. If the owner disagrees with the valuation, either party may seek judicial resolution

## Owner's Right to Challenge

Property owners have the constitutional right to **challenge the expropriation in court**. Common grounds for challenge include:

- The stated purpose does not qualify as public utility
- The compensation offered is below fair market value
- Procedural requirements were not followed

Legal counsel is strongly recommended for any property owner facing expropriation proceedings.
