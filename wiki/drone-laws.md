# Drone and UAV Regulations in El Salvador

## Legal Framework

Drone operations in El Salvador are governed by the Civil Aviation Law, **Decreto 582** (Ley de Aviacion Civil), which establishes the regulatory framework for all aircraft operating within Salvadoran airspace. Drones, referred to in the legislation as *aeronaves no tripuladas*, fall under the jurisdiction of the **Autoridad de Aviacion Civil (AAC)**, the country's Civil Aviation Authority.

## Authorization and Registration

Any person or entity wishing to operate a drone in El Salvador must obtain prior authorization from the AAC. This applies to both recreational and commercial operators, though the requirements differ in scope. Commercial drone use carries stricter obligations: operators must register the aircraft with the AAC, provide proof of liability coverage, and demonstrate competency in piloting. Commercial applications include aerial photography, surveying, agricultural spraying, and delivery services.

Recreational operators are also expected to follow AAC guidelines, though registration requirements are less burdensome for small drones below certain weight thresholds.

## Flight Restrictions

Decreto 582 and its implementing regulations impose several geographic and operational restrictions on drone flights:

- **Airport proximity**: Drones may not operate within a defined radius of any airport or aerodrome without explicit AAC clearance.
- **Military zones**: Flights over or near military installations are strictly prohibited.
- **Populated areas**: Operating drones over densely populated areas, public gatherings, or events is restricted due to safety concerns.
- **Altitude limits**: Drones must remain below designated altitude ceilings to avoid interfering with manned aircraft.
- **Night operations**: Flying drones at night requires special authorization.

## Penalties for Violations

Unauthorized drone operation, flying in restricted zones, or failure to register a commercial drone can result in administrative fines imposed by the AAC. Repeat offenders face escalating penalties, including confiscation of the aircraft. If a drone operation causes injury or property damage, the operator may also face civil liability and criminal prosecution under the Penal Code.

## Practical Considerations

Operators planning commercial drone work should apply for AAC authorization well in advance, as the approval process can take several weeks. Maintaining flight logs and carrying proof of authorization during operations is strongly recommended.
