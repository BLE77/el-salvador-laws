# Decreto 856 (2017) — Administrative Procedures Law

## Summary

The Administrative Procedures Law (Ley de Procedimientos Administrativos, or LPA) is El Salvador's first comprehensive statute governing the relationship between the public administration and citizens. Enacted on December 15, 2017, and entering into force twelve months after its publication in the Diario Oficial (February 13, 2018), this law fills a long-standing gap in Salvadoran law by establishing uniform rules that all government agencies must follow when issuing administrative acts, conducting procedures, imposing sanctions, and responding to citizen petitions. The law applies to the Executive Branch, autonomous entities, municipalities, and even the Legislative and Judicial branches when they exercise administrative powers. It contains 168 articles organized across titles covering general principles, administrative acts, procedures, appeals, government liability, sanctioning powers, and regulatory rulemaking.

The law was motivated by several considerations: the absence of a uniform procedural code for public administration, the need to guarantee the principle of legality, the importance of modernizing government through information technology, and the goal of making public administration more efficient, transparent, and respectful of fundamental rights. The President returned the decree with observations on January 9, 2018; the Legislative Assembly partially accepted those observations on January 30, 2018, before final enactment.

## Key Provisions

**Article 1 — Purpose.** The law regulates: (1) the validity and effectiveness requirements for all administrative acts; (2) the rights of citizens vis-a-vis public administration; (3) the patrimonial liability regime of the State and its officials; and (4) the exercise of regulatory power and the principles governing administrative sanctioning procedures.

**Article 2 — Scope of Application.** The law applies to the Executive Branch and all its agencies, autonomous entities, municipalities, and any public institution. It also applies to the Legislative and Judicial branches, the Court of Accounts, the Attorney General, the Human Rights Ombudsman, the Prosecutor General, the Superior Council of Public Health, the Supreme Electoral Tribunal, and concessionaires of the public administration when they exercise administrative powers.

**Article 3 — General Principles.** All administrative activity must respect the principles of legality, proportionality, anti-formalism, efficacy, celerity and ex officio impulse, economy, good faith, and due process. The anti-formalism principle is notable: no non-essential formal requirement may unjustifiably block the initiation, processing, or conclusion of a procedure.

**Article 4 — Simplification and No Redundant Requirements.** The administration may not demand documents it already possesses, documents the citizen has previously submitted, or documents that appear in public registries. It may not impose requirements not backed by law. Each institution must prepare an annual regulatory improvement plan.

**Article 17 — Rights of Citizens.** Citizens have the right to: (1) good administration, meaning equitable, just, objective, and impartial treatment with resolution within a reasonable time; (2) interact with the administration through available technological means; (3) access public information and administrative files; (4) security and confidentiality of personal data; (5) respectful and dignified treatment, with special preferential treatment for vulnerable persons (disabled, minors, pregnant women, elderly); (6) file complaints, suggestions, and appeals; and (7) receive public services meeting defined quality standards.

**Article 23 — Duty to State Reasons (Motivation).** Administrative acts that impose obligations, limit or deny rights, resolve appeals, modify prior criteria, contain a ruling on interim measures, revoke or modify prior acts, or are issued under discretionary authority must be supported by a statement of the relevant facts and legal grounds.

**Article 36 — Absolute Nullity.** An administrative act is absolutely null and void when: (a) it is issued by an authority manifestly lacking competence by subject matter or territory; (b) it is issued in complete disregard of the legally established procedure; (c) its content is materially or legally impossible or constitutes a criminal offense; (d) it is issued disregarding constitutional rights and freedoms; or (e) it is issued by a collegial body without the required quorum.

**Article 39 — Relative Nullity.** An administrative act is relatively null when it incurs in any irregularity not qualifying as absolute nullity, including incompetence by rank, omission of non-essential procedural steps, or failure to act within prescribed time limits when prejudicial. Relative nullity can be cured through convalidation.

**Article 55 — Right to Compensation.** Citizens have the right to be compensated by the public administration for injuries to their property or rights resulting from either the normal or abnormal functioning of the administration. State liability is institutional and predominantly objective, except for special rules applicable to constitutional rights violations.

**Article 60 — Liability for Constitutional Rights Violations.** When damages result from violation of constitutional rights (under Article 245 of the Constitution), liability is personal, subjective, and falls directly on the public servant. This represents a distinct liability track with stricter personal accountability.

**Article 71 — Content of a Petition.** A citizen's petition must contain: the authority addressed, the petitioner's name and personal details, address for notifications, the names of known third-party interested parties, the facts and legal grounds, the petition in precise terms, signature, and date.

**Article 82 — Time Limits for the Administration.** The administration must issue procedural acts within the following maximum deadlines: mere-formality acts within 5 days; expert opinions and technical reports within 20 days (extendable by another 20); and non-technical administrative reports within 15 days.

**Article 89 — Maximum Resolution Period.** When no specific time limit is prescribed by law, the administration must resolve and notify within 60 business days from the start of the procedure.

**Article 114 — Positive Administrative Silence.** In procedures initiated at the citizen's request, if the maximum resolution period expires without an express resolution being notified, the citizen's petition is deemed granted (positive silence). Exceptions include: pure constitutional petition rights with no statutory framework, requests involving the transfer of public domain or public service powers, appeals against administrative acts, and cases where a specific law prescribes negative silence.

**Article 124 — Administrative Appeals.** Two types of administrative appeals exist: (1) the appeal of reconsideration (recurso de reconsideración), which is optional and filed before the same body that issued the act; and (2) the appeal proper (recurso de apelación), which is mandatory before accessing the Contentious-Administrative Jurisdiction. An extraordinary review appeal is also available against final acts under limited circumstances.

**Article 127 — Suspension of Execution on Appeal.** Filing an appeal does not automatically suspend the challenged act. However, the appellate body may suspend execution when: (a) execution could cause irreparable harm; and (b) the appeal's grounds are reasonably justified. For acts ordering payment of liquid sums or imposing sanctions, filing an appeal automatically suspends execution.

**Article 140 — Principles Governing Sanctioning Powers.** The administration's sanctioning power is subject to: reservation of law (sanctions must be authorized by statute), typicality (only infractions and sanctions defined by law may be applied), non-retroactivity, presumption of innocence, proportionality, non bis in idem (no double punishment for the same act), and culpability.

**Article 146 — Non Bis In Idem.** Acts already sanctioned penally or administratively may not be sanctioned again when there is identity of subject, facts, and legal basis. When sanctions overlap partially, the prior sanction must be taken into account as a mitigating factor.

**Article 161 — Regulatory Impact Assessment.** Before deciding to regulate or not, the administration must conduct a Regulatory Impact Assessment (Evaluación de Impacto Regulatorio, or EIR) that includes problem definition, public consultations, stakeholder hearings, evidence gathering, and evaluation of alternatives.

**Article 162 — Rulemaking Procedure.** Administrative regulations must follow a defined procedure: (1) initiative by the responsible body or, where sector law permits, by private parties; (2) collection of legal, economic, environmental, technical, or scientific studies; (3) public hearing of affected citizens with a minimum 15-day comment period; (4) a reasoned report responding to public comments; and (5) publication.

**Article 163 — Derogation and Exceptions.** The LPA expressly repeals all provisions in general or special laws that contradict it. However, it does not repeal specialized procedures in: tax and customs matters, social security benefits, forced expropriation, military administration, public procurement contractor selection, and environmental matters. Those areas continue under their special laws, with the LPA as a supplementary source.

## Who This Affects

- **All citizens and residents** who interact with any public institution, whether filing permits, licenses, petitions, complaints, or appeals. The law guarantees their right to timely resolution, non-redundant documentation requirements, and dignified treatment.
- **Businesses and entrepreneurs** who must obtain authorizations, licenses, or permits from any government agency. The law simplifies procedures, prohibits agencies from demanding documents already in their possession, and mandates one-stop windows (ventanillas unicas) in sectors requiring multiple agency approvals.
- **Foreign investors and traders** dealing with foreign-origin documents, as the law limits requirements for legalization or apostille of foreign public documents in commercial and customs operations.
- **Government agencies and public servants** at all levels (central, autonomous, and municipal), who must comply with the procedural standards, time limits, duty to state reasons, and notification rules. Failure to meet deadlines triggers positive silence in favor of citizens.
- **Concessionaires of public services** that exercise administrative powers delegated by the state.
- **Lawyers and legal practitioners** handling administrative law cases, who now have a unified procedural framework for challenging administrative acts and pursuing state liability claims.
- **Vulnerable populations** (persons with disabilities, minors, pregnant women, elderly persons) who are entitled to preferential treatment and accessibility accommodations under Article 17.

## Related Laws

- **Constitution of the Republic of El Salvador (1983)** — The constitutional foundation, particularly Articles 11 (due process), 18 (right of petition), 86 (principle of legality), and 245 (personal liability of public officials for constitutional violations).
- **Ley de la Jurisdicción Contencioso Administrativa (Decreto 760, 2017)** — The companion law establishing the judicial jurisdiction for challenging administrative acts after exhausting administrative appeals under the LPA.
- **Ley de Acceso a la Información Pública (Decreto 534, 2011)** — Governs the right to access public information and government records, referenced repeatedly in the LPA.
- **Ley de Procedimiento para la Imposición del Arresto o Multa Administrativos (Decreto 457, 1990)** — Expressly repealed by Article 163 of the LPA.
- **Ley de Reclamaciones Pecuniarias de Extranjeros y Nacionales contra la Nación (1910)** — Expressly repealed by Article 163 of the LPA.
- **Ley del Seguro Social** — Its procedural provisions are repealed, though the social security benefits procedures themselves are exempted from the LPA.
- **Código Tributario and customs laws** — Exempt from the LPA, with their own specialized procedures preserved.
- **Decreto Ejecutivo 90 (2015)** — Created the Regulatory Improvement Agency (Organismo de Mejora Regulatoria), which oversees the Regulatory Impact Assessment process mandated by the LPA until a permanent entity is established by law.

## Common Questions

**1. When did this law take effect?**
The law was published in the Diario Oficial on February 13, 2018 (Tomo 418, No. 30), and entered into force twelve months later, in February 2019. Procedures initiated before that date continue under the prior rules.

**2. Does this law apply to tax and customs proceedings?**
No. Article 163 expressly exempts tax and customs procedures, social security benefit procedures, forced expropriation, military administration, public procurement contractor selection, and environmental procedures. Those areas retain their own specialized procedural rules, but the LPA applies supplementarily to anything not covered by the special law.

**3. What happens if the government does not respond to my petition on time?**
Under Article 114, if you initiated the procedure and the government fails to resolve and notify within the maximum legal period (60 business days if no special period applies), your petition is deemed granted by operation of positive administrative silence. There are exceptions for certain categories of requests, including those involving the public domain or public service powers, and appeals against prior administrative acts.

**4. What appeals can I file against an administrative decision?**
You have two options: (1) an optional reconsideration appeal (recurso de reconsideración) filed before the same body within 10 days (or one month for a presumed act), which must be resolved within one month; and (2) a mandatory appeal proper (recurso de apelación) filed before the superior hierarchical authority within 15 days, which must be resolved within 60 business days. You must exhaust the appeal proper before accessing the Contentious-Administrative courts. Additionally, an extraordinary review appeal is available against final acts in narrow circumstances (manifest error of fact, newly discovered essential documents, fraud, or falsified evidence).

**5. Can I seek monetary compensation from the government for harm caused by its actions?**
Yes. Article 55 establishes that citizens have the right to be indemnified for injuries to their property or rights resulting from either normal or abnormal functioning of the public administration. The claim is processed administratively under the LPA's common procedure. Compensable damages include patrimonial, physical, and moral damages, both consequential losses and lost profits. If the government does not resolve the claim within 60 days, the indemnification is deemed denied, and the citizen may proceed to court.

**6. What protections exist in administrative sanctioning proceedings?**
The law provides strong procedural protections: the principle of legality (sanctions must be authorized by statute), typicality (only legally defined infractions and sanctions apply), presumption of innocence, proportionality (the sanction must match the gravity of the infraction), non bis in idem (no double jeopardy), and the right to a hearing. Sanctions imposed through administrative proceedings are not enforceable while an appeal is pending. An infractor who voluntarily acknowledges responsibility may receive a reduced sanction of up to one-quarter off.

**7. Does the government have to explain why it made a particular decision?**
Yes. Article 23 requires that all administrative acts imposing obligations, denying or limiting rights, resolving appeals, departing from prior criteria, or exercising discretionary authority must contain a statement of the relevant facts and legal grounds (motivation). An unmotivated act in these categories is defective and challengeable.

**8. Can I interact with government agencies electronically?**
Yes. The law encourages and mandates the progressive adoption of information and communication technologies. Articles 18-22 establish the right to interact electronically with the administration, the validity of electronically issued documents, interinstitutional data exchange, and the use of electronic signatures and authentication mechanisms. Certifications and records may be issued and transmitted electronically.
