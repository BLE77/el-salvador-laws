# Medical Malpractice

Medical malpractice in El Salvador is addressed through the **Penal Code (Codigo Penal), Decreto 1030**, which covers negligent injuries and death by professionals, and the **Health Code (Codigo de Salud), Decreto 955**, which regulates the practice of medicine.

## Legal Framework

There is no standalone medical malpractice law in El Salvador. Instead, claims are pursued through:

- **Criminal law:** The Penal Code penalizes negligent injury (lesiones culposas) and negligent homicide (homicidio culposo) committed by professionals, including doctors, surgeons, nurses, and other healthcare providers.
- **Civil law:** Patients or their families can file civil lawsuits for **damages (danos y perjuicios)** resulting from medical negligence.
- **Administrative proceedings:** Complaints can be filed with the **Consejo Superior de Salud Publica (CSSP)**, the regulatory body overseeing health professionals, which can impose disciplinary sanctions.

## Elements of a Malpractice Claim

To succeed in a medical malpractice case, the patient must prove:

1. **Duty of care:** A professional relationship existed between the doctor and patient.
2. **Breach of duty:** The healthcare provider failed to meet the accepted standard of medical care.
3. **Causation:** The breach directly caused the patient's harm.
4. **Damages:** The patient suffered measurable injury, such as physical harm, additional medical costs, lost income, or pain and suffering.

## How to Pursue a Claim

- **Criminal complaint:** File a denuncia at the Fiscalia General de la Republica (FGR) for negligent injury or death.
- **Civil lawsuit:** File a demand for damages in the civil courts, with the assistance of an attorney.
- **Administrative complaint:** Report the provider to the CSSP, which can suspend or revoke medical licenses.

## Practical Considerations

- Obtain and preserve all **medical records** as early as possible.
- Expert medical testimony is typically required to establish the standard of care and prove breach.
- Both public hospital and private clinic practitioners can be held liable.
