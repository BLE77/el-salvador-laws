# Decreto 671 (1970) --- Commercial Code

## Summary
Decree 671 of 1970 constitutes the Commercial Code (Código de Comercio) of El Salvador, establishing the legal framework governing merchants, acts of commerce, and commercial goods. Enacted on December 23, 1970, and effective March 1, 1971, the code modernized commercial law by codifying rules for commercial entities, contracts, negotiable instruments, and bankruptcy proceedings. It defines who qualifies as a merchant, mandates registration in the Commercial Registry, and distinguishes between commercial and civil obligations. The code remains the foundational statute for commercial litigation and business regulation in El Salvador.

## Key Provisions
- **Article 1**: Establishes the scope of the code, stating that merchants, acts of commerce, and commercial goods shall be governed by this code and other commercial laws.
- **Articles 2–5**: Define who qualifies as a merchant (comerciante), including individuals and legal entities habitually engaging in commercial acts as their principal occupation.
- **Articles 7–22**: Enumerate specific acts of commerce (actos de comercio), distinguishing between absolute and relative commercial acts for determining jurisdiction and applicable law.
- **Article 24**: Mandates that all merchants must register in the Commercial Registry (Registro Mercantil) and maintain proper commercial books (libros de comercio) as a condition of operating legally.
- **Book II (Various Articles)**: Governs commercial companies (sociedades comerciales), including general partnerships, limited liability companies, corporations (sociedades anónimas), and their formation, governance, and dissolution.
- **Book III (Various Articles)**: Addresses commercial obligations and contracts, including sales, commercial agency, factoring, and other transactions subject to stricter commercial standards than civil contracts.
- **Articles on Negotiable Instruments**: Regulate bills of exchange, promissory notes, and checks, establishing rules for endorsement, acceptance, and protest of these instruments.
- **Book IV (Various Articles)**: Establishes bankruptcy (quiebra) procedures, including declaration of insolvency, appointment of trustees, creditor priorities, and reorganization mechanisms for distressed commercial entities.

## Commercial Warranties and Defective Products (Decreto 671/1970)

Under the Commercial Code (Decreto 671/1970), commercial sellers have warranty obligations for the goods they sell:

### Hidden Defects (Vicios Ocultos)
The Commercial Code (Decreto 671) includes provisions on *vicios ocultos* (hidden defects) -- defects that were not apparent at the time of sale and that render the product unfit for its intended use or significantly diminish its value. If you buy a product (including a vehicle) from a commercial seller under Decreto 671 (1970) and it has a hidden defect, you have two remedies:
- **Accion redhibitoria**: Return the product and get a full refund (rescission of the sale)
- **Accion quanti minoris**: Keep the product but get a proportional price reduction

### Lemon Law Equivalent for Vehicles
El Salvador has no law called a "lemon law" but Decreto 671 (1970) (Commercial Code) and Decreto 776 (2005) (Consumer Protection Law) together provide protection for defective vehicle purchases:
- A car dealer who sells a defective vehicle is liable under the Commercial Code (Decreto 671/1970) for hidden defects
- The Consumer Protection Law (Decreto 776/2005) adds the right to replacement, repair, or refund for defective products
- File a complaint with the Defensoria del Consumidor (under D-776/2005) or pursue a civil commercial claim (under D-671/1970)

## Who This Affects
This decree applies to all natural and legal persons engaging in commercial activities within El Salvador, including sole proprietors, partnerships, corporations, and foreign entities operating locally. It governs relationships between merchants, their creditors, commercial warranty disputes, and judicial proceedings in commercial courts (juzgados de lo mercantil).

## Common Questions
- **Q: Is there a lemon law for cars in El Salvador?** -- A: No specific "lemon law" exists, but Decreto 671 (1970) (Commercial Code) and Decreto 776 (2005) (Consumer Protection Law) provide remedies for defective vehicles. Under D-671, hidden defects allow you to return the car (*accion redhibitoria*) or get a price reduction. Under D-776, you can demand replacement, repair, or refund from the Defensoria del Consumidor.
- **Q: Can I get a refund if I buy a defective car from a dealer?** -- A: Yes. Under Decreto 671 (1970) for hidden defects (*vicios ocultos*) and Decreto 776 (2005) for consumer protection, you have the right to a remedy. Start with a formal written complaint to the dealer; if unresolved, file with the Defensoria del Consumidor under D-776/2005 or pursue a commercial lawsuit under D-671/1970.
