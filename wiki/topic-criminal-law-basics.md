# Criminal Law Basics in El Salvador

## Summary

Criminal law in El Salvador is governed primarily by the Penal Code (Decreto 1030/1997) and the Criminal Procedure Code (Decreto 733/2008). The Penal Code defines all crimes and their punishments. The Criminal Procedure Code establishes how crimes are investigated, prosecuted, and tried. Additional special laws cover specific areas such as drugs (Decreto 153/2003), weapons (Ley de Control de Armas, Municiones, Explosivos y Articulos Similares), money laundering (Ley Contra el Lavado de Dinero y de Activos), and domestic violence (Ley Contra la Violencia Intrafamiliar). Since 2022, El Salvador has also operated under a State of Exception regime that suspends certain constitutional rights.

Criminal responsibility begins at age 18 (Art. 17, D-1030/1997). Minors between 12 and 17 are subject to a separate juvenile justice regime. The maximum prison sentence is 60 years (Art. 71, D-1030/1997). All persons are presumed innocent until proven guilty by a final conviction (Art. 2, D-733/2008).

## Common Crimes and Penalties

The following penalties are from the Penal Code (Decreto 1030/1997). Penalties shown are the statutory ranges; judges impose specific sentences within these ranges based on the circumstances of each case.

### Theft (Hurto) -- Art. 207

Taking someone else's movable property with the intent to profit, without violence or intimidation.

| Type | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Simple theft (*hurto simple*) | 2-5 years in prison |
| Aggravated theft (*hurto agravado*, Art. 208) -- theft from a house, vehicle, workplace, or taking items of historical/scientific value, or committed by an employee or domestic worker | 3-6 years in prison |
| Petty theft -- value does not exceed two minimum monthly wages | Reduced penalty; may qualify for alternative penalties |

### Robbery (Robo) -- Art. 212

Theft committed using violence against a person or intimidation (threats). This is the critical distinction from hurto: robbery involves force or fear.

| Type | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Simple robbery (*robo*) | 6-10 years in prison |
| Aggravated robbery (*robo agravado*, Art. 213) -- using weapons, committed by two or more persons, targeting a vehicle, or with significant injury to the victim | 8-13 years in prison |

### Assault / Bodily Injury (Lesiones) -- Arts. 142-147

Causing physical injury to another person. The penalty depends on the severity of the injury, measured by the number of days the victim is incapacitated.

| Severity | Incapacity Period | Penalty |
|---|---|---|
| Minor injuries (*lesiones leves*) | Up to 10 days | Fine or alternative penalty |
| Injuries (*lesiones*) | 5-20 days | 1-3 years in prison |
| Aggravated injuries (*lesiones graves*) | Over 20 days, or permanent damage (loss of limb, organ, sense) | 3-6 years in prison |
| Very serious injuries (*lesiones muy graves*) | Permanent severe disability | 4-8 years in prison |

### Threats (Amenazas) -- Art. 154

Threatening another person with causing harm to them, their family, or their property.

| Type | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Simple threats | 1-3 years in prison |
| Threats with a weapon | 2-4 years in prison |
| Threats against a public official | Increased penalty |

### Fraud / Swindle (Estafa) -- Art. 215

Deceiving another person to obtain money, property, or an unlawful economic advantage. This covers scams, confidence tricks, false pretenses, and similar schemes.

| Type | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Simple fraud (*estafa*) | 2-5 years in prison |
| Aggravated fraud -- large amounts, targeting vulnerable persons, or exploiting a position of trust | 3-7 years in prison |

### Money Laundering (Lavado de Dinero y Activos)

Governed by the Ley Contra el Lavado de Dinero y de Activos. Money laundering means concealing, disguising, or integrating assets derived from criminal activity (drug trafficking, corruption, fraud, extortion, etc.) into the legitimate financial system.

| Type | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Money laundering | 5-15 years in prison |
| Aggravated money laundering -- committed by a public official, financial institution employee, or involving large amounts | Increased penalties within the range |
| Failure to report suspicious transactions (for obligated entities such as banks, notaries, accountants) | Administrative and criminal penalties |

### Domestic Violence (Violencia Intrafamiliar)

Governed by the Ley Contra la Violencia Intrafamiliar (Decreto 902/1996) and the Penal Code. Domestic violence includes physical, psychological, sexual, and economic abuse within a family or household.

| Type | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Domestic violence (*violencia intrafamiliar*, Art. 200 CP) | 1-3 years in prison |
| Aggravated domestic violence -- habitual abuse, abuse of a minor, pregnant woman, elderly person, or person with disabilities | 2-4 years in prison; may increase to 4-8 years |
| Femicide (*feminicidio*, Art. 45 LEIV) -- killing a woman motivated by gender hatred or contempt, within a relationship of power | 20-35 years in prison (Ley Especial Integral para una Vida Libre de Violencia para las Mujeres, D-520/2010) |

Victims of domestic violence can seek protective orders (*medidas de proteccion*) through family courts or the PNC (National Civil Police), even without filing criminal charges.

### DUI -- Driving Under the Influence

Driving under the influence of alcohol or drugs is addressed in both the Penal Code and traffic regulations.

| Type | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Driving under the influence (*conduccion temeraria*, Art. 147-E CP) -- driving in a way that endangers others while intoxicated | 1-3 years in prison, plus suspension of driving license |
| Causing injury while DUI | Penalty for the injuries caused, plus aggravating circumstance for intoxication |
| Causing death while DUI | Homicide penalties apply, with intoxication as an aggravating factor; 6-12 years or more |

The legal blood alcohol limit in El Salvador is **0.05 g/dL (0.05%)**. Police can administer breathalyzer tests at checkpoints. Refusing a breathalyzer test can result in administrative penalties including license suspension.

### Weapons Offenses

Weapons are regulated by the Ley de Control de Armas, Municiones, Explosivos y Articulos Similares and related provisions of the Penal Code.

| Offense | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Illegal possession of a firearm (*tenencia ilegal de armas de fuego*) -- possessing a firearm without the required license/registration | 3-5 years in prison |
| Illegal carrying of a firearm (*portacion ilegal de armas*) -- carrying a registered firearm without a carry permit, or carrying in a prohibited area | 3-5 years in prison |
| Possession of a war weapon or restricted firearm (automatic weapons, grenades, etc.) | 5-10 years in prison |
| Manufacturing, trafficking, or selling illegal weapons | 5-15 years in prison |
| Carrying a knife or bladed weapon in a threatening manner or in prohibited areas | Administrative sanctions or criminal penalties depending on circumstances |

See the "Weapons Laws" section below for details on legal ownership and carrying rules.

### Other Notable Crimes and Penalties

| Crime | Article (D-1030/1997) | Penalty |
|---|---|---|
| Simple homicide (*homicidio simple*) | Art. 128 | 10-20 years |
| Aggravated homicide | Art. 129 | 30-50 years |
| Rape (*violacion*) | Art. 158 | 6-10 years |
| Rape of a minor under 15 | Art. 159 | 14-20 years |
| Sexual harassment (*acoso sexual*) | Art. 165 | 3-5 years (4-8 years if victim under 15) |
| Kidnapping (*secuestro*) | Art. 149 | 5-10 years (30-45 if ransom demanded) |
| Extortion (*extorsion*) | Art. 214 | 10-15 years |
| Gang membership (*asociaciones ilicitas*) | Art. 345 | 8-12 years |
| Tax evasion (*evasion de impuestos*) | Art. 249-A | 4-6 years |
| Environmental contamination | Art. 255 | 4-8 years |
| Embezzlement by public official (*peculado*) | Art. 335 | Prison + disqualification |

## Criminal Procedure: How the System Works

The Criminal Procedure Code (Decreto 733/2008) governs how crimes are investigated and prosecuted.

### Filing a Criminal Complaint (Denuncia)

Anyone can report a crime. There are several ways to do it:

1. **Go to the PNC (National Civil Police)**: Visit any police station and file a report (*denuncia*). The police are obligated to receive it. You do not need a lawyer. The report can be oral or written.

2. **Go to the Fiscalia General de la Republica (Attorney General's Office)**: File the complaint directly with prosecutors. This is the entity that ultimately decides whether to prosecute.

3. **Call 911**: For emergencies or crimes in progress.

4. **File at a Juzgado de Paz (Justice of the Peace court)**: In smaller municipalities, the local judge can receive complaints.

**Important distinctions:**
- **Public action crimes** (most serious crimes -- homicide, robbery, rape, extortion, drug trafficking, etc.): The state prosecutes these automatically. Anyone can report them, and the Fiscalia must investigate. The victim does not need to "press charges" -- the state acts on its own.
- **Crimes requiring a private complaint (*delitos de accion publica previa instancia particular*)**: Some crimes like minor injuries, threats, or trespassing require the victim to formally complain before the state can prosecute. If the victim does not complain, the case does not move forward.
- **Private action crimes (*delitos de accion privada*)**: A few minor offenses (certain defamation cases, minor property damage) are pursued only by the victim through a private complaint directly to a court.

### How a Criminal Case Proceeds

1. **Investigation Phase (*Instruccion/Investigacion*)**: The Fiscalia, assisted by the PNC, investigates the crime. They collect evidence, interview witnesses, and identify suspects. The investigation may last up to 4 months for detained suspects.

2. **Charges (*Requerimiento Fiscal*)**: If the suspect is detained, the Fiscalia must file formal charges within 72 hours (Art. 268). If the suspect is not detained, there is more time, but the Fiscalia must act within established deadlines.

3. **Preliminary Hearing (*Audiencia Inicial*)**: A Juez de Paz reviews the charges and evidence to determine if there is enough basis to proceed. The judge decides on pretrial detention or release on bail/conditions.

4. **Instruction Phase (*Instruccion*)**: A Juez de Instruccion supervises the formal investigation. Both sides present evidence and arguments. This phase typically lasts 6 months (extendable to 12 in complex cases).

5. **Trial (*Vista Publica*)**: A Tribunal de Sentencia (sentencing court, usually a panel of judges) hears the case. The trial is oral and public. Witnesses testify, evidence is presented, and both sides give closing arguments. The defense always speaks last (Art. 392).

6. **Verdict and Sentencing**: The court issues a verdict. If guilty, the sentence is imposed within the statutory range for the crime.

7. **Appeals**: The convicted person can appeal (*apelacion*) to a Camara de lo Penal (criminal appellate court). Further appeal by *casacion* to the Sala de lo Penal of the Supreme Court is available for legal errors (Art. 452).

### Rights of the Accused

The Constitution and D-733/2008 guarantee the following rights:

- **Presumption of innocence** (Art. 2, D-733/2008) -- you are innocent until proven guilty by a final conviction
- **Right to a lawyer** (Art. 4) -- from the moment of detention; if you cannot afford one, the state must provide a public defender
- **Right to remain silent** -- you cannot be compelled to testify against yourself
- **Right to know the charges** against you
- **Right to present evidence** and call witnesses in your defense
- **Right to a public trial** conducted orally
- **Right to an interpreter** if you do not speak Spanish
- **No double jeopardy** -- you cannot be tried twice for the same crime
- **Habeas corpus** -- anyone who believes they are illegally detained can petition the Constitutional Chamber of the Supreme Court
- **Limits on pretrial detention** -- up to 12 months for less serious crimes, 24 months for serious crimes (Art. 8)

### Abbreviated Procedure (Plea Bargain)

El Salvador has an abbreviated procedure (*procedimiento abreviado*, Art. 419, D-733/2008) that functions similarly to a plea bargain. The prosecutor and defense agree on a reduced sentence. The accused must freely consent after understanding the consequences. The judge must approve the agreement.

## State of Exception (Regimen de Excepcion)

### What It Is

A State of Exception is a constitutional mechanism (Art. 29-31, Constitution of El Salvador) that allows the government to temporarily suspend certain constitutional rights when the country faces war, public calamity, or serious disturbances to public order. The Legislative Assembly (Asamblea Legislativa) must approve the declaration, which is valid for 30 days and can be renewed.

Since March 2022, El Salvador has maintained an ongoing State of Exception in response to a spike in gang-related violence. It has been renewed repeatedly by the Legislative Assembly.

### Suspended Rights

During the State of Exception, the following rights are suspended:

1. **Freedom of association** (Art. 7 Constitution) -- gatherings and associations can be restricted
2. **Right to be informed of the reasons for arrest** (Art. 12 Constitution) -- police are not required to immediately explain why you are being detained
3. **Right to legal defense from the moment of detention** -- the mandatory appointment of a lawyer upon detention is relaxed
4. **Privacy of correspondence and telecommunications** (Art. 24 Constitution) -- authorities can intercept communications without a warrant

### Detention Rules Under the State of Exception

- Police can arrest and detain suspects without an arrest warrant
- The normal 72-hour deadline for the Fiscalia to file charges is extended to 15 days
- Pretrial detention limits are extended
- Minors aged 12-17 can be prosecuted as adults for certain gang-related crimes
- Large-scale arrests are conducted in communities identified as having gang presence
- Tens of thousands of people have been detained under this regime since 2022

### Important Notes

- The State of Exception does **not** suspend all rights. The right to life, the prohibition on torture, and the right to a fair trial (eventually) remain in force.
- Habeas corpus petitions remain legally available, though in practice their effectiveness has been debated.
- The State of Exception is a temporary measure that requires periodic legislative renewal. It has been renewed continuously since March 2022.
- International human rights organizations have raised concerns about due process under the State of Exception.

## Self-Defense (Legitima Defensa)

Self-defense is a complete defense under Salvadoran law. If self-defense is proven, the person is not criminally responsible for the harm caused.

### Requirements (Art. 27, numeral 2, D-1030/1997)

To claim legitimate self-defense, all of the following must be present:

1. **Unlawful aggression** (*agresion ilegitima*) -- someone is attacking or about to attack you, and the attack is illegal (you cannot claim self-defense against a lawful police arrest)
2. **Reasonable necessity of the means used** (*necesidad razonable del medio empleado*) -- the force you use must be proportional to the threat. You cannot shoot someone who slaps you.
3. **No sufficient provocation by the defender** (*falta de provocacion suficiente*) -- you cannot start a fight and then claim self-defense

### Key Points

- Self-defense applies to protecting yourself, other people, or your property
- You can use deadly force only when facing a deadly threat
- There is no explicit "duty to retreat" in Salvadoran law, but using force when you could safely avoid the confrontation may undermine the "reasonable necessity" requirement
- Defense of your home (*defensa del domicilio*) has somewhat broader protections -- an intruder entering your home at night may be treated as presenting an inherently threatening situation
- Excessive self-defense (*exceso en la defensa*) -- using disproportionate force -- is still a crime, but carries a reduced penalty
- The burden of proof is on the person claiming self-defense to show the requirements were met

## Weapons Laws

### Firearms

Firearms are regulated by the Ley de Control de Armas, Municiones, Explosivos y Articulos Similares, administered by the Ministry of National Defense.

**Legal ownership requires:**
- Be at least 18 years old (21 for some categories)
- Obtain a firearms license (*matricula de arma*) from the Ministry of Defense
- Pass a background check (no criminal record for violent crimes)
- Pass a psychological evaluation
- Complete a firearms safety course
- Register each firearm individually

**Types of permits:**
- **Tenencia (possession)**: Allows keeping a firearm at home or business. Does not authorize carrying it in public.
- **Portacion (carry)**: Allows carrying a registered firearm on your person in public. Requires a separate carry permit, which is harder to obtain and has additional requirements.

**Prohibited weapons:**
- Automatic firearms (machine guns)
- Military weapons (grenades, rocket launchers, etc.)
- Silencers (without special authorization)
- Modified weapons (sawed-off shotguns, etc.)

**Prohibited places for carrying firearms (even with a permit):**
- Government buildings
- Schools and universities
- Hospitals
- Sports stadiums
- Bars and nightclubs
- Public demonstrations
- Polling stations during elections

### Knives and Bladed Weapons

There is no single comprehensive "knife law" in El Salvador. The rules are scattered:

- **Carrying a knife as a tool** (for work, cooking, etc.) is generally legal
- **Carrying a large knife, machete, or bladed weapon in an urban area** without a legitimate purpose can be treated as carrying a weapon and may result in arrest, particularly under the State of Exception
- **Using a knife or bladed weapon in the commission of a crime** (robbery, assault) is an aggravating factor that increases the penalty
- **Switchblades and concealed blades** are treated more strictly than ordinary knives
- During the State of Exception, police have broad discretion to detain anyone carrying weapons of any kind in suspicious circumstances

### Penalties Summary

| Offense | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Possessing an unregistered firearm | 3-5 years in prison |
| Carrying a firearm without a carry permit | 3-5 years in prison |
| Possessing a military/restricted weapon | 5-10 years in prison |
| Selling/trafficking illegal weapons | 5-15 years in prison |
| Using a weapon in the commission of another crime | Aggravating factor; increases penalty for the underlying crime |

## Common Penalties and Prison Terms

### Types of Penalties (Art. 45, D-1030/1997)

**Principal penalties:**
- **Prison** (*prision*): 6 months to 60 years
- **Weekend arrest** (*arresto de fin de semana*): 4-150 weekends
- **House arrest** (*arresto domiciliario*): 1-30 days
- **Fines** (*multa*): 5-360 day-fines (a day-fine is based on the offender's daily income)
- **Community service** (*prestacion de trabajo de utilidad publica*): 4-150 weekly sessions

**Accessory penalties (Art. 46):**
- Absolute disqualification from public office
- Special disqualification (loss of a specific right, such as driving)
- Expulsion from the country (for foreigners)
- Loss of driving privileges

### Alternatives to Prison

- **Conditional suspension of sentence** (Art. 77): For prison sentences of 3 years or less, the judge may suspend the sentence and impose a probation period of 2-5 years. If the person complies with conditions, the sentence is discharged.
- **Replacement of short sentences** (Art. 74): Prison sentences of 6 months to 1 year may be replaced with weekend arrest, community service, or fines.
- **Conditional release / parole** (Art. 86): After serving two-thirds of the sentence, a prisoner with good behavior may be released on conditions.

## Drinking Age and Alcohol Rules

- The **legal drinking age** in El Salvador is **18 years old**
- It is illegal to sell or serve alcohol to persons under 18
- Selling alcohol to minors can result in administrative sanctions and fines for the establishment
- Public intoxication can result in administrative detention
- The **legal blood alcohol limit for driving** is **0.05 g/dL**
- Bars and liquor stores are subject to licensing and operating hour restrictions set by municipal ordinances

## Drug Penalties (Reference: Decreto 153/2003)

Drug offenses are governed by the Drug Regulation Law (Decreto 153/2003). Key penalties:

| Offense | Penalty | Article |
|---|---|---|
| Possession of less than 2 grams | 1-3 years in prison | Art. 34 |
| Possession of 2 grams or more | 3-6 years in prison | Art. 34 |
| Possession with intent to traffic | 6-10 years in prison | Art. 34 |
| Drug trafficking | 10-15 years in prison + fine | Art. 33 |
| Promoting/encouraging cultivation or trafficking | 8-12 years in prison | Art. 35 |
| Administering drugs to another person without medical purpose | 3-6 years in prison | Art. 41 |
| Administering drugs to a minor or without consent | 6-8 years in prison | Art. 41 |

**Aggravating factors** (Art. 54) that increase penalties: involving minors, committing offenses in schools or military facilities, being a law enforcement officer, using public office, or acting as part of organized crime.

Alcohol and tobacco are **not** covered by this law -- they are regulated separately (Art. 2, D-153/2003).

For full details, see the [Drug Regulation Law wiki page](decreto-153-2003-drug-regulation-law.md).

## Common Questions

### 1. How do I press charges / report a crime in El Salvador?
Go to any PNC (police) station or directly to the Fiscalia General de la Republica (Attorney General's Office) and file a *denuncia* (complaint). You can also call 911 for emergencies. You do not need a lawyer to file a report. For most serious crimes (homicide, robbery, rape, drug trafficking), the state prosecutes automatically -- you do not need to "press charges" in the American sense. For some lesser crimes (minor injuries, threats, trespassing), the victim must file a formal complaint before the state can prosecute.

### 2. Can I own a gun in El Salvador?
Yes, but you must obtain a firearms license (*matricula de arma*) from the Ministry of National Defense. You must be at least 18, pass a background check and psychological evaluation, and complete a firearms safety course. Each firearm must be registered. A separate carry permit (*portacion*) is required to carry a gun in public. Possessing an unregistered firearm is a crime punishable by 3-5 years in prison.

### 3. What is the State of Exception and how does it affect me?
The State of Exception (*regimen de excepcion*) is a constitutional emergency measure that suspends certain rights, including freedom of association, the right to be informed of the reasons for arrest, the right to a lawyer immediately upon detention, and privacy of communications. It has been in effect continuously since March 2022. Under the State of Exception, police can arrest suspects without warrants, and the deadline for filing charges is extended from 72 hours to 15 days. Tens of thousands of people have been detained under this regime.

### 4. What happens if I get caught with drugs?
Possession of less than 2 grams carries 1-3 years in prison. Two grams or more carries 3-6 years. If there is evidence of intent to sell or distribute, the penalty is 6-10 years. Drug trafficking carries 10-15 years plus fines. There is no legal exception for personal use -- any amount of an illicit drug can result in criminal prosecution (Art. 34, D-153/2003).

### 5. Can I defend myself if someone attacks me?
Yes. Salvadoran law recognizes legitimate self-defense (*legitima defensa*, Art. 27, D-1030/1997). You must show that: (1) the aggression was unlawful, (2) the force you used was reasonably proportional to the threat, and (3) you did not provoke the attack. If all three requirements are met, you are not criminally responsible. Using excessive force (more than necessary) is still a crime, but with a reduced penalty.

### 6. What is the legal drinking age?
The legal drinking age in El Salvador is 18. Selling or serving alcohol to anyone under 18 is illegal and can result in sanctions for the establishment.

### 7. What is the penalty for DUI?
Driving under the influence of alcohol (above 0.05 g/dL blood alcohol) or drugs is punishable by 1-3 years in prison and suspension of your driving license. If you cause injury or death while DUI, the penalties are significantly higher -- you face the penalties for the injuries or homicide caused, with intoxication as an aggravating factor.

### 8. Is carrying a knife illegal?
There is no blanket ban on carrying knives. Carrying a knife for a legitimate purpose (work, cooking, agriculture) is generally legal. However, carrying a large knife or bladed weapon in an urban area without a clear legitimate purpose can lead to trouble, especially during the State of Exception when police have broad discretion. Using a knife in the commission of a crime is an aggravating factor.

### 9. What is money laundering and what is the penalty?
Money laundering (*lavado de dinero*) means concealing or disguising the origin of money or assets derived from criminal activity. The penalty is 5-15 years in prison. Banks, notaries, accountants, real estate agents, and other professionals have legal obligations to report suspicious transactions. Failing to report can itself result in criminal and administrative penalties.

### 10. How long can the police hold me without charges?
Under normal circumstances, the Fiscalia must file formal charges within 72 hours of detention (Art. 268, D-733/2008). Under the State of Exception, this deadline is extended to 15 days. Pretrial detention (while awaiting trial) is limited to 12 months for less serious crimes and 24 months for serious crimes, though these limits can be extended in complex proceedings (Art. 8, D-733/2008).

### 11. What is the maximum prison sentence in El Salvador?
60 years. This is the absolute cap even when multiple sentences are combined (Art. 71, D-1030/1997). There is no death penalty and no life sentence.

### 12. Can I get bail?
Yes, in many cases. The judge at the preliminary hearing (*audiencia inicial*) decides whether to impose pretrial detention or release the accused on bail or alternative measures (reporting obligations, travel restrictions, etc.). However, for serious crimes (homicide, aggravated robbery, rape, kidnapping, drug trafficking, extortion, gang-related offenses), pretrial detention is the norm. Under the State of Exception, bail is rarely granted.

### 13. What are my rights if I am arrested?
Under normal circumstances: you have the right to know why you are being arrested, the right to a lawyer immediately (the state must provide one if you cannot afford one), the right to remain silent, the right to notify a family member, the right not to be tortured or mistreated, and the right to be brought before a judge within 72 hours. Under the State of Exception, some of these rights (particularly the right to be immediately informed of the reason for arrest and the right to a lawyer upon detention) are suspended.

### 14. What is the penalty for domestic violence?
Domestic violence carries 1-3 years in prison, increasing to 2-4 years for aggravated cases (habitual abuse, abuse of minors, pregnant women, elderly, or disabled persons). Femicide -- killing a woman motivated by gender-based hatred or contempt -- carries 20-35 years. Victims can obtain protective orders (*medidas de proteccion*) through family courts or the PNC, even without filing criminal charges.

### 15. Do crimes have a statute of limitations?
Yes, most crimes do. The statute of limitations (*prescripcion*) varies depending on the severity of the crime and is generally tied to the maximum penalty. However, certain crimes never expire: torture, terrorism, kidnapping, genocide, war crimes, forced disappearance, political persecution, and sexual crimes against minors (Art. 99, D-1030/1997).
