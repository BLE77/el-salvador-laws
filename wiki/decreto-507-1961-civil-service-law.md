# Decreto 507 (1961) — Civil Service Law

## Summary

The Civil Service Law (Ley de Servicio Civil) is the foundational statute governing the employment relationship between the State of El Salvador (including municipalities) and its public servants. Originally enacted in 1961 by the Civic-Military Directorate, it has been amended numerous times to modernize labor protections for government workers. The law establishes the administrative career system (carrera administrativa), regulates hiring, promotions, transfers, disciplinary sanctions, dismissal procedures, and creates specialized enforcement bodies -- the Civil Service Commissions and the Civil Service Tribunal. Major amendments have added chapters on collective labor rights, including the right to form unions and negotiate collective bargaining agreements in the public sector, as well as economic benefits for voluntary resignation and enhanced job stability protections for pregnant workers and employees with chronic illnesses.

The law applies to all public servants across the Presidency, Ministries, the Legislative Assembly, the Judiciary, independent organs, departmental governorships, and municipalities, with specific exclusions for elected officials, high-ranking appointees, military and police personnel, and certain other categories.

## Key Provisions

### Article 1 — Name and Purpose
The statute is officially named the "Civil Service Law." Its special purpose is to regulate the relationship between the State and municipalities with their public servants, guarantee the protection and stability of those servants, ensure efficiency of public institutions, and organize the administrative career through selection and promotion based on merit and aptitude.

### Article 2 — Scope of Application
The law covers public servants in all public institutions: the Presidency, Ministries, Legislative Assembly, Judiciary, independent organs, departmental governorships, and municipalities. Teachers and foreign service members are governed by special laws but retain the social rights contained in this statute.

### Article 4 — Exclusions from the Administrative Career
The following are excluded from the administrative career: elected officials; Ministers and Vice-Ministers; the Attorney General and Ombudsman; Presidential secretaries; departmental governors; Supreme Court justices and other senior judicial officials; members of the Civil Service Tribunal, the Supreme Electoral Tribunal, and certain other tribunals; military and police members; prison commanders and surveillance personnel; directors, sub-directors, managers, department heads, treasurers, auditors, and others who handle public funds; and persons under temporary contract. However, any person providing permanent services essential to institutional operations under contract is included in the administrative career.

### Article 5 — Scope of Exclusions
Even those excluded from the administrative career remain subject to the duties, prohibitions, and responsibilities established by the law. Their social rights are governed by Chapter XI (Collective Labor Rights).

### Articles 6-10 — Institutional Organization
The law creates Civil Service Commissions in each major government institution (one per ministry, court, municipality of a departmental capital, etc.) and a central Civil Service Tribunal. Each Commission has three principal members and three alternates serving two-year terms. The Tribunal has three members (appointed respectively by the Legislative Assembly, the Executive, and the Supreme Court) serving three-year terms, with its seat in San Salvador.

### Articles 12-13 — Powers of Commissions and Tribunal
Commissions maintain employee rosters, select eligible candidates for hiring, conduct fitness tests, handle first-instance disciplinary cases, and report to the Tribunal. The Tribunal hears appeals, resolves disputes between Commissions, rehabilitates dismissed employees, and provides advisory opinions on the law's application.

### Article 18 — Requirements for Entry into the Civil Service
Applicants must be Salvadoran (or Central American/foreigner meeting constitutional requirements), at least 18 years old (or hold a qualifying degree), pass fitness examinations, be physically capable, demonstrate good conduct, be selected from a list of eligible candidates, and complete a three-month probationary period. HIV testing may not be required of any applicant.

### Article 19 — Disqualifications from Entry
Persons convicted of intentional crimes (during the sentence period), those with outstanding arrest warrants for non-bailable offenses, those previously dismissed under this law (until rehabilitated by the Tribunal), those with contagious diseases, and habitual alcoholics may not enter the administrative career.

### Articles 20-27 — Personnel Selection and Probation
Vacancies not filled by promotion are filled through competitive fitness examinations. Commissions publish notices, administer tests, select the three best-qualified candidates, and present the shortlist to the appointing authority. A three-month probationary period applies; unsatisfactory employees may be removed during this period without formality. After successful probation, the employee gains full protection under the law.

### Article 29 — Rights of Public Servants
Protected employees enjoy: permanence in their position (no dismissal, suspension, transfer, or demotion except as provided by law); the right to promotion; full salary and benefits; holidays, vacations, and leave; Christmas bonuses; access to service records; retirement and pension rights under special laws; funeral expenses; the right to participate in collective bargaining through unions; the right to unionize; economic benefits for voluntary resignation; extended job stability during pregnancy; and protection from dismissal for employees with chronic disabling illnesses requiring medical treatment.

### Article 29-A — Extended Maternity Protection
Pregnant women enjoy extended job stability from the moment of conception until six months after postnatal leave ends. Any agreement to waive this right is void. Employers who violate this protection face fines of three to six monthly minimum wages.

### Articles 30, 30-A through 30-F — Severance and Voluntary Resignation Benefits
When a position is eliminated, employees receive severance equal to one month's salary per year of service, subject to caps based on salary level. Employees who resign voluntarily receive an economic benefit equal to fifteen days of basic salary per year of service (proportional for partial years), capped at twice the daily legal minimum wage. Resignations must be submitted by the last business day of August for the following fiscal year's budget to cover the payment. This benefit may only be claimed once; a five-year waiting period applies before claiming it again after re-entering public service. The resignation benefit is exempt from income tax.

### Articles 31-32 — Duties and Prohibitions
Employees must attend punctually, perform duties with diligence and probity, maintain confidentiality, reject bribes, respect superiors, serve the public courteously, and treat colleagues properly. Prohibited conduct includes: imposing politically motivated sanctions on subordinates, soliciting political declarations, collecting political contributions, displaying party insignia at work, holding incompatible private employment, discriminating against unionized workers, using violence to force union membership, obstructing union formation, refusing to accept employee resignations, and assigning physically incompatible work to pregnant women or chronically ill employees.

### Articles 33-40 — Promotions, Transfers, and Demotions
Positions are filled by promotion from the immediately lower class after at least two years of service. If multiple candidates exist, competitive examination among eligible employees is required. Employees may be transferred to equivalent positions; transfers to a different locality require consent or Commission authorization. Demotion is only permitted upon proof of negligence or misconduct, with appeal rights to the Tribunal.

### Articles 41-51 — Disciplinary Regime
Sanctions range in severity: (a) private oral warning, (b) written warning, (c) fine (up to 10% of monthly salary), (d) suspension without pay (up to one month), (e) postponement of promotion for up to two years, (f) reduction in rank, and (g) dismissal or removal. Warnings may be imposed by supervisors directly. Fines and suspensions require Commission proceedings with a four-day evidentiary period and are appealable to the Tribunal within three business days. All sanctions may be applied based on the moral strength of evidence.

### Articles 52-63 — Dismissal and Removal
Employees in the administrative career may only be dismissed or removed for causes specified in the law and through prescribed procedures. Causes for dismissal include: repeated serious failure of duties, a third suspension within one year, unauthorized private employment, manifest lack of fitness, criminal conviction, using one's position for electioneering, and declaring a strike. Causes for removal include: grave breach of duties (confidentiality, probity, etc.), violating prohibitions, gross immorality, intoxication at work, malicious property damage, criminal conviction in the exercise of functions, and abandonment of position (presumed after eight consecutive days of unjustified absence). The procedure requires written notice to the Commission, a three-day response period for the employee, an eight-day evidentiary phase, and a decision. Appeals go to the Tribunal, which decides within three days. Dismissals without cause or without following proper procedures are void; the Tribunal can order reinstatement and back pay for up to three months.

### Articles 73-101 — Union Rights (Chapter XI)
Public servants have the right to freely form unions with at least 35 members from the same institution. Unions must register with the Ministry of Labor. Union activities include collective bargaining, monitoring legal compliance, representing members, creating cooperative and social welfare institutions, and acquiring property. Unions must remain independent from political parties. Governance is through assemblies and a board of directors (3 to 11 members). Sanctions for union infractions include fines, suspension of union activities (1 to 6 months), and dissolution.

### Articles 102-160 — Collective Bargaining and Dispute Resolution
Public sector unions may negotiate collective bargaining agreements covering general working conditions, career development, social benefits, and workplace safety. Agreements require approval by the union assembly and are binding for three years. Collective disputes of a legal nature are heard by Labor Chambers in San Salvador (for disputes against the State) or by labor judges in the relevant jurisdiction (for disputes against municipalities). Economic disputes follow a structured process: direct negotiation, conciliation (up to 30 business days), and arbitration if conciliation fails. Arbitral awards are binding and enforceable.

## Who This Affects

- **All government employees** in the executive, legislative, and judicial branches, as well as independent institutions and municipalities, who are part of the administrative career
- **Municipal workers** in all 262 municipalities of El Salvador
- **Heads of public institutions and supervisors** who must follow prescribed hiring, disciplinary, and dismissal procedures
- **Pregnant public employees** who receive extended job stability protections
- **Employees with chronic disabling illnesses** who are protected from dismissal during treatment
- **Public sector unions** formed under this law and their members
- **Job applicants** seeking to enter the civil service through competitive examination
- **Excluded officials** (elected officials, ministers, judges, military, police, senior directors) who are still bound by the law's duties and prohibitions even though they are outside the administrative career
- **The Civil Service Tribunal and Commissions** as the specialized bodies responsible for enforcement

## Related Laws

- **Constitution of El Salvador (1983)** — Articles 218-222 establish the constitutional basis for the civil service and administrative career
- **Decreto 15 (1972) — Labor Code (Codigo de Trabajo)** — General labor law that applies to private sector workers and fills gaps in public sector regulation
- **Ley de la Jurisdiccion Contencioso Administrativa** — Governs administrative litigation that may arise from civil service disputes
- **Ley General de Prevencion de Riesgos en los Lugares de Trabajo** — Workplace safety law referenced for protections of chronically ill employees (Arts. 64, 82)
- **Disposiciones Generales de Presupuestos** — Budget law referenced for contract employees (Art. 83) and overtime calculations
- **ILO Conventions 87, 98, 135, and 151** — International labor conventions on freedom of association, collective bargaining, and workers' representatives, whose ratification triggered the collective rights chapter
- **Ley de Salarios** — Determines salary scales and per diem compensation for Tribunal members
- **Ley de la Corte de Cuentas** — Audit court law relevant to employees handling public funds
- **Penal Code (Decreto 1030, 1997)** — Referenced for criminal sanctions against officials who fail to comply with Tribunal orders

## Common Questions

### 1. Can a government employee be fired without cause in El Salvador?
No. Articles 52-55 require that employees in the administrative career may only be dismissed or removed for specific causes listed in the law (Arts. 53-54) and only after following a formal procedure before the Civil Service Commission. Dismissals without legal cause or without following the prescribed process are void, and the Civil Service Tribunal can order reinstatement plus back pay for up to three months.

### 2. What protections exist for pregnant public employees?
Article 29-A provides extended job stability from the moment of conception until six months after postnatal leave concludes. Any agreement between the employee and employer to waive this protection is void. Employers who violate this provision face fines of three to six monthly minimum wages from the commerce and services sector. The pregnant employee must still fulfill her work obligations upon returning from postnatal leave.

### 3. How does someone enter the civil service?
Entry requires meeting the qualifications in Article 18 (nationality, age, fitness test, physical capacity, good conduct), passing competitive examinations administered by the relevant Civil Service Commission, being selected from a shortlist of three candidates, and completing a three-month probationary period. After successful probation, the employee gains full legal protection. HIV testing cannot be required.

### 4. What happens when a government position is eliminated?
Under Article 30, the employee receives severance compensation equal to one month's salary per year of service (or fraction exceeding six months), subject to caps based on salary level. Payments are made in equal monthly installments. If the employee takes another public position, severance payments are suspended but may resume if the new position is later eliminated.

### 5. Can public employees form unions?
Yes. Chapter XI (added by reform) grants public servants the right to form unions with at least 35 members from the same institution. Unions must register with the Ministry of Labor and can negotiate collective bargaining agreements, represent members, and create social welfare institutions. However, military personnel, police, senior officials with decision-making power, judges, and certain other excluded categories cannot unionize.

### 6. What is the voluntary resignation benefit?
Under Articles 30-A through 30-F, employees who voluntarily resign receive an economic benefit of fifteen days of basic salary per year of service (proportional for partial years). The salary used for calculation is capped at twice the daily legal minimum wage for the commerce and services sector. Resignations must be filed by the last business day of August so the institution can budget for the payment. This benefit is exempt from income tax and may only be claimed once per five-year period.

### 7. What disciplinary sanctions can be imposed on public employees?
Article 41 establishes a graduated scale: (a) private oral warning, (b) written warning, (c) fine up to 10% of monthly salary, (d) suspension without pay up to one month, (e) postponement of promotion up to two years, (f) reduction in rank, and (g) dismissal or removal. Warnings can be imposed by direct supervisors. More serious sanctions require formal proceedings before the Civil Service Commission and are appealable to the Civil Service Tribunal within three business days.

### 8. What role does the Civil Service Tribunal play?
The Civil Service Tribunal is the central appellate and supervisory body for civil service matters. It hears appeals from Commission decisions, resolves disputes between Commissions, rehabilitates dismissed employees, provides advisory opinions on the law's application, maintains a registry of all employee records, and trains Commission members. Its three members are appointed by the Legislative Assembly, the Executive (in Council of Ministers), and the Supreme Court, each serving three-year terms.
