# Taxes in El Salvador

## Summary

El Salvador has three main taxes that affect individuals and businesses: (1) income tax (*Impuesto sobre la Renta*, ISR) governed by Decreto 134 (1991); (2) VAT/sales tax (*Impuesto a la Transferencia de Bienes Muebles y a la Prestacion de Servicios*, IVA) governed by Decreto 296 (1992); and (3) the Tax Code (*Codigo Tributario*, Decreto 230/2000) that establishes general rules for all taxes, filing deadlines, penalties, and the rights and duties of taxpayers. The tax authority is the Ministerio de Hacienda, administered through the Direccion General de Impuestos Internos (DGII).

---

## Income Tax (Decreto 134/1991 -- Ley de Impuesto sobre la Renta)

### Income Tax Rates for Individuals

El Salvador uses a **territorial income tax system** under Decreto 134 (1991): only income earned or generated within El Salvador is taxable. Income from foreign sources is generally **not** subject to Salvadoran income tax under Art. 1 and Art. 16 of D-134/1991.

**Individual income tax brackets (Art. 37, D-134/1991):**

| Annual Income | Tax Rate |
|---|---|
| Up to $4,064 | 0% (exempt) |
| $4,064.01 to $9,142.86 | 10% on the excess over $4,064 |
| $9,142.87 to $22,857.14 | $507.92 + 20% on the excess over $9,142.86 |
| Over $22,857.14 | $3,251.79 + 30% on the excess over $22,857.14 |

### Corporate Income Tax Rate

- **25%** of net taxable income for most companies
- **30%** for companies with annual taxable income exceeding $150,000

### Territorial Principle -- Foreign Income

Under Decreto 134 (1991) Art. 16: income derived from sources **outside** El Salvador is generally **not subject** to Salvadoran income tax. This means:
- Money earned from remote work for a foreign company while physically outside El Salvador is not taxable
- Dividends, interest, or royalties from foreign sources are not taxable
- **Exception**: certain categories of income may be taxable if the activity generating them is connected to El Salvador (e.g., services rendered in El Salvador for a foreign client)

**Digital nomads and remote workers**: If you work remotely for a foreign company while based in El Salvador, your income is generally not subject to Salvadoran income tax under D-134/1991.

### Deductible Business Expenses (Decreto 134/1991)

Under Art. 29 of Decreto 134 (1991), allowable deductions for businesses include:
- **Rent and utilities**: Office rent, electricity, water, and telecommunications expenses used in generating income are deductible
- Salaries, wages, social security (ISSS), and pension (AFP) contributions paid on behalf of employees
- Depreciation of business assets
- Interest on business loans
- Advertising and marketing expenses
- Professional fees (legal, accounting)
- Business travel expenses related to income generation

**Condition**: The expense must be necessary to generate or preserve taxable income (Art. 29, D-134/1991). Personal expenses disguised as business expenses are not deductible.

### Small Business Threshold

Under Decreto 134 (1991) and the Tax Code (Decreto 230/2000):
- Annual gross income below $4,064: exempt from income tax
- Small taxpayers (*pequeños contribuyentes*) with annual sales below $150,000 may qualify for simplified tax regimes
- All businesses must register with the DGII and file annual tax returns regardless of income level, though those below the threshold owe no tax

## VAT / IVA (Decreto 296/1992 -- Ley de IVA)

### IVA Rate

The standard VAT rate (*IVA*) in El Salvador is **13%** (Art. 54, Decreto 296/1992), applied to:
- Sales of movable goods
- Imports of goods
- Provision of services (including digital/online services)

### Exempt Items and Services (Art. 45-46, D-296/1992)

Key IVA exemptions include:
- Basic food staples (*canasta basica*): vegetables, grains, basic foods
- Medicines and pharmaceutical products
- Educational services provided by government-recognized institutions
- Financial services
- Personal services provided by employees (wages are not subject to IVA)
- Export of goods (zero-rated at 0%)
- Public transportation services

### IVA Registration Threshold

- Businesses with annual sales exceeding **$5,714.29** must register as IVA taxpayers (*contribuyentes de IVA*) with the DGII under Decreto 296/1992
- Registered businesses must issue tax receipts (comprobantes de credito fiscal) for all transactions

## Tax Code (Decreto 230/2000 -- Codigo Tributario)

The Tax Code (Decreto 230/2000) establishes the general rules governing **all** taxes in El Salvador, including filing procedures, payment deadlines, penalties for non-compliance, and taxpayer rights.

### Tax Filing Deadlines (Decreto 230/2000 and Decreto 134/1991)

| Tax | Filing Period | Deadline |
|---|---|---|
| **Annual income tax return (ISR)** | Calendar year (Jan 1 -- Dec 31) | **April 30** of the following year |
| **IVA monthly declaration** | Each calendar month | 15th of the following month |
| **Annual tax balance (IVA)** | Calendar year | April 30 of the following year |
| **Advance income tax payments** | Monthly | Monthly installments required for certain taxpayers |

Under Decreto 230 (2000), Art. 94: taxpayers must file their annual income tax return and pay any balance due by **April 30** of the year following the tax year. For example, the return for 2025 income is due April 30, 2026.

### Penalties for Non-Payment (Decreto 230/2000)

Under Decreto 230 (2000), failing to pay taxes results in:
- **Late filing penalty**: 5% per month of the unpaid amount (up to 50%)
- **Late payment interest**: Annual interest rate set by the DGII (historically 1-2% monthly)
- **Tax evasion** (deliberately concealing income): Criminal offense under Art. 249-A of the Penal Code (Decreto 1030/1997), punishable by 4-6 years in prison
- **Tax fraud**: Fines up to 3 times the evaded amount plus criminal prosecution under both D-230/2000 and D-1030/1997
- **Closure of business**: The DGII can order temporary closure of a business for serious or repeated violations under Decreto 230 (2000)

### Taxpayer Rights (Decreto 230/2000)

Under Art. 165 of the Tax Code (Decreto 230/2000), taxpayers have the right to:
- Be presumed to be acting in good faith
- Receive clear information about their tax obligations
- Contest tax assessments through administrative appeals
- Have audits conducted respectfully and professionally
- Pay the correct amount -- no more than legally owed

## Do I Have to Pay Taxes on Foreign Income?

Under Decreto 134 (1991) (territorial principle, Art. 1 and 16):
- **No** -- income earned outside El Salvador from foreign sources is generally NOT subject to Salvadoran income tax
- **Yes** -- income earned from activities performed or sourced in El Salvador IS taxable, even if received from a foreign payer
- El Salvador does not have worldwide taxation like the US. A Salvadoran resident earning salary from a US company for work done in the US does not pay Salvadoran income tax on that salary.

---

## Do I Have to File Taxes If My Business Makes Less Than $50,000/Year?

Yes, you must file under Decreto 230 (2000) (Tax Code), but you may owe little or no tax:

1. **Income tax**: If net taxable income is below $4,064/year, no income tax is owed under Decreto 134 (1991). However, you must still file the annual return if you are registered as a taxpayer.
2. **IVA**: If annual sales exceed $5,714, you must file monthly IVA declarations under Decreto 296 (1992) and Decreto 230 (2000).
3. **Registration**: All businesses operating in El Salvador must register with the DGII under the Tax Code (Decreto 230/2000). Failure to register is a violation of D-230/2000 even if no tax is owed.

## Can I Pay Taxes in Bitcoin?

Yes. Under Decreto 57 (2021) (Bitcoin Law), bitcoin is legal tender in El Salvador. This means:
- Tax payments to the government can be made in bitcoin
- The exchange rate used is the reference rate at the time of payment
- The Tax Code (Decreto 230/2000) and Income Tax Law (Decreto 134/1991) apply regardless of the currency used

## Common Questions

### 1. What is the income tax rate in El Salvador?
The income tax rate for individuals ranges from 0% to 30% depending on income level (Decreto 134/1991). No tax on income below $4,064/year. Income from $4,064-$9,143 is taxed at 10%. Income from $9,143-$22,857 at 20%. Income above $22,857 at 30%. Corporations pay 25% (30% if income exceeds $150,000) (Decreto 134/1991).

### 2. Do I have to pay taxes on money I earn outside of El Salvador?
Generally no. El Salvador uses a territorial system under Decreto 134 (1991): only income sourced in or generated within El Salvador is taxable. Under Decreto 230 (2000) (Tax Code), however, you must still report all income activities to the DGII. Income from abroad that has no connection to El Salvador is not subject to Salvadoran income tax under D-134/1991.

### 3. What is the IVA rate and what is exempt?
The IVA (VAT) rate is 13% under Decreto 296 (1992). Exempt items include basic food staples, medicines, educational services, financial services, and exports (zero-rated). Most other goods and services are subject to the 13% IVA.

### 4. When is the tax filing deadline in El Salvador?
The annual income tax return is due **April 30** of the year following the tax year, under Decreto 230 (2000) (Tax Code) and Decreto 134 (1991). Monthly IVA declarations are due by the 15th of the following month under Decreto 296 (1992) and Decreto 230 (2000).

### 5. What happens if I don't pay my taxes?
Under the Tax Code (Decreto 230/2000): late filing penalties of 5% per month (up to 50%), plus monthly interest charges. Deliberate tax evasion is a criminal offense under Art. 249-A of the Penal Code (Decreto 1030/1997), punishable by 4-6 years in prison. The DGII can also close your business and seize assets for serious violations (Decreto 230/2000).

### 6. Can I deduct rent and utilities as business expenses?
Yes. Under Art. 29 of Decreto 134 (1991), rent paid for business premises and utilities (electricity, water, telecommunications) used to generate income are deductible business expenses. The expenses must be necessary for the business and properly documented with receipts and invoices.

### 7. My small business makes less than $50k per year -- do I still need to file taxes?
Yes. Under the Tax Code (Decreto 230/2000), all registered businesses must file. If annual sales exceed $5,714, you must file monthly IVA declarations (Decreto 296/1992). If your net income is below $4,064, no income tax is owed under Decreto 134 (1991), but you still file the return. Contact the DGII (Ministerio de Hacienda) to confirm your specific obligations.
