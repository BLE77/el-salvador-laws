# Decreto 776 (2005) -- Consumer Protection Law

## Summary
The Consumer Protection Law protects the rights of consumers in El Salvador, ensuring fairness, certainty, and legal security in transactions between consumers and providers of goods and services. It creates the Consumer Protection Agency (*Defensoria del Consumidor*) as the main enforcement body and establishes rules on pricing, advertising, product safety, warranties, financial services, e-commerce, and dispute resolution. The law applies to all consumer transactions regardless of whether the provider is public or private.

## Key Provisions
- **Article 1**: The purpose of the law is to protect consumer rights in order to ensure balance, certainty, and legal security in relationships between consumers and providers.
- **Article 4**: Key definitions:
  - **Consumer or user** (*consumidor o usuario*): any natural or legal person who acquires, uses, or enjoys goods or services as a final consumer, not for resale or business use (except micro and small businesses).
  - **Provider** (*proveedor*): any natural or legal person that produces, manufactures, imports, distributes, markets, or provides goods or services to consumers.
- **Article 5**: Basic consumer rights include:
  - Protection from price increases on essential goods and services
  - Protection from charges for undelivered goods or unperformed services
  - Right to complete, accurate, truthful, and timely information about products and services
  - Protection against all forms of illegal advertising
  - Right to safety in goods and services consumed
  - Right to compensation for damages caused by defective products or services
  - Right to choose freely among available products and services
- **Article 8**: Producers and sellers of food, beverages, and medicines must post consumer rights notices visibly in their establishments.
- **Article 9**: Products containing toxic, caustic, corrosive, or abrasive substances must comply with specific labeling requirements under pesticide and agricultural product laws.
- **Article 14**: Sellers of goods on installment plans (*venta a plazos*) must clearly disclose the cash price, total financing cost, interest rate, payment schedule, and all fees.
- **Article 18**: Prohibited practices for all providers include: tying the sale of one product to the purchase of another (unless naturally complementary); requiring blank checks or promissory notes; making improper charges; discriminating among consumers; and retaining documents that consumers need for other dealings.
- **Article 21-B**: E-commerce providers must present a clear order summary before completing any transaction, including individual prices, shipping costs, and the total amount. Consumers have the right to cancel an online order before it is confirmed.
- **Article 29**: Food, beverage, and medicine producers must print expiration dates, chemical additives, and nutritional information on packaging.
- **Article 31**: Illegal advertising (*publicidad ilicita*) includes false or deceptive advertising, advertising that exploits fear or superstition, and comparative advertising that denigrates competitors.
- **Article 38**: Providers are strictly liable for damages caused by defects in their products or services, especially for food, beverages, pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, appliances, and transportation.
- **Article 51**: The Sanctions Tribunal (*Tribunal Sancionador*) may impose coercive fines of up to 100 monthly minimum wages to compel providers to cease prohibited conduct, comply with requirements, or observe precautionary measures.
- **Article 73**: The Consumer Protection Agency (*Defensoria del Consumidor*) is advised by a Consultative Council that includes the Superintendent of Competition, the head of CONACYT, and representatives from universities and business associations.
- **Article 102**: The Agency may deploy undercover delegates (*delegado encubierto*) posing as consumers to verify compliance with promotions, pricing rules, and advertising regulations.
- **Article 125**: Consumer disputes may be resolved through arbitration (*arbitraje de consumo*). The responding party has 5 business days to accept or reject arbitration; silence is treated as refusal.
- **Article 141**: Decisions of the Sanctions Tribunal may be challenged through a nullity appeal (*recurso de nulidad*) before the competent Second Instance Chamber.
- **Article 158-160**: All government institutions that interact with consumer rights must coordinate with the Consumer Protection Agency and send it statistics on consumer complaints.

## Who This Affects
All consumers (individuals and small businesses buying goods or services for personal use) and all providers (manufacturers, importers, distributors, retailers, and service providers) in El Salvador. It also applies to financial service providers, e-commerce platforms, and advertisers.

## Related Laws

- Decreto 528 (2004) -- Competition Law: prevents anticompetitive practices that harm consumers

## Common Questions
- **Q: What are my basic rights as a consumer?** -- A: You have the right to accurate product information, safe products, fair pricing, compensation for defective goods, and protection from deceptive advertising (Art. 5).
- **Q: Can I return a defective product?** -- A: Yes. Providers are liable for damages caused by product defects and must provide remedies including replacement, repair, or refund (Art. 38).
- **Q: What can I do if a provider overcharges me?** -- A: File a complaint with the Consumer Protection Agency (*Defensoria del Consumidor*), which can investigate, mediate, or refer the matter to the Sanctions Tribunal (Arts. 51, 102).
- **Q: Are online purchases protected?** -- A: Yes. E-commerce providers must show clear order summaries, disclose all costs, and allow cancellation before order confirmation (Art. 21-B).
- **Q: What are the penalties for violating consumer protection law?** -- A: The Sanctions Tribunal can impose fines of up to 100 monthly minimum wages for noncompliance, plus order the provider to cease the violation (Art. 51).
- **Q: Is there consumer arbitration in El Salvador?** -- A: Yes. The Consumer Protection Agency offers arbitration as an alternative to litigation. Both parties must agree to arbitrate (Art. 125).
