# Government Structure and Public Services in El Salvador

## Summary

El Salvador is a unitary republic divided into 14 departments and subdivided into municipalities, each governed by an elected municipal council. The national government operates through three branches: the Executive (President), the Legislative (Asamblea Legislativa with 84 deputies), and the Judicial (Supreme Court and subordinate courts). Municipalities are the primary delivery mechanism for everyday public services -- garbage collection, local roads, water distribution, public markets, cemeteries, and parks. Citizens have constitutional and statutory rights to participate in government, vote in elections, access public records, challenge government decisions through administrative appeals, and report corruption. This page covers the practical laws governing how government works and how ordinary people interact with it.

The key statutes are:

| Area | Primary Law |
|---|---|
| Municipal government and services | Decreto 274/1986 -- Municipal Code |
| Elections and voting | Decreto 413/2013 -- Electoral Code |
| Access to public records | Decreto 534/2011 -- Ley de Acceso a la Informacion Publica |
| Challenging government decisions | Decreto 856/2017 -- Administrative Procedures Law |
| Corruption crimes | Decreto 1030/1997 -- Penal Code (Title XV) |
| Civil servants | Decreto 507/1961 -- Civil Service Law |

## Government Structure Basics -- The Three Branches

El Salvador's Constitution (1983) establishes three independent branches of government:

### 1. Legislative Branch -- Asamblea Legislativa

- **84 deputies** elected for 3-year terms, distributed among departments by population (Art. 13, D-413/2013).
- Sole power to create, modify, and repeal laws.
- Approves the national budget, ratifies international treaties, and elects the Attorney General, judges of the Supreme Court, and other key officials.

### 2. Executive Branch -- President of the Republic

- The President is elected by direct popular vote for a 5-year term.
- Heads the national administration, appoints ministers, commands the armed forces.
- Can veto legislation passed by the Assembly (see "How Laws Are Made" below).

### 3. Judicial Branch -- Corte Suprema de Justicia

- The Supreme Court is the highest judicial authority, organized into four chambers (Constitutional, Civil, Criminal, and Contentious-Administrative).
- Courts of first instance and courts of appeals operate throughout the country.
- The Contentious-Administrative Chamber (Sala de lo Contencioso Administrativo) reviews the legality of government decisions after administrative appeals are exhausted (D-760/2017).

### Administrative Divisions

- **14 departments** (San Salvador, La Libertad, Santa Ana, San Miguel, etc.), each headed by a governor appointed by the President.
- **Municipalities** -- each department is divided into municipalities. Each municipality is an autonomous political-administrative unit with its own elected government (Art. 2, D-274/1986).

## Municipal Government -- How Your Alcaldia Works

### Structure of the Municipal Council

Every municipality is governed by an elected **concejo municipal** (municipal council) composed of (Art. 24, D-274/1986):

| Role | Function |
|---|---|
| **Alcalde (Mayor)** | Presides over the council, represents the municipality legally and administratively (Art. 47) |
| **Sindico** | Legal adviser to the council, audits contracts and accounts, ensures legality (Art. 51) |
| **Regidores (Council members)** | Participate in all council votes and decisions |

The number of regidores depends on population:

| Population | Council Members (regidores) |
|---|---|
| Up to 10,000 | 2 |
| 10,001 - 20,000 | 4 |
| 20,001 - 50,000 | 6 |
| 50,001 - 100,000 | 8 |
| Over 100,000 | 10 |

Council decisions are made by majority vote: simple majority for ordinary matters, two-thirds for important matters, and three-quarters for loans and certain financial decisions (Art. 29, D-274/1986).

### What Services Municipalities Provide

Article 4 of the Municipal Code lists approximately 30 areas of municipal competency. The most important for daily life:

- **Garbage collection and waste disposal** -- the municipality is responsible for solid waste collection and management
- **Water supply** -- municipal water systems (often through decentralized entities like ANDA at the national level, but local distribution falls under municipal responsibility)
- **Local roads and streets** -- construction, maintenance, and naming of streets, sidewalks, and public spaces
- **Public markets** -- regulation and administration of local markets
- **Cemeteries** -- establishment and management of public cemeteries
- **Parks and recreation** -- maintenance of public parks, plazas, and recreational areas
- **Zoning and construction permits** -- land use regulation, building permits, and urban planning
- **Street lighting** -- public illumination of roads and public areas
- **Local transport regulation** -- bus routes, terminals, and traffic management within the municipality
- **Civil registry** -- registration of births, deaths, and marriages (delegated from the national registry)
- **Municipal police** -- local policing and public order

Municipalities can deliver these services directly, through delegated municipal entities, or through concessions awarded by public bidding (Art. 7, D-274/1986).

### How to Complain About Municipal Services

If your garbage is not being collected, your street has potholes, or any municipal service is failing, you have several options:

**1. Direct complaint to the alcaldia.** Go to your local alcaldia and file a written complaint. Under Article 17 of the Administrative Procedures Law (D-856/2017), you have the right to dignified treatment and a response within a reasonable time.

**2. Attend a cabildo abierto (open town hall).** Article 115 of the Municipal Code requires municipalities to promote citizen participation, including public council sessions and open town halls where residents can raise issues directly with the mayor and council.

**3. Communal associations.** If you belong to a communal association (asociacion comunal), the association can present collective complaints and proposals to the council on behalf of the community (Arts. 118-121, D-274/1986).

**4. Formal administrative petition.** Under D-856/2017, you can file a formal written petition to the municipality. The petition must include your name, ID, address, the facts, and what you are requesting (Art. 71). The municipality must respond within 60 business days. If it does not respond, your petition is deemed granted under the principle of positive administrative silence (Art. 114, D-856/2017).

**5. Transparency request.** If you want to know how the municipality is spending money on services, you can request budget documents, contracts, and audit reports under the transparency obligations in Articles 125-A through 125-F of the Municipal Code, or through the Ley de Acceso a la Informacion Publica (D-534/2011).

**6. Appeal to higher authorities.** If the municipality issues a formal decision you disagree with, you can file administrative appeals (see "How to Challenge Government Decisions" below).

### Municipal Finances

- Municipal income comes from local taxes, fees for services, the FODES fund (a constitutionally mandated transfer from the national government for municipal development), donations, and loans (Art. 63, D-274/1986).
- Tax rates must be proposed by the municipality and approved by the Asamblea Legislativa as law (Art. 3).
- A **municipal tax clearance certificate** (solvencia municipal) is required before transferring property or modifying corporate registrations (Art. 100).
- Municipalities must publish an annual budget (fiscal year January 1 to December 31) and render annual accountability reports within the first 60 days of each year (Arts. 72, 125-F).

## How Laws Are Made -- The Legislative Process

### Step-by-Step Process

**1. Initiative.** A bill (proyecto de ley) can be introduced by:
- Any deputy of the Asamblea Legislativa
- The President of the Republic through the Council of Ministers
- The Supreme Court (on matters related to the judiciary)
- Municipal councils (on matters related to local government)

**2. Committee review.** The bill is assigned to the relevant legislative committee, which studies it, may hold hearings, and produces a report with a recommendation.

**3. Debate and vote.** The full Assembly debates the bill. Approval requires a simple majority (43 of 84 deputies) for ordinary laws. Certain laws require a qualified majority of two-thirds (56 deputies), including amendments to the Constitution.

**4. Presidential action.** Once approved by the Assembly, the bill is sent to the President, who has three options:
- **Sign and publish** -- the bill becomes law upon publication in the Diario Oficial.
- **Veto** -- the President returns the bill to the Assembly with objections. The Assembly can override the veto with a two-thirds vote (56 deputies).
- **Do nothing** -- if the President neither signs nor vetoes within the constitutional deadline, the bill becomes law automatically.

**5. Publication.** All laws must be published in the Diario Oficial (the official government gazette) to take effect. Many laws specify an effective date that is a certain number of days or months after publication.

### Types of Legislative Acts

- **Decreto Legislativo** -- a law passed by the Assembly (this is what most "decretos" in Salvadoran law are)
- **Decreto Ejecutivo** -- a regulation or executive order issued by the President
- **Ordenanza Municipal** -- a local ordinance issued by a municipal council

## Elections and Voting

### Who Runs Elections

The **Tribunal Supremo Electoral (TSE)** is the supreme authority on electoral matters. It consists of five Magistrates serving 5-year terms: three nominated by the three political parties or coalitions that received the most votes in the last presidential election, and two elected by the Supreme Court (Art. 46, D-413/2013).

### Who Can Vote

All Salvadoran citizens aged 18 or older have the right to vote. Voting is both a right and a civic duty. The vote is free, direct, equal, and secret (Art. 3, D-413/2013).

**The following persons cannot vote** (Art. 7):
- Those under a formal arrest warrant
- Persons declared mentally incapacitated or judicially incompetent
- Persons convicted of a crime
- Those who buy or sell votes
- Those who sign fraudulent voter petitions
- Those of "notoriously immoral conduct"

### What Elections Are Held

| Election | Term | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| President and Vice-President | 5 years | Every 5 years |
| Deputies (Asamblea Legislativa) | 3 years | Every 3 years |
| Municipal councils (Mayor + council) | 3 years | Every 3 years |
| PARLACEN deputies | 5 years | Every 5 years |

### How Voting Works

- Voters are registered in the **registro electoral** organized by municipality (Art. 85).
- In legislative elections, voters can **vote for individual candidates by name** (voto cruzado) or for an entire party list by marking the party flag (Art. 206).
- Once the final count (escrutinio final) is completed without challenge, results become legally binding and the TSE issues a formal decree (Art. 220).
- Election results can be challenged through a **nullity petition** (nulidad de eleccion) filed within 24 hours of the election, based on fraud, voter intimidation, or procedural irregularities (Arts. 271-273).

### Employer Obligations on Election Day

Employers must grant paid leave to employees assigned to electoral duties, including training days, Election Day service, and the day after the election (Art. 114, D-413/2013).

## Freedom of Information -- Accessing Public Records

### The Law

The **Ley de Acceso a la Informacion Publica** (D-534/2011) establishes the right of every person to access information held by any public institution. It creates the **Instituto de Acceso a la Informacion Publica (IAIP)** as the independent body that oversees compliance and resolves disputes.

### How to Request Government Documents

**1. Identify the institution.** Determine which government body holds the information you want (a ministry, municipality, autonomous entity, etc.).

**2. Submit a request.** Every public institution must have an Information Officer (Oficial de Informacion). Submit a written request (in person, by mail, or electronically where available) specifying:
- Your name and identification
- A description of the information requested (as specific as possible)
- Your preferred format for receiving the information

**3. Wait for a response.** The institution must respond within **10 business days**, extendable by another 10 days in complex cases.

**4. Receive the information or a denial.** If the request is denied, the institution must explain the specific legal grounds for denial in writing.

**5. Appeal a denial.** If you disagree with a denial, you can appeal to the IAIP, which will review the case and issue a binding decision.

### What Information Is Public

As a general rule, all information produced, obtained, or held by any public institution using public funds is public. This includes:
- Budgets and financial statements
- Government contracts and procurement records
- Salaries of public officials
- Audit reports
- Meeting minutes of public bodies
- Municipal ordinances and regulations

### What Information Is Exempt

Certain categories of information may be classified as reserved (confidential) for a limited time:
- National security and defense information
- Ongoing criminal investigations
- Information whose disclosure would endanger a person's life or safety
- Personal data protected by privacy laws

Classified information must be declassified after a maximum of 7 years (extendable to 12 in exceptional cases).

### The IAIP

The **Instituto de Acceso a la Informacion Publica** is an independent body that:
- Ensures all public institutions comply with the law
- Resolves appeals when information requests are denied
- Can order institutions to release information
- Publishes annual reports on government transparency compliance

## Reporting Corruption

### What Counts as Corruption Under the Law

The Penal Code (D-1030/1997) defines several corruption offenses committed by public officials. The most important:

| Crime | Penal Code Article | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Peculado (Embezzlement)** | Art. 335 | A public official who diverts, uses, or allows others to use public funds or property for personal benefit. Carries prison and disqualification from office. |
| **Negociaciones ilicitas (Illicit dealings)** | Art. 329 | A public official who exploits their position to benefit personally from government contracts. Carries prison and disqualification. |
| **Cohecho (Bribery)** | Arts. 330-331 | A public official who solicits or accepts money or gifts in exchange for performing or failing to perform an official duty. |
| **Enriquecimiento ilicito (Illicit enrichment)** | Art. 333 | A public official whose wealth increases disproportionately to their lawful income during or after their time in office. |
| **Prevaricato** | Art. 310 | A judge or public official who knowingly issues a decision contrary to law. |

All corruption offenses carry both prison sentences and **disqualification from public office** as an accessory penalty (Art. 46, D-1030/1997).

### How to File a Denuncia (Criminal Complaint) Against a Public Official

**1. Go to the Fiscalia General de la Republica (Attorney General's Office).** The Fiscalia is the institution responsible for investigating and prosecuting crimes, including corruption. You can file a denuncia at any Fiscalia office.

**2. What to include in your denuncia:**
- Your identification (though anonymous complaints are accepted in practice)
- The name and position of the public official, if known
- A detailed description of the corrupt act -- what happened, when, where
- Any evidence you have: documents, photographs, recordings, witness names

**3. What happens next.** The Fiscalia opens an investigation. If it finds sufficient evidence, it brings charges before a criminal court. The accused official has the right to due process, a defense attorney, and a trial.

**4. Alternative: file with the Corte de Cuentas de la Republica.** The Corte de Cuentas is the supreme audit institution of El Salvador. It audits the use of public funds and can impose administrative sanctions (fines, disqualification) on officials who misuse public resources. You can report suspected financial irregularities directly to the Corte de Cuentas.

**5. For misuse of information or lack of transparency.** If a government institution is hiding public information or failing to comply with transparency obligations, file a complaint with the IAIP (see "Freedom of Information" above).

### Penalties for Corruption

Corruption convictions carry:
- **Prison time** -- varies by offense, generally 3-10 years depending on severity
- **Absolute disqualification from public office** -- the convicted official is barred from holding any public position for the duration of the sentence plus an additional period
- **Restitution** -- courts can order the official to return misappropriated funds
- **Civil liability** -- under Article 245 of the Constitution and Article 60 of D-856/2017, public officials who violate constitutional rights bear **personal, subjective liability** for damages

### Whistleblower Protections

The Ley de Acceso a la Informacion Publica (D-534/2011) and general principles of administrative law provide some protection for persons who report corruption. Public employees who disclose information about corruption or illegality within their institution should not be punished for doing so, particularly when the disclosure relates to information that is legally required to be public.

## How to Challenge Government Decisions -- Administrative Appeals

When a government agency issues a decision that affects you -- denying a permit, imposing a fine, revoking a license, or any other administrative act -- you have the right to challenge it. The process is governed by the **Ley de Procedimientos Administrativos** (D-856/2017).

### Your Rights When Dealing With Government

Article 17 of D-856/2017 guarantees every citizen the right to:
- **Good administration** -- equitable, just, objective, and impartial treatment with resolution within a reasonable time
- **Interact electronically** with government agencies (where available)
- **Access public files** related to your case
- **Dignified treatment** -- with preferential treatment for vulnerable persons (disabled, elderly, pregnant, minors)
- **File complaints, suggestions, and appeals**
- **Receive quality public services**

The government cannot demand documents it already has, documents you previously submitted, or documents available in public registries (Art. 4). Every administrative decision that limits your rights must include a written explanation of the facts and legal basis (Art. 23).

### Step-by-Step: Challenging a Government Decision

**Step 1 -- Receive the decision.** The government must notify you of its decision in writing, including the legal basis and an explanation of your appeal rights.

**Step 2 -- Optional: Recurso de reconsideracion (Reconsideration).** Within **10 business days** of notification, you can ask the same body that issued the decision to reconsider. This is optional. The body has one month to respond (Art. 124).

**Step 3 -- Mandatory: Recurso de apelacion (Appeal).** Within **15 business days** of notification (or within 15 days of the reconsideration decision if you filed one), file an appeal with the **superior hierarchical authority**. This is mandatory before you can go to court. The appellate authority has 60 business days to decide (Art. 124).

**Step 4 -- Suspension of the decision.** Filing an appeal does not automatically stop the government from enforcing its decision, except in two cases: (a) the decision orders you to pay money, or (b) the decision imposes a sanction -- in both cases, filing the appeal automatically suspends enforcement (Art. 127). In other cases, you can request suspension if enforcement would cause irreparable harm.

**Step 5 -- Judicial review.** If the administrative appeal fails, you can challenge the decision before the **Sala de lo Contencioso Administrativo** (Contentious-Administrative Chamber) of the Supreme Court under D-760/2017. This is the judicial review stage where a court evaluates whether the government acted lawfully.

### Positive Administrative Silence -- When the Government Does Not Respond

If you filed a petition or application and the government fails to respond within the legal deadline (60 business days if no specific period applies), your petition is **deemed granted automatically** under the principle of positive administrative silence (Art. 114, D-856/2017). Important exceptions: this does not apply to appeals against prior decisions, requests involving public domain property, or cases where a specific law prescribes negative silence.

### Government Liability -- Getting Compensation

If the government causes you harm through its actions or inactions, you have the right to monetary compensation. Article 55 of D-856/2017 establishes that the State is liable for injuries to citizens' property or rights resulting from either normal or abnormal government functioning. Compensable damages include patrimonial losses, physical harm, moral damages, and lost profits. If the government does not resolve your compensation claim within 60 days, it is deemed denied and you can proceed to court.

## Common Questions

### 1. My garbage has not been collected for weeks. What can I do?

Garbage collection is a municipal responsibility under Article 4 of the Municipal Code (D-274/1986). Go to your alcaldia and file a written complaint. You can also raise the issue at a cabildo abierto (open town hall), which municipalities are required to hold under Article 115. If the municipality ignores your complaint, you can file a formal administrative petition under D-856/2017, and if they do not respond within 60 business days, your petition is deemed granted by positive administrative silence (Art. 114).

### 2. How do I report a corrupt government official?

File a **denuncia** at the Fiscalia General de la Republica (Attorney General's Office). Describe what the official did, provide any evidence you have, and include the official's name and position if known. The Fiscalia will investigate and, if warranted, prosecute under the corruption provisions of the Penal Code (Arts. 329-335, D-1030/1997). For financial irregularities involving public funds, you can also report to the Corte de Cuentas de la Republica.

### 3. Can I request a copy of my municipality's budget or contracts?

Yes. Under the Ley de Acceso a la Informacion Publica (D-534/2011), all information produced or held by public institutions using public funds is public. Submit a written request to the municipality's Information Officer. They must respond within 10 business days. Additionally, Articles 125-A through 125-F of the Municipal Code require municipalities to proactively publish budgets, contracts, audit reports, and council meeting minutes.

### 4. How do I challenge a fine or denial of a permit from a government agency?

First file a **recurso de apelacion** (appeal) with the agency's superior authority within 15 business days of being notified of the decision. This is mandatory before going to court. If the appeal is denied, you can then challenge the decision judicially before the Sala de lo Contencioso Administrativo (Arts. 124-127, D-856/2017). If the decision involves a fine, filing the appeal automatically suspends enforcement (Art. 127).

### 5. What happens if a government agency never responds to my application?

Under the principle of **positive administrative silence** (Art. 114, D-856/2017), if the government fails to respond within the legal deadline (60 business days by default), your application is deemed granted automatically. This means you can proceed as if the application was approved. Exceptions apply for appeals against prior decisions and requests involving public domain property.

### 6. Who can vote in El Salvador, and how does voting work?

All Salvadoran citizens aged 18 or older can vote. Voting is by secret ballot. In legislative elections, you can vote for individual candidates by name (voto cruzado) or for a whole party list (Art. 206, D-413/2013). The TSE (Tribunal Supremo Electoral) organizes all elections. You must be registered in the electoral register to vote (Art. 85). Persons with outstanding arrest warrants, criminal convictions, or those who buy/sell votes cannot vote (Art. 7).

### 7. How are laws actually passed in El Salvador?

A bill is introduced in the Asamblea Legislativa by a deputy, the President, the Supreme Court, or a municipal council. It goes to a committee for review, then to a floor vote. Ordinary laws require 43 of 84 deputies to pass. The President can sign it into law, veto it (the Assembly can override with 56 votes), or let it become law without signature. All laws must be published in the Diario Oficial to take effect.

### 8. Can I sue the government for damages it caused me?

Yes. Article 55 of the Administrative Procedures Law (D-856/2017) establishes that the State is liable for harm to citizens' property or rights from both normal and abnormal government operations. You must first file an administrative claim. If the government does not resolve it within 60 days, it is deemed denied, and you can proceed to the Contentious-Administrative courts. Compensable damages include financial losses, physical injury, moral harm, and lost profits.

### 9. What is the difference between the Fiscalia General and the Corte de Cuentas?

The **Fiscalia General de la Republica** is the Attorney General's Office -- it investigates and prosecutes crimes, including corruption. It can bring criminal charges that result in prison sentences. The **Corte de Cuentas de la Republica** is the supreme audit institution -- it audits public finances and imposes administrative sanctions (fines, disqualification) on officials who mismanage public funds. For a corruption complaint, you can go to either or both: the Fiscalia for criminal prosecution and the Corte de Cuentas for financial accountability.

### 10. Does the government have to explain why it denied my request or application?

Yes. Article 23 of the Administrative Procedures Law (D-856/2017) requires that every administrative act that imposes obligations, limits or denies rights, resolves appeals, departs from prior criteria, or involves discretionary authority must contain a written statement of the relevant facts and legal grounds. An unexplained denial is legally defective and can be challenged through administrative appeals and, ultimately, judicial review.
