# Tipping Rules in El Salvador

There is **no law in El Salvador that requires customers to leave a tip**. Tipping (propina) is a voluntary practice, though it is culturally appreciated in service industries. However, the distinction between a voluntary tip and a mandatory service charge is important.

## Restaurant Service Charges

Many restaurants in El Salvador, particularly mid-range and upscale establishments, add a **10% service charge** (propina or cargo por servicio) directly to the bill. Key points about this practice:

- When printed on the bill, this charge is **legally a fee**, not a voluntary tip — the customer is obligated to pay it
- The restaurant is responsible for distributing this amount to its workers
- Customers can ask to have it removed if the service was unsatisfactory, though this is uncommon and may be refused
- Not all restaurants add this charge; it varies by establishment

## Tips Belong to Workers

Tips given directly to service workers belong to the worker. Employers cannot confiscate or pool tips without the employee's consent. There is no statutory framework governing tip distribution among staff, so internal arrangements vary by workplace.

## Where Tipping Is Common

Tipping is customary but not required in:

- **Restaurants** without a service charge: 10% is a common voluntary tip
- **Hotels**: Small tips for bellhops and housekeeping
- **Taxis**: Rounding up the fare is common but not expected
- **Delivery services**: Small tips are appreciated
- **Salons and barber shops**: Tips at the customer's discretion

## No Minimum Tip Law

There is no legal minimum tip amount. The decision of whether and how much to tip is entirely at the customer's discretion unless a service charge appears on the bill.
