# Decreto 1027 (1997) — Penitentiary Law

## Summary

The Penitentiary Law of El Salvador (Ley Penitenciaria), enacted by Legislative Decree No. 1027 on April 24, 1997, and effective April 20, 1998, is the primary statute governing the execution of criminal sentences, security measures, and pretrial detention in El Salvador. It replaced the prior 1973 Law on Penal Centers and Rehabilitation.

The law establishes a progressive regime of imprisonment designed around the constitutional mandate to rehabilitate offenders, educate them, and form work habits, with the ultimate goal of social reintegration. It creates a system of specialized courts (Judges and Chambers of Penitentiary Surveillance and Sentence Execution), administrative bodies (the General Directorate of Penal Centers, National and Regional Criminological Councils), and classifies penal institutions into admission centers, preventive centers, sentence-serving centers (ordinary, open, lesser detention, security, and maximum-security), and special centers.

The law has been amended at least 16 times between 1998 and 2025, with significant reforms addressing organized crime, telecommunications blocking inside prisons, visitor regulation, prison personnel discipline, sentence reduction through labor, special penitentiary benefits for the terminally ill, accessibility for persons with disabilities, and the establishment of maximum-security centers.

## Key Provisions

**Art. 1 -- Scope of Application.** The law regulates the execution of all sentences and security measures under the Penal Code and special legislation, as well as pretrial detention.

**Art. 2 -- Purpose of Sentence Execution.** Sentence execution must provide the convicted person with favorable conditions for personal development, enabling harmonious social reintegration upon release.

**Art. 3 -- Mission of Penitentiary Institutions.** Penitentiary institutions have the fundamental mission of pursuing the social rehabilitation of the convicted and the prevention of crime, as well as the custody of pretrial detainees. All persons deprived of liberty are classified as "inmates" (internos).

**Art. 5 -- Humanity and Equality.** Torture and degrading treatment during sentence execution are absolutely prohibited. No inmate may be discriminated against on the basis of nationality, sex, race, religion, political opinion, economic or social condition, or any other circumstance.

**Art. 6 -- Judicial Control.** All sentences are executed under the strict supervision of the Judge of Penitentiary Surveillance and Sentence Execution, and the corresponding appellate Chamber. For persons convicted under the Organized Crime Law, the specialized Organized Crime courts exercise this function.

**Art. 9 -- Rights of Inmates.** Inmates have a comprehensive set of rights including: adequate sanitary and medical facilities (free, timely, and accessible to persons with disabilities); sufficient food; to be called by their own name; dignity in all circumstances; respect for personal customs within regulations; non-afflictive paid work; freedom of movement within the center consistent with their regime; access to rehabilitative information; maintenance of family relationships; adequate facilities for family and intimate visits; private interviews with the judge, the center director, and their attorney; legal assistance during all sentence-execution proceedings; and decisions based on technical-scientific criteria.

**Art. 13 -- Obligations of Inmates.** Inmates must comply with center rules and disciplinary sanctions, respect others' rights, perform hygiene duties, maintain assigned property, participate in assigned rehabilitation programs, and other duties established by law.

**Art. 14 -- Prohibitions on Inmates.** Inmates are forbidden from possessing weapons, alcoholic beverages, drugs, unauthorized medications, money, jewelry, pornographic or violent materials, telecommunications devices (cell phones, radios, electronic devices, chips, phone cards), lighters, or clothing resembling state institution uniforms or obstructing visual identification.

**Art. 14-A through 14-G -- Visitor Regime.** Detailed regulations govern family and general visits (limited to second-degree relatives by blood or affinity, maximum five registered visitors, one-year lock on changes), intimate visits (in designated areas, two-hour maximum, only for proven partners), visitor requirements (registration, criminal background checks, visitor ID card), visitor obligations and prohibitions, sanctions for visitor violations (suspensions ranging from six months to fifteen years depending on severity), and the due process procedure for imposing visitor suspensions.

**Art. 18 -- Administrative Bodies.** The administrative agencies are: the General Directorate of Penal Centers; the National Criminological Council; the Regional Criminological Councils; the Technical Criminological Teams; and the Penitentiary School.

**Art. 33-37 -- Judicial Bodies.** The judicial bodies for applying the law are: the Chambers of Penitentiary Surveillance and Sentence Execution (appellate level); the Judges of Penitentiary Surveillance and Sentence Execution (first instance); and the Department of Probation and Supervised Freedom. The judges have 16 enumerated powers including controlling sentence execution, granting or revoking conditional release, computing sentences, resolving complaints, ordering release upon completion, and visiting penal centers.

**Art. 68-80 -- Classification of Penal Centers.** Centers are classified by function: admission centers (initial observation and diagnosis, up to 30 days); preventive centers (pretrial detainees only, with sectors for different age groups and security needs); sentence-serving centers (ordinary, open, lesser detention, security, and maximum-security); and special centers (health treatment). Centers must have dormitories, clinics, sports facilities, visiting areas, schools, libraries, workshops, intimate visit rooms, cafeterias, videoconference rooms, and disability-accessible infrastructure. A 300-meter construction-free buffer zone is required around new facilities. A 1,000-meter prohibition on telecommunications transmission equipment applies around all penal centers.

**Art. 79-A -- Maximum-Security Centers.** Reserved for highly dangerous inmates who exhibit hostile behavior, direct violence, or membership in criminal leadership. No family or intimate visits are permitted. The Director General authorizes all transfers to and from these facilities.

**Art. 95-102 -- Progressive Regime Phases.** Sentence execution proceeds through four phases: (1) Adaptation phase (up to 60 days, orientation and assessment); (2) Ordinary phase (structured work, education, recreation, and community-style living); (3) Trust phase (available after one-third of the sentence, with exit permits, more visits, and greater freedom of movement); and (4) Semi-liberty phase (available after two-fourths of the sentence, allowing outside work, broader exit permits, placement in open or lesser detention centers, and community reintegration support).

**Art. 103 -- Special Internment Regime.** Inmates convicted of drug trafficking, organized crime, aggravated homicide, femicide, rape, kidnapping, extortion, illicit associations, or terrorism offenses are subject to a special regime involving isolated confinement, restricted movement, supervised communications, no intimate visits, and controlled family visits.

**Art. 105-A -- Sentence Reduction Through Work.** Convicted inmates can reduce their sentence at a rate of two days credit for each day of effective labor. This benefit is excluded for inmates under the special internment regime and for those convicted of certain serious offenses.

**Art. 39-A through 39-H -- Special Penitentiary Benefits.** Conditional release and early conditional release may be granted to inmates with incurable terminal illnesses or severe chronic degenerative conditions who do not represent a social risk, provided they have served at least half their sentence and met behavioral requirements. Persons over 60 who have served one-third of their sentence are also eligible. These benefits are excluded for convicts of serious organized crime offenses, homicide, kidnapping, sexual crimes, robbery, extortion, corruption, or crimes against humanity.

**Art. 80-A through 80-E -- Telecommunications Traffic Blocking.** The Minister of Justice and Public Security, with technical input from the Superintendent of Electricity and Telecommunications, must implement permanent measures to cut all telecommunications traffic to and from penal centers, including requiring commercial operators to adopt technical solutions within 24 hours, prohibiting Wi-Fi signals within 100 meters of penal centers, and authorizing inspections of nearby properties with fines escalating from 2 to 20 minimum wages for repeat offenders.

**Art. 128-132 -- Discipline.** Disciplinary measures range from written warnings to solitary confinement in individual cells for up to 30 days and suspension of visits for up to six months. Corporal punishment, confinement in dark cells, and any cruel, inhuman, or degrading measure are prohibited. A Disciplinary Board composed of the center director and two members of the technical team adjudicates cases through oral hearings with the inmate's right to defense.

## Who This Affects

- **Convicted persons** serving prison sentences or subject to security measures under the Penal Code or special laws
- **Pretrial detainees** held under judicial detention orders
- **Penitentiary personnel**, including security staff, administrative employees, and professional/technical staff, who are subject to the law's disciplinary regime, training requirements, and code of conduct
- **Families and visitors** of inmates, who must comply with registration, identification, and behavioral requirements to access penal centers
- **Judges of Penitentiary Surveillance and Sentence Execution**, who exercise judicial oversight over all aspects of sentence execution
- **The General Directorate of Penal Centers** and its administrative organs (Criminological Councils, Technical Teams, Penitentiary School)
- **The Attorney General, Procurator General, and Human Rights Ombudsman**, who participate in sentence-execution proceedings within their respective mandates
- **Telecommunications operators**, who must comply with signal-blocking requirements around penal centers
- **Property owners near penal centers**, who are subject to construction restrictions and Wi-Fi signal regulations within specified perimeters
- **Civil society organizations and patronatos**, which may participate in inmate assistance, post-release support, and rehabilitation programs

## Related Laws

- **Penal Code (Decreto 1030, 1997)** -- Defines the crimes, sentences, and security measures that this law executes
- **Code of Criminal Procedure (Codigo Procesal Penal)** -- Governs pretrial detention rules and procedural requirements referenced throughout the Penitentiary Law
- **Organized Crime Law (Ley Contra el Crimen Organizado y Delitos de Realizacion Compleja)** -- Establishes specialized jurisdiction for sentence execution in organized crime cases
- **Law Against Terrorism (Ley Especial Contra Actos de Terrorismo)** -- Defines terrorism offenses subject to the special internment regime
- **Law on Proscription of Gangs (Ley de Proscripcion de Maras, Pandillas, Agrupaciones, Asociaciones y Organizaciones de Naturaleza Criminal)** -- Covers gang-related offenses subject to special regime restrictions
- **Drug Activities Law (Ley Reguladora de las Actividades Relativas a las Drogas)** -- Drug trafficking offenses that trigger the special internment regime
- **Anti-Extortion Law (Ley Especial Contra el Delito de Extorsion)** -- Contains telecommunications-related infractions and penalties referenced in the prison telecommunications blocking provisions
- **Telecommunications Law (Ley de Telecomunicaciones)** -- Governs procedures and sanctions for telecommunications operators who fail to comply with prison signal-blocking orders
- **Organic Judicial Law (Ley Organica Judicial)** -- Regulates the creation, organization, and jurisdiction of the Penitentiary Surveillance courts and the Department of Probation and Supervised Freedom
- **Juvenile Criminal Law (Ley Penal Juvenil)** -- Governs minors subject to internment, with cross-references added by the 2025 reform regarding sentence-serving centers for minors

## Common Questions

**What is the progressive regime, and how does an inmate advance through it?**
The progressive regime has four phases: adaptation (up to 60 days of orientation), ordinary (structured daily routines of work, education, and recreation), trust (after one-third of sentence, with exit permits and expanded privileges), and semi-liberty (after half the sentence, allowing outside work and placement in open centers). Advancement is decided by the Regional Criminological Council based on periodic evaluations of the inmate's behavior, participation in programs, and personal development. Regression to an earlier phase is also possible for non-compliance.

**Can inmates reduce their sentences by working?**
Yes. Under Article 105-A, inmates can earn sentence reduction at a rate of two days of credit for every one day of effective labor or community support activities, under the direction and supervision of the Regional Criminological Council. However, this benefit is excluded for inmates under the special internment regime and those convicted of certain serious offenses including aggravated homicide, kidnapping, extortion, sexual crimes, terrorism, organized crime, and drug trafficking.

**What rights do pretrial detainees have compared to convicted inmates?**
Pretrial detainees retain all constitutional rights and the rights established in this law. They cannot be forced to participate in penitentiary activities beyond those necessary to preserve the purpose of their detention. They may work voluntarily and have access to the same basic services (medical care, food, family visits) as convicted inmates.

**Who can visit an inmate, and what are the rules?**
Only persons within the second degree of consanguinity or affinity who have been pre-registered by the inmate may visit. Each inmate may register up to five visitors, and the visitor list cannot be changed for one year. Visitors must pass criminal background checks, present government-issued ID, and carry a visitor card issued by the General Directorate. Children visit in specially designated child-friendly areas. Intimate visits are limited to proven spouses or stable partners, last a maximum of two hours, and take place in designated areas. Visitors who violate rules face suspension periods ranging from six months to fifteen years depending on the severity of the infraction.

**What happens to inmates convicted of serious crimes like organized crime or terrorism?**
They are placed under the special internment regime (Article 103), which involves isolated confinement, restricted movement within the center, prohibition of television, supervised written materials, monitored phone calls, family visits only through glass or video with no physical contact, and an absolute prohibition on intimate visits. Those deemed most dangerous are placed in maximum-security centers where no family or intimate visits are permitted at all.

**What oversight mechanisms exist to protect inmates' rights?**
Multiple layers of oversight exist: Judges of Penitentiary Surveillance conduct periodic visits to penal centers and must interview any inmate who requests it; inmates can file judicial complaints (quejas) when their fundamental rights are violated, which must be resolved in oral hearing within 72 hours; the Human Rights Ombudsman receives monthly updated lists of all detainees; the Attorney General, Procurator General, and Human Rights Ombudsman all participate in sentence-execution proceedings; and prison directors must immediately notify the Ombudsman and Attorney General whenever a state of emergency is declared.

**Can inmates access conditional release or parole?**
Yes. The law provides for conditional release when inmates meet the requirements established in the Penal Code, including having served the required portion of their sentence and demonstrating good conduct and rehabilitation progress. The Regional Criminological Council prepares an individualized social reintegration prognosis, and the Judge of Penitentiary Surveillance decides. Special early release provisions exist for terminally ill inmates, those with severe chronic degenerative diseases, and persons over 60 years old, though these benefits are excluded for inmates convicted of serious offenses listed in Article 39-F.

**How are telecommunications blocked inside prisons?**
The Minister of Justice and Public Security is required to implement permanent measures to eliminate all telecommunications traffic from and to penal centers. This includes requiring commercial telecommunications operators to adopt technical blocking solutions within 24 hours, prohibiting new antenna installation within 500 meters of penal centers, banning Wi-Fi signals within 100 meters, authorizing inspections of nearby properties suspected of providing wireless signals, and imposing escalating fines (2 to 20 minimum wages) and service disconnection for repeat offenders. Operators who fail to cooperate face penalties under the Anti-Extortion Law.
