# Decreto 274 (1986) — Municipal Code

## Summary
The Municipal Code is the primary law governing the organization, operation, and autonomous powers of all municipalities in El Salvador. It establishes municipalities as the fundamental political-administrative unit of the state, defines their competencies ranging from local development planning to public service delivery, and sets out the structure of municipal government through elected councils (concejos) led by a mayor (alcalde). The Code also regulates municipal finance, citizen participation, transparency obligations, personnel, and the procedures for expropriation of property for public works.

## Key Provisions
- **Article 1**: The Code develops the constitutional principles concerning the organization, operation, and exercise of the autonomous powers of municipalities.
- **Article 2**: Defines the municipality as the primary political-administrative unit within the state, with its own legal personality, territorial jurisdiction, and autonomy for self-governance.
- **Article 3**: Municipal autonomy extends to: creating/modifying fees and public contributions; approving its own budget; free management of its competencies; appointing and removing officials; issuing local ordinances and regulations; and proposing tax rates to the Legislative Assembly.
- **Article 4**: Enumerates 30 areas of municipal competency, including local development planning, public health, education and culture promotion, environmental protection, local transport regulation, civil registry, public markets, waste collection, cemeteries, municipal police, zoning and land use, and housing programs.
- **Article 7**: Municipal public services may be delivered directly by the municipality, through municipal entities via delegation or contract, or through concessions awarded by public bidding.
- **Article 11**: Municipalities may form associations with other municipalities to defend shared interests or cooperate on joint works and services.
- **Article 20**: To create a new municipality requires a population of at least 50,000 inhabitants, a defined territory, an urban center of at least 20,000 inhabitants, sufficient resources, and conformity with national development plans.
- **Article 24**: Municipal government is exercised by an elected council (concejo) composed of a mayor (alcalde), a legal advisor (sindico), and a number of council members (regidores) proportional to the population, ranging from 2 additional members for municipalities up to 10,000 inhabitants to 10 additional members for municipalities over 100,000.
- **Article 29**: Council decisions require simple majority (half plus one), qualified majority (two-thirds), or special qualified majority (three-quarters), depending on the matter.
- **Article 30**: Lists the council's powers, including appointing the municipal secretary and treasurer, issuing ordinances and regulations, approving the budget, approving contracts, adjudicating procurement, creating decentralized municipal entities, and setting salaries for officials.
- **Article 47**: The mayor is the legal and administrative representative of the municipality, head of local government and administration.
- **Article 51**: The sindico serves as the municipality's legal counsel, overseeing contract legality, auditing municipal accounts, and advising the council and mayor.
- **Article 63**: Lists 15 categories of municipal income, including taxes, fees, contributions, service revenues, FODES fund transfers (constitutionally mandated development fund), donations, loans, and asset sales.
- **Article 67**: Loans from domestic or foreign institutions not requiring state guarantees need approval by three-quarters of council members; loans with state guarantees additionally require Legislative Assembly authorization.
- **Article 72**: Municipalities must operate under an approved annual budget of income and expenditures. The fiscal year runs January 1 to December 31.
- **Article 100**: Municipal tax clearance certificates (solvencias) are required before any property transfer or corporate modification can be registered in the Property Registry or Commerce Registry.
- **Article 106-107**: Municipalities with annual income below 5 million colones (or dollar equivalent) must have internal auditing; those above that threshold must also hire an external auditor.
- **Article 115-117**: Municipal governments are obligated to promote citizen participation through mechanisms including public council sessions, open town halls (cabildo abierto), popular consultations, participatory budgeting, and local development committees. Popular consultations are binding if at least 40% of voters in the most recent municipal election participate.
- **Article 118-121**: Residents may form communal associations (asociaciones comunales) with a minimum of 25 members (at least 30% women), which receive legal personality from the municipal council.
- **Article 125-A through 125-F**: Establish transparency obligations requiring municipalities to provide public access to ordinances, budgets, plans, audit reports, and council meeting minutes, and to render annual accountability reports.
- **Article 126-137**: Set out municipal sanctions (fines up to 8 monthly minimum wages for commerce), enforcement procedures with due process protections, and administrative appeals (revision, revocation, and appeal to the council).
- **Article 138-155**: Regulate voluntary purchase and forced expropriation of property for public works, including valuation procedures, judicial expropriation proceedings, and payment terms.

## Who This Affects
This law affects every resident, business, and property owner within any of El Salvador's municipalities, as well as all elected municipal officials, municipal employees, communal associations, and any entity interacting with local government services, permits, or taxes.

## Related Laws
- **Constitution of El Salvador, Articles 202-207**: Constitutional foundation for municipal autonomy, the FODES fund, and municipal governance structure.
- **Decreto 86 (1991) — General Municipal Tax Law (Ley General Tributaria Municipal)**: Governs the general framework for municipal taxation, referenced in Articles 69 and 71.
- **Decreto 868 (2000) — Law on Procurement and Contracting of Public Administration (LACAP)**: Governs municipal procurement procedures, referenced in Articles 94 and 96.
- **Decreto 762 (2023) — Special Law for Municipal Restructuring**: Related law for reorganization of municipalities.
- **Decreto 122 (2024) — Law Creating the Directorate of Territorial Planning and Construction**: Took over certain municipal planning functions.
- **Decreto 323 (2025) — Law Creating the Directorate of National and Local Commercial Markets**: Related to municipal market regulation.

## Common Questions
- **Q: What is the basic structure of municipal government in El Salvador?** — A: Under Article 24, each municipality is governed by an elected municipal council (concejo) composed of a mayor (alcalde), a legal advisor (sindico), and council members (regidores) whose number depends on population size. The council is the supreme authority of the municipality, and the mayor presides over it.

- **Q: What services are municipalities responsible for providing?** — A: Article 4 lists approximately 30 areas of competency including waste collection, cemeteries, local transport regulation, public markets, zoning and construction permits, civil registry, street and park maintenance, municipal police, and promotion of education, health, culture, and sports.

- **Q: How are municipal taxes and fees established?** — A: Under Article 3, municipalities can create, modify, and eliminate fees for services and public contributions. However, municipal tax rates must be proposed to and approved by the Legislative Assembly as law. Article 69 requires that all municipal tax laws specify the taxable event, taxpayer, amount, deductions, and penalties.

- **Q: Can residents participate in municipal governance?** — A: Yes. Articles 115-117 require municipalities to promote citizen participation through public council sessions, open town halls, popular consultations, participatory budgeting, and local development committees. A popular consultation is binding on the council if at least 40% of the electorate from the most recent municipal election participates.

- **Q: What are communal associations and how are they formed?** — A: Articles 118-121 allow residents of neighborhoods, villages, or hamlets to form communal associations with at least 25 members (30% minimum women). These associations receive legal personality from the municipal council and participate in analyzing community problems, proposing solutions, and supporting local development projects.

- **Q: What transparency obligations do municipalities have?** — A: Articles 125-A through 125-F require municipalities to provide public access to ordinances, budgets, municipal plans, property valuations, council meeting minutes, and audit reports. They must also render annual accountability reports covering finances, public investment projects, and completed works, published within the first 60 days of each year.

- **Q: Can the municipality take private property?** — A: Yes. Articles 138-155 allow municipalities to acquire private property voluntarily or through expropriation for public utility or local social interest purposes. The process requires council approval, published notices, an official appraisal, attempts at voluntary negotiation, and ultimately a judicial expropriation proceeding with fair compensation if no agreement is reached.

- **Q: What are the requirements to be elected to the municipal council?** — A: Article 26 requires candidates to be Salvadoran, a layperson (not clergy), in exercise of citizenship rights, at least 21 years old, literate, of notable morality and education, and a native or resident of the municipality for at least one year before the election. Article 27 lists disqualifications including municipal contractors, those with pending lawsuits against the municipality, active military or police, and liquor dealers.
