# Right to Protest and Assembly in El Salvador

The right to peaceful assembly and protest is guaranteed by **Article 7 of the Constitution of El Salvador**. This right allows citizens to gather for lawful purposes without prior authorization from the government.

## Constitutional Framework

Article 7 establishes:

- The right to **assemble peacefully** and without arms for any lawful purpose
- **No prior government authorization** is required to hold a peaceful demonstration
- The right extends to both public and private gatherings
- Assemblies for political, social, religious, or cultural purposes are all protected

## Limitations on Assembly

While the right is broadly protected, there are legal limits:

- **Violent gatherings** — police have authority to disperse assemblies that become violent or threaten public safety
- **Road blockages** — obstructing public roads (bloqueo de vias) can result in criminal charges, as it infringes on others' freedom of movement
- **Armed assemblies** — gatherings where participants carry weapons are not constitutionally protected
- **Private property** — assemblies cannot take place on private property without the owner's consent

## State of Exception

Under **Article 29 of the Constitution**, during a declared **state of exception** (regimen de excepcion), the right to assembly may be temporarily suspended. This requires Legislative Assembly approval and is limited in duration. During El Salvador's state of exception (declared in 2022 and extended), assembly rights have faced significant restrictions.

## Practical Guidance

Organizers of large demonstrations typically coordinate with the **PNC** to ensure safety and traffic management, even though formal permits are not legally required. Participants should carry identification. If police order a dispersal, failure to comply can result in detention. Document any interactions with authorities and be aware that photography and recording of police actions is legal.
