# Electricity Theft Laws in El Salvador

Electricity theft (conexion ilegal or hurto de energia electrica) is a criminal offense in El Salvador, prosecuted under the **Penal Code (Decreto 1030 of 1997)** and regulated by **SIGET (Superintendencia General de Electricidad y Telecomunicaciones)**.

## What Constitutes Electricity Theft

Illegal electricity connections include:

- **Direct connections** — bypassing the meter to tap into power lines without authorization
- **Meter tampering** — manipulating, altering, or damaging the electric meter to undercount consumption
- **Unauthorized reconnection** — reconnecting service after it has been legally disconnected for non-payment
- **Diverting electricity** — redirecting electricity from a neighboring property or public infrastructure

## Criminal Penalties

Under the Penal Code, electricity theft is treated as a form of **theft (hurto)** or **fraud (estafa)** depending on the method used:

- **Fines** proportional to the value of the stolen electricity
- **Imprisonment** — sentences vary based on the amount stolen and whether it is a repeat offense
- **Criminal record** — a conviction results in a permanent criminal record

## Civil Liability

In addition to criminal penalties, the offender faces **civil liability** for the value of all electricity consumed illegally. The electricity company can calculate estimated consumption based on the type of connection and duration of the illegal use, and demand full payment.

## Detection and Enforcement

Electricity distribution companies (such as CAESS, DELSUR, EEO, and others) conduct regular inspections to detect illegal connections. Upon discovery, the company can:

- **Disconnect service** immediately
- **File a criminal complaint** with the PNC or Fiscalia
- **Demand civil restitution** for consumed electricity

## Reporting

Suspected electricity theft can be reported to the local **electricity distribution company** or to the **PNC**. SIGET handles regulatory oversight and can investigate complaints about utility companies' practices.
