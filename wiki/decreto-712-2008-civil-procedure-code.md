# Decreto 712 (2008) -- Civil and Commercial Procedure Code

## Summary
The Civil and Commercial Procedure Code (*Codigo Procesal Civil y Mercantil*, CPCM) replaced the century-old 1881 Code of Civil Procedures to modernize civil and commercial litigation in El Salvador. It establishes the rules for filing lawsuits, presenting evidence, conducting hearings, issuing judgments, and enforcing court decisions in civil and commercial matters. The code embraces oral proceedings, party-driven litigation, and constitutional due process principles.

## Key Provisions
- **Article 1**: Every person has the right to bring a claim before the courts, oppose claims already filed, exercise all procedural acts for their defense, and have the process decided in accordance with constitutional norms and legal provisions.
- **Article 2**: Judges are bound by the Constitution, laws, and other legal norms. They may not disregard or disobey them. Any judge may examine the constitutionality of norms on which a decision depends.
- **Article 7**: Facts underlying claims and defenses may only be introduced by the parties (*principio dispositivo*). Evidentiary activity must focus exclusively on facts asserted by the parties.
- **Article 10**: The judge must give the parties equal treatment and ensure they have the same opportunities to assert their rights and present their defenses (*principio de igualdad*).
- **Article 27**: The Supreme Court of Justice sitting in plenary hears competence conflicts, cassation appeals when the Civil Chamber heard the case on appeal, and abstentions and recusals.
- **Article 28**: The Civil Chamber of the Supreme Court of Justice hears cassation appeals, exequatur proceedings (recognition of foreign judgments), appeals from second-instance chamber decisions, and petitions for review of final judgments.
- **Article 56**: Recusal of judges must be filed before the court hearing the case, stating the facts and attaching supporting documents. The recused judge submits a report, and the competent tribunal decides.
- **Article 82**: A third party with a direct and legitimate interest in the outcome may intervene as a co-litigant (*coadyuvante*) supporting either the plaintiff or defendant.
- **Article 104**: Claims by or against multiple parties (*acumulacion subjetiva*) are permitted when there is a connection by reason of the same title or cause of action.
- **Article 207**: Hearings are oral. If a hearing must be suspended and the judge is replaced, the entire hearing must be repeated before the new judge, except for unrepeatable evidence already in the record.
- **Article 253**: Conciliation hearings follow a structured format: identity verification, each party states their position, and the judge facilitates discussion toward a settlement.
- **Article 297**: If the parties fail to reach a settlement, the preparatory hearing continues with examination of procedural defects and other preliminary matters.
- **Article 311**: In the ordinary process (*proceso comun*), the trial hearing (*audiencia probatoria*) is where all evidence is presented orally before the judge.
- **Article 411**: After the evidence is presented, the parties give oral closing arguments (*alegatos finales*). The judge sets a reasonable time for each side, not less than one hour or more than three hours.
- **Article 417**: The judge must issue a written judgment within 15 days of the trial hearing.
- **Article 464**: In the executive process (*proceso ejecutivo*), the debtor may oppose execution on grounds including payment, prescription, defective title, settlement, or accord and satisfaction.
- **Article 524**: Cassation appeal (*recurso de casacion*) for procedural violations may be based on: lack of jurisdiction, lack of competence, improper procedure, lack of capacity, failure to serve process, denial of admissible evidence, among others.
- **Article 588**: Once a forced execution order is issued, it is not suspended by the filing of a review petition, unless the court finds sufficient cause and the debtor posts adequate security.
- **Article 698-705**: Provisions for execution of obligations to pay money, deliver property, or perform specific acts, including procedures for liquidating amounts owed for fruits, rents, or profits.

## Who This Affects
All individuals and legal entities involved in civil or commercial disputes in El Salvador, including contract disputes, debt collection, property claims, corporate litigation, family-related property matters, and enforcement of judgments. It governs the work of judges, lawyers, and litigants in civil and commercial courts.

## Common Questions
- **Q: What types of civil processes exist?** -- A: The code provides for the ordinary process (*proceso comun*) for complex matters, the abbreviated process (*proceso abreviado*) for simpler claims, the executive process (*proceso ejecutivo*) for enforceable titles, and special proceedings.
- **Q: Are civil trials oral or written?** -- A: Primarily oral. Hearings are conducted orally before the judge, though written submissions (complaints, answers) initiate the process (Art. 207).
- **Q: How long does a judge have to issue a judgment?** -- A: 15 days after the trial hearing in the ordinary process (Art. 417).
- **Q: Can foreign judgments be enforced in El Salvador?** -- A: Yes, through an exequatur proceeding before the Civil Chamber of the Supreme Court of Justice (Art. 28).
- **Q: What is a cassation appeal?** -- A: The highest level of appeal, heard by the Supreme Court, for violations of law or essential procedural requirements. It does not re-examine facts (Art. 524).
- **Q: Is mediation or conciliation required before trial?** -- A: The code provides for conciliation hearings as part of the process. If the parties reach a settlement, the judge approves it and the case is resolved without trial (Art. 253).
