# Decreto 3 (1983) -- General Budget Provisions

## Summary

Decreto 3 of 1983 establishes the standing operational rules that govern the execution of both the General Budget (central government) and the Special Budgets of autonomous official institutions in El Salvador. Originally covering treasury operations, budgeting, accounting, personnel, procurement, and supplies, the law has been heavily amended over the decades: nearly all of its original treasury, budget, and accounting provisions (Chapters I and II, Articles 2-70 Bis) were repealed by subsequent financial administration reforms. What remains in force is a detailed body of rules on government personnel matters (appointment, compensation, leave, incompatibilities, overtime, pensions), public property management, subsidies, and institution-specific fiscal provisions for entities such as ANTEL, the National Lottery, the University of El Salvador, and the National Pension Institute (INPEP).

## Key Provisions

**Art. 1 -- Scope and Supremacy.** These General Budget Provisions apply to all operations arising from the execution of the General Budget by central government units and Special Budgets of autonomous institutions. In case of conflict with any other law, these provisions prevail as special law.

**Art. 71 -- Appointments Without Executive Decree.** Certain lower-level positions (customs guards, prison guards, school watchmen, nursery staff) may be filled without a formal Executive Decree. Unit heads may authorize subordinate directors to appoint and remove such personnel according to service needs.

**Art. 72 -- Teaching Staff Appointments.** The Ministry of Education is authorized to appoint preschool, basic education (grades 1-9), and secondary-level teaching staff directly to positions in the Salary Law, assigning salaries per the National Teaching Profession Escalafon Law. The Court of Accounts verifies compliance with the correct level, class, and category.

**Art. 78 -- Personnel Action Transcription.** No appointment is valid until the corresponding decree is transcribed to the relevant agency and the Court of Accounts within ten days (30 days for Ministry of Education teaching staff). Publication in the Official Gazette is not required for general staff actions; only senior government officials require Gazette publication.

**Art. 81 -- Taking Office and Cessation.** Detailed rules govern when employees are deemed to have taken office (assuming duties and responsibilities) and when they cease. Designated senior officials must present clearances from the Court of Accounts confirming no outstanding judgments from prior management of public funds, plus tax and municipal solvency certificates, before taking office.

**Art. 82 -- Salary Accrual Rules.** Employees earn salary from the day they take office until they leave. Specific rules address handover periods: if handover takes one day, only the outgoing employee earns salary that day; if longer, the incoming employee earns half-salary during the overlap. Employees transferred from another public post continue earning their prior salary during the transition.

**Art. 83 -- Service Contracts.** Personal service contracts with the government require that: the work be professional or technical in nature, not constitute regular continuous institutional activity, no equivalent vacant Salary Law position exists, and prior written authorization from the Ministry of Finance is obtained at least 15 days in advance.

**Art. 84 -- Office Hours.** Standard public office hours are Monday through Friday, 8:00 to 16:00, with a 40-minute meal break. The Legislative Assembly sets its own schedule. Offices with special-law schedules are exempt but must maintain at least the same total hours.

**Art. 87 -- Contracts Requiring Notarization.** State contracts requiring public deed must be submitted for prior consultation to the Ministry of Finance and the Court of Accounts (except real estate purchases). Notarization is performed by the legal department heads of the relevant agency, without charging fees.

**Art. 93-94 -- Leave Without Pay.** Unpaid leave may not exceed two months per year, except medical leave which may extend to six months. Combined medical and non-medical unpaid leave in a single year cannot exceed six months total. Educators receiving educational credit loans may receive unpaid leave for the duration of their studies.

**Art. 95 -- Dual Employment Prohibition.** No person, civilian or military, may earn more than one salary from public funds, with enumerated exceptions for education directors serving evening classes, doctors in locations without other physicians, and certain diplomatic and judicial positions.

**Art. 96 -- Professional Practice Prohibition.** Ministers, Secretaries of State, the Fiscal General, Court of Accounts magistrates, Directors and Deputy Directors General, and full-time government lawyers are prohibited from private legal practice. Government engineers and architects are prohibited from private activities similar to their official duties.

**Art. 100 -- Tardiness and Absence Penalties.** The first three tardiness incidents in a month result in salary deduction for the period between scheduled start time and actual arrival. Beyond three incidents, the deduction doubles. Unexcused absences are penalized at double the corresponding salary; beyond two in a month, the penalty doubles again.

**Art. 108 -- Medical and Hospital Subsidies.** Public employees holding Salary Law positions and contract employees who require hospitalization may receive a subsidy. The subsidy covers care at official hospitals in economy class or charitable institutions, with a fixed amount scale based on salary level.

**Art. 109 -- Funeral Subsidies.** Agency heads may authorize funeral subsidies for active employees, retirees, and pensioned teachers. The subsidy equals one month's salary for active employees (with a minimum floor) and is paid to the designated beneficiary or next of kin.

**Art. 111 -- Life Insurance.** All public employees (regardless of age), whether appointed per the Salary Law, contract, or daily wage payroll, are entitled to a state-funded life insurance benefit of 30,000 colones, payable to their beneficiaries either directly by the state or through a contracted insurer.

**Art. 112 -- Salary for Assigned Functions Only.** The Court of Accounts will reject salary payments for employees performing duties other than those specified for their appointed position. Personnel may be temporarily assigned to another agency for up to six months (one year for Ministry of Education staff).

**Art. 113 -- Overtime.** Overtime is regulated by salary thresholds. Agencies with legally required uninterrupted 24/7 services (police, telecommunications, defense) must organize shifts, with prior notification to the Budget Directorate. Other agencies may authorize overtime pay at rates established by the law, subject to budget availability.

**Art. 116 -- Salary Categories.** Establishes four to five salary categories for each position. For salaries up to 600 colones: Category 1 at 100%, Category 2 at 90%, Category 3 at 80%, Category 4 at 70%. For salaries above 601 colones: five categories from 100% down to 80% in 5% steps.

**Art. 144 -- Prohibition on Dual Pensions.** No person may receive two state pensions simultaneously, or a pension combined with any other constant or frequent state or municipal remuneration. Exceptions exist for the professional pension under the Teaching Assistance Law.

**Art. 148-149 -- Transfer of State Property.** Detailed rules govern the sale, exchange, donation, and transfer of movable and immovable state property. Immovable property transfers between the state and autonomous institutions require formal agreements. Surplus movable property may be donated to other agencies, autonomous institutions, or municipalities.

**Art. 153 -- Foreign Real Estate.** The state may acquire real property abroad for diplomatic and consular offices, with pricing approved by the Ministries of Finance and Foreign Affairs and payment in installments as fiscally convenient.

**Art. 154-155 -- Vehicle Compensation.** Public employees using personal vehicles on official missions outside their duty station may be compensated at 0.25 colones per kilometer, authorized by the Ministry of Finance with a favorable report from the Budget Directorate.

**Art. 156-159 -- Subsidies to Autonomous Institutions and Private Entities.** The Executive may grant subsidies to autonomous institutions and private entities cooperating in cultural or welfare objectives, subject to budget availability. Recipient entities must submit financial reports to the Court of Accounts and return unused funds.

## Who This Affects

- **All central government employees and officials**, whose appointments, salaries, leave, overtime, disciplinary deductions, and benefits are governed by these provisions.
- **Autonomous official institutions** (ISSS, CEL, INPEP, ANTEL, University of El Salvador, National Lottery, etc.), each with dedicated chapters governing their specific budget operations, salary structures, and administrative rules.
- **The Ministry of Finance (Hacienda)** and **Court of Accounts (Corte de Cuentas)**, which exercise supervisory and approval authority over personnel actions, contracts, property transfers, and subsidy disbursements.
- **The Ministry of Education** and its teaching staff, subject to specialized appointment, transfer, leave, and salary rules.
- **Private entities receiving state subsidies**, which must account for the funds and return any unused balances.
- **Pensioners and retirees** under the pre-reform pension system, subject to the dual-pension prohibition and related benefit rules.

## Related Laws

- **Constitution of El Salvador** -- Establishes the framework for public budgeting and financial administration.
- **Ley Organica de Administracion Financiera del Estado (AFI Law)** -- The comprehensive financial administration law that repealed Chapters I and II (treasury, budget, and accounting provisions) of this decree.
- **Ley de Salarios** -- The annual Salary Law that establishes positions and salary levels referenced throughout.
- **Ley de Servicio Civil** -- Civil service law governing public employment terms and conditions.
- **Ley del Escalafon del Magisterio Nacional** -- Teaching career escalation law determining educator salary categories.
- **Ley de Asuetos, Vacaciones y Licencias de los Empleados Publicos** -- Public employee holiday, vacation, and leave law supplemented by these provisions.
- **Ley de Pensiones y Jubilaciones Civiles** -- Civil pension law subject to the dual-pension prohibition in Art. 144.
- **Decreto 652 (2023) -- Ley de Compras Publicas** -- The modern procurement law that governs acquisitions, while personnel contracting under these budget provisions remains excluded (Decreto 652, Art. 3(c)).
- **Ley del INPEP** -- National Pension Institute law, with institution-specific budget provisions in this decree.

## Common Questions

**1. Are the treasury and accounting chapters of this law still in force?**
No. Articles 2 through 70 Bis (Chapters I and II), which originally governed treasury operations, budgetary accounting, revenue concentration, and fund management for central government, were repealed by the Organic Financial Administration Law (AFI Law). The surviving provisions primarily address personnel, property, subsidies, and institution-specific fiscal rules.

**2. What rules govern government employee salaries?**
An employee earns salary from the day of taking office until cessation. Salary categories (Art. 116) allow the same position to be compensated at different percentage levels (70%-100%) based on the employee's assigned category. Employees performing duties outside their appointed position description are not entitled to salary for those functions (Art. 112).

**3. How is overtime regulated?**
Agencies with legally required continuous services must organize rotating shifts with Budget Directorate oversight. Other agencies may authorize overtime at established rates, but overtime pay is not recognized for employees traveling on official commission (who receive per diem instead). Senior officials above certain salary thresholds are generally not eligible for overtime.

**4. What are the penalties for tardiness and absence?**
The first three late arrivals in a month result in a salary deduction proportional to the time missed. After three instances, the deduction doubles. Each unexcused absence costs double the corresponding salary, and beyond two in a month the penalty doubles again. Leaving work without authorized leave is treated the same as an unexcused absence.

**5. Can a public employee hold two government positions?**
Generally no. Article 95 prohibits earning more than one salary from public funds, with narrow exceptions for educators serving evening classes, doctors in underserved locations, and certain diplomatic or judicial roles. Article 144 separately prohibits receiving two state pensions simultaneously.

**6. Does this law still govern government procurement?**
No. Government procurement of goods, services, and works is now governed by Decreto 652 of 2023 (Ley de Compras Publicas). However, this law continues to govern the contracting of personal services that create an employment relationship (as opposed to procurement contracts), and Decreto 652 explicitly excludes such personnel contracts from its scope.

**7. What happens to state property that is no longer needed?**
Surplus immovable property may be sold, exchanged, or transferred between the state and autonomous institutions via formal agreement. Surplus movable property may be donated to other government agencies, autonomous institutions, or municipalities upon authorization by the relevant unit head or the Ministry of Finance. Leftover construction materials of low value may be donated to charitable organizations.

**8. What institution-specific provisions remain in force?**
The law contains dedicated chapters with fiscal and administrative rules for ANTEL (telecommunications), the National Lottery, the University of El Salvador, INPEP (pensions), the Proveeduria General (general supply), and other autonomous entities. These include institution-specific salary structures, bonus provisions (such as ANTEL's Christmas bonus and June compensation), procurement thresholds, and operational rules.
