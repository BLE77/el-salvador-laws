# Decreto 153 (2003) -- Drug Regulation Law

## Summary
This law regulates all activities related to drugs in El Salvador, replacing the earlier 1991 drug law. It defines what substances count as drugs, creates the National Anti-Drug Commission (*Comision Nacional Antidrogas*, CNA) to coordinate government anti-drug policy, establishes penalties for drug trafficking and possession, and sets rules for importing, manufacturing, and distributing controlled substances for legitimate medical and scientific purposes.

## Key Provisions
- **Article 2**: Drugs are defined as substances specified in international conventions ratified by El Salvador, those listed in the Health Code, and generally any substance that acts on the central nervous system and can cause physical or psychological dependence. Seeds, flowers, plants, and raw materials used to make drugs are also included. Alcohol, tobacco, and solvents are regulated by separate laws.
- **Article 3**: All activities related to the following categories of substances are prohibited without authorization: narcotics, depressants, stimulants, hallucinogens, and cannabis.
- **Article 4**: Authorized activities related to controlled substances are limited to medical, scientific, industrial, or other purposes expressly permitted by law.
- **Article 5**: Defines key legal terms including *Agente Encubierto* (undercover agent), *Compra Controlada* (controlled purchase), and *Entrega Vigilada* (monitored delivery).
- **Article 6**: Creates the CNA under the direction of the President to plan, coordinate, and evaluate anti-drug strategies. The CNA includes representatives from the Ministries of Interior, Health, Education, Defense, and the Superior Council of Public Health.
- **Article 14**: The Superior Council of Public Health (*Consejo Superior de Salud Publica*) must authorize the import of all controlled substances and maintain a registry of authorized importers.
- **Article 22**: Cultivation or production of drugs requires authorization from the Superior Council of Public Health, limited to registered pharmaceutical or research laboratories.
- **Article 33**: Trafficking (*trafico ilicito*) -- selling, buying, transporting, storing, or distributing drugs without authorization -- is punishable by 10-15 years in prison and a fine of 50-2,500 minimum monthly wages.
- **Article 34**: Possession (*posesion o tenencia*) of less than 2 grams of illicit drugs is punishable by 1-3 years in prison. Possession of 2 grams or more carries 3-6 years. Possession with intent to traffic carries 6-10 years.
- **Article 35**: Promoting or encouraging drug cultivation or trafficking is punishable by 8-12 years in prison.
- **Article 41**: Administering drugs to another person without therapeutic purpose or medical prescription carries 3-6 years. If administered to a minor or without consent, 6-8 years.
- **Article 42**: Altering or forging medical prescriptions to obtain drugs carries 3-6 years.
- **Article 50**: Intermediation between drug producers and consumers carries 5-10 years.
- **Article 52**: Preparatory acts, conspiracy, and criminal association related to drug offenses are independently punishable.
- **Article 54**: Aggravating circumstances include: involving minors, acting in schools or military facilities, being a law enforcement officer, using a public office, or being part of organized crime.
- **Article 58**: The police, through undercover agents, may conduct controlled purchases authorized in writing by the Attorney General (*Fiscal General*).
- **Article 60**: The Attorney General authorizes and supervises "monitored delivery" (*entrega vigilada*) procedures as provided in international conventions.
- **Article 70**: In a final conviction, all assets seized in connection with drug crimes pass to a special fund (*Patrimonio Especial de Delitos Relativos al Narcotrafico*) for anti-drug enforcement agencies.

## Who This Affects
Everyone in El Salvador. The law applies to any person involved in drug-related activities, whether production, trafficking, possession, or consumption. It also regulates pharmacies, laboratories, importers, and healthcare professionals who handle controlled substances. Law enforcement officers have specific roles and protections under this law.

## Common Questions
- **Q: What is the penalty for drug possession in El Salvador?** -- A: Possession of less than 2 grams carries 1-3 years in prison. Two grams or more carries 3-6 years. If the intent is to traffic, the penalty is 6-10 years (Art. 34).
- **Q: What is the penalty for drug trafficking?** -- A: 10-15 years in prison plus a fine of 50-2,500 minimum monthly wages (Art. 33).
- **Q: Are alcohol and tobacco covered by this law?** -- A: No. While they fall within the general subject of drugs, they are regulated by separate, specific laws (Art. 2).
- **Q: Who oversees drug policy in El Salvador?** -- A: The National Anti-Drug Commission (CNA), which operates under the direction of the President and includes representatives from multiple government ministries (Art. 6).
- **Q: Can police conduct undercover drug buys?** -- A: Yes. Controlled purchases by undercover agents must be authorized in writing by the Attorney General (Art. 58).
- **Q: What happens to assets seized in drug cases?** -- A: Upon conviction, they pass to a special government fund dedicated to anti-narcotics operations (Art. 70).
